In 1883, Alice Claypoole Vanderbilt attended her sister-in-law\u2019s famed masquerade party in a dazzling gold-and-silver gown, custom from Paris, dubbed Electric Light, complete with hidden batteries that illuminated a torch she held above her head.<\/p>\n

Today\u2019s A-listers hope to generate their own electricity at Monday night\u2019s Met Gala, the dress code for which is \u201cgilded glamour.\u201d Yet instead of cutting-edge frocks like Mrs. Vanderbilt\u2019s, some insiders fear that attendees will embrace the tackiest aspects of late-19th-century Manhattan society. Will dresses resemble the costumes on the popular HBO series \u201cThe Gilded Age,\u201d in which Carrie Coon\u2019s social-climbing Bertha swans around her Fifth Avenue mansion in couture?<\/p>\n

While flamboyance has helped make Anna Wintour\u2019s annual Met Gala the most anticipated red carpet of the year \u2014 \u201cIt\u2019s a bigger deal than the Oscars,\u201d said Christina Pacelli, who has dressed celebs such as Laverne Cox for the big evening \u2014 some observers say the get-ups have gotten too garish.\u00a0<\/p>\n

While flamboyance has helped make Anna Wintour\u2019s annual Met Gala the most anticipated red carpet of the year, some observers say the get-ups have gotten too garish.NY Post photo composite In 1996, Princess Diana attended the gala in a sleek Dior slip dress. Bertrand Rindoff Petroff\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s turned into a costume party,\u201d designer and frequent gala chair Tom Ford told journalist Amy Odell in her book \u201cAnna: The Biography,\u201d out Tuesday.<\/p>\n

\u201c[It] used to just be very chic people wearing very beautiful clothes going to an exhibition about the 18th century,\u201d Ford continued. \u201cYou didn\u2019t have to look like the 18th century, you didn\u2019t have to dress like a hamburger, you didn\u2019t have to arrive in a van where you were standing up because you couldn\u2019t sit down because you wore a chandelier.\u201d<\/p>\n

Ford may have a slightly idealized view of the galas of yore\u00a0\u2014 at least one person showed up to 1981\u2019s ball, themed for the exhibit \u201cThe Eighteenth-Century Woman,\u201d dressed in knee breeches! But the clothes and themes have gotten kookier in the days since Princess Diana attended in a sleek Dior slip dress.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Designer Gianni Versace and model Naomi Campbell at the benefit in 1995.Gene Shaw\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

Recent years have seen Rihanna don a pope hat for 2018\u2019s \u201cHeavenly Bodies,\u201d Jared Leto carrying a replica of his own head for 2019\u2019s \u201cCamp\u201d and Lil Nas X model a sexy C-3PO costume for last year\u2019s \u201cAmerican Independence,\u201d which he shed to reveal a sparkly Versace catsuit underneath.\u00a0<\/p>\n

And lest you think Ford was exaggerating, Katy Perry did<\/em> wear a chandelier and a hamburger costume \u2014 on the same night.<\/p>\n

Katy Perry wore a hamburger costume in 2019.Kevin Mazur\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u2018It\u2019s very Halloween\u2019<\/h2>\n

\u201cSome of the things that Kim Kardashian has worn \u2014 I mean, it\u2019s very Halloween,\u201d said John Tiffany, a fashion historian and brand consultant who once assisted Eleanor Lambert, the legendary fashion publicist who dreamed up the Met\u2019s first Costume Institute benefit, then called the Party of the Year, in 1948. Back then, Tiffany said, the party was a fund-raiser dinner, but in the 1970s, when freshly fired Vogue editor Diana Vreeland started manning the Costume Institute, the gala became linked with whatever fashion exhibit was opening at the museum, \u201cwhich were always completely over the top.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s always been a creative party,\u201d said Dennita Sewell, a fashion professor at Arizona State University who worked at the Costume Institute in the 1990s \u2014 when lower-rung staffers could actually attend the party. \u201cPeople always dressed up, but it wasn\u2019t so extreme \u2026 No one would have done something that wasn\u2019t graceful and elegant.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Kim Kardashian in Balenciaga in 2021.Justin Lane\/EPA <\/p>\n

\u201cThe themes were noted,\u201d she added, \u201cbut it wasn\u2019t like the whole party was competing with the exhibition.\u201d<\/p>\n

Sometimes it can feel that way.<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s gone from an industry event celebrating the history of fashion to one celebrating celebrity,\u201d stylist Tracy Taylor told The Post. \u201cDesigners were really the focus of the galas in the 20th century and early 21st century: Alexander McQueen, Halston \u2014 Halston would have never designed something that you couldn\u2019t sit in! But lately, the focus is on themes, and I do feel like it\u2019s encouraging more extreme interpretations and outfits.\u201d<\/p>\n

Last year, Lil Nas X sported a sexy C3PO costume, for \u201cAmerican Independence,\u201d which he shed to reveal a sparkly Versace catsuit underneath. Mike Coppola\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

The invite list has changed to include more celebrities \u2014 particularly, in recent years, musicians, who are used to wearing costumes onstage and often treat fashion as performance.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhen you\u2019re a musician like Rihanna, it\u2019s not such a huge stretch to look outlandish,\u201d Taylor said. \u201cThey\u2019re expected to be a bit more flamboyant or really be creative and show who they are through how they dress.\u201d And that translates to the red carpet.<\/p>\n

Jared Leto holding a replica of his own head in 2019. The theme that year was \u201cCamp.\u201d Karwai Tang\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

Gilded controversy<\/h2>\n

\u201cGilded glamour\u201d is a dress code that allows for lots of different interpretations \u2014 from a corset gown with a huge bustle and swaths of sumptuous taffeta to a slinky gold lam\u00e9 slip to a sequin frock \u2014 and plenty of ways to up the ante.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The Gilded Age was one of \u201cenormous growth and wealth due to industrialization and real estate, and the dresses reflected that opulence,\u201d Taylor said. \u201cIt was about these new celebrities and peacocking, and that\u2019s what the Met Gala is about.\u201d<\/p>\n

Yet it could read as tone deaf. The era, which spanned from 1870 to 1900, was also characterized by extreme poverty \u2014 with exploited immigrant families living in crowded, unsanitary tenements on the Lower East Side while Fifth Avenue\u2019s titans dined on oysters and lobster in their Parisian couture (modeled, perversely, on 17th-century French court fashion).\u00a0<\/p>\n

Rihanna donned a pope hat for 2018\u2019s \u201cHeavenly Bodies.\u201dCarlo Allegri\/REUTERS <\/p>\n

\u201cThe world is in a state of flux,\u201d said Bronwyn Cosgrave, host of the podcast \u201cFashion Conversations,\u201d citing the war in Ukraine and the uptick in violence in the US. \u201cIn New York City, where the Met Gala takes place, there\u2019s huge problems with homelessness, problems with mental health \u2026 I\u2019m not sure gilded glamour is what we need.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Others argue that it\u2019s exactly what\u2019s needed right now. <\/p>\n

\u201cWhen times are tough, people turn to fantasy,\u201d Phyllis Magidson, a fashion curator who worked with the Museum of the City of New York, told The Post.<\/p>\n

Carrie Coon plays social-climbing Bertha in the popular HBO series \u201cThe Gilded Age.\u201d Photograph by Alison Rosa\/HBO <\/p>\n

\u201cEverybody\u2019s battered, and what better way to escape than through period fashion?\u201d<\/p>\n

Some attendees, too, are embracing the gala\u2019s gloriously gaudy theme.<\/p>\n

\u201cI think that dressing on theme is part of the fun of it, personally,\u201d Katy Perry\u2019s stylist, Tatiana Waterford, told The Post. \u201cKaty always dresses on theme. But she\u2019s always had a distinctive sense of style that lends itself to an over-the-top Met Gala look.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

That said, even Perry plans to tone things down this year.\u00a0\u201cShe won\u2019t look kooky, but it\u2019s Katy, so there will be no shortage of drama,\u201d Waterford said. \u201cI wish I could reveal more but you\u2019ll just have to wait and see!\u201d<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
