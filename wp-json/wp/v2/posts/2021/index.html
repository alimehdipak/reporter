\u201cAnnie\u201d alum Aileen Quinn achieved worldwide fame in 1982 after starring as the little, red-haired orphan girl in the John Huston-directed family flick.<\/p>\n

However, being a child star wasn\u2019t for her and she decided she wanted a better \u201cTomorrow.\u201d Quinn, now 50, decided to take a break from Tinseltown when she hit 18.<\/p>\n

Quinn beat out more than 8,000 girls for the role of Annie and scored the gig when she was just 9 years old. Albert Finney, Tim Curry, Carol Burnett and Bernadette Peters also starred in the film.<\/p>\n

She divulged why she skipped out on Hollywood in an interview with Fox News, noting she wanted to explore the world.<\/p>\n

She revealed that her parents felt that education was very important and they were \u201cstrict\u201d about her getting three hours of school a day.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhen I came back from filming, my teachers didn\u2019t cut me any breaks,\u201d Quinn said. \u201cDon\u2019t get me wrong, they loved it, they were happy for me, and they wanted my autograph, too, but they were like, \u2018Welcome back to test-taking. Be your usual A+ student.\u2019 \u201c<\/p>\n

Director John Huston, Aileen Quinn and other members of the cast of \u201cAnnie\u201d were on set in 1981.Getty Images <\/p>\n

The singer stated that she had been under contract in the early 1980s and was able to do theater and even recorded an album, \u201cBobby\u2019s Girl,\u201d for Columbia Records.<\/p>\n

\u201cBut after that, I wanted to get back to some normalcy. I wanted to go to college and explore my other options,\u201d Quinn explained. \u201cI wanted to learn about the world. I lived in a foreign country and got to experience that for a while. I got to learn a different language and explore another part of the world that was different. So in a way, it was a blessing in disguise. I was able to grow up, have a normal life, date and see the world, which felt right to me.\u201d<\/p>\n

After she graduated from college, she started to do more stage work. She also said she found it \u201chard\u201d to separate herself from her character years after the movie wrapped.<\/p>\n

Quinn was approached about several \u201cAnnie\u201d sequels; however, those films never made it to production.WireImage <\/p>\n

\u201cI\u2019ve always looked younger, so it was a little hard to transition. But the great thing about theater is I got to play everything,\u201d she said. \u201cLuckily, theater kept me growing, and I was never pigeonholed.\u201d<\/p>\n

Quinn later kept herself busy with the theater and \u201cdid that for 25 years straight.\u201d The stage lights were a place where she felt \u201cembraced.\u201d <\/p>\n

The Pennsylvania native was asked to make several sequels \u2014 she met with directors and read scenes about potential projects, which would be more \u201cadventurous\u201d and based on the \u201cAnnie\u201d comic strips \u2014 but the plans never materialized. <\/p>\n

Albert Finney and Aileen Quinn in 1982\u2019s \u201cAnnie.\u201dGetty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cI don\u2019t know what happened because that was on the corporate side of things, and I was so young,\u201d she said. <\/p>\n

\u201cBut other things were happening [for me],\u201d Quinn added. \u201cI was asked to be on certain sitcoms and do certain other projects and I had to turn them down for seven years. By then, I had grown up and I was a teenager.\u201d<\/p>\n

Nowadays, Quinn is pretty \u201cbusy\u201d and she\u2019s trying to get back into film and TV after moving to Los Angeles. She also has her own band, Aileen Quinn and the Leapin\u2019 Lizards, and hopes to start touring soon. They\u2019re working on their third album now, and she\u2019s continuing to hone her songwriting skills. <\/p>\n
