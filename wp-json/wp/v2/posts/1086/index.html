Google employees upset over the company\u2019s decision to remove heated bidets from its offices may get some relief, after all.<\/p>\n

Tushy, a New York-based bidet maker, has offered to step in after Bay-area Google employees, who are scheduled to return to the office beginning on April 4 \u2014 reportedly raised a stink over the company\u2019s plans to remove heated bidet toilets from the office premises.<\/p>\n

The tech giant started removing the Toto-branded toilet seats because they were in violation of California\u2019s code for commercial buildings,\u00a0according to Protocol, which reportedly saw a maintenance ticket ordering the removals.<\/p>\n

Company staffers took to the internal meme generator to complain about the new policy, with one worker noting: \u201cThe removal of bidets in the office is my #2 issue with RTO,\u201d referring to returning to the office.<\/p>\n

Edgar Tovar, who handles maintenance at company facilities, told staffers that the bidet removal \u201chas the added benefit of supporting Google\u2019s water sustainability commitments.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cMany of our buildings are migrating to recycled water systems which cannot support bidets,\u201d he wrote.<\/p>\n

Googlers took to the internal meme generator to voice their displeasure over the company\u2019s decision to remove heated bidets.Twitter <\/p>\n

In response, Tushy founder Miki Agrawal wrote a letter to Google management offering to ship portable bidets to its Bay Area office.<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s impossible to be a hands-on Googler if one hand is tied up itching a dirty butt,\u201d company founder Agrawal wrote.<\/p>\n

The Post has reached out to Google to inquire as to whether the Alphabet, Inc.-owned outfit will take up Tushy on its offer.<\/p>\n

TUSHY is offering to send portable bidets to Google offices after the company ordered the heated bidets removed.Twitter <\/p>\n

Bidet toilets are specially outfitted so that those who need to go to the bathroom can have a stream of water strategically aimed at the relevant areas.<\/p>\n

In the old days, especially in Europe, bidets were placed alongside traditional toilets as an option to maintain hygiene.<\/p>\n

The word \u201cbidet\u201d is French for \u201cpony\u201d or \u201csmall horse\u201d \u2014 which is appropriate since the use of a bidet requires one to straddle it as if riding a four-legged animal.<\/p>\n

Proponents of bidets say that it is an environmentally-friendly alternative to toilet paper.Getty Images\/iStockphoto <\/p>\n

Bidets remain widely used in western Europe, South America, the Middle East, and Asia, but they have not caught on as much in the United States, where toilet paper is typically the sole method of cleaning one\u2019s backside.<\/p>\n

Proponents of bidets argue that it would be beneficial for the environment since it would save trees that are needed to be cut down in order to produce toilet paper.<\/p>\n

In the early days of the coronavirus pandemic,\u00a0sales of bidets skyrocketed\u00a0as shoppers started panic-buying toilet paper.<\/p>\n
