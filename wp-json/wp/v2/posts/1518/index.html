<\/p>\n

A mass of solar particles is heading our way after a \u2018dead sunspot\u2019 exploded on the solar surface.<\/p>\n

The explosion on April 11 led to a coronal mass ejection (CME), which is a type of flare that can cause solar storms on Earth.<\/p>\n

A dead sunspot explosion may sound intense but it\u2019s unlikely to have a huge impact on Earth.<\/p>\n

Sunspots are dark regions that pop up on the Sun due to magnetism inside the burning mass.<\/p>\n

They can last between a few hours to a few months and are considered \u2018dead\u2019 when they start breaking apart.<\/p>\n

A sunspot called AR2987 exploded recently and was considered \u2018dead\u2019 until it randomly \u2018restarted\u2019.<\/p>\n

Physicist Philip Judge explained to LiveScience: \u201cOccasionally, sunspots can \u2018restart,\u2019 with more magnetism appearing later (days, weeks) at the same region, as if a weakness was made in the convection zone, or as if there is an unstable region under the surface that is particularly good at generating magnetic fields beneath.\u201d<\/p>\n

The sunspot explosion on April 11 could impact Earth in a few days time.<\/p>\n

According to the experts at SpaceWeather.com: \u201cA CME is heading for Earth.<\/p>\n

\u201cNOAA forecasters say that G2-class geomagnetic storms are possible when it arrives on April 14th.<\/p>\n

\u201cDuring such storms, auroras can be seen as far south as, e.g., New York and Idaho (geomagnetic latitude 55 degrees).\u201d<\/p>\n

A coronal mass ejection previously erupted at the Sun on January 23, 2012.NOAA\/National Weather Service’s Space Weather Prediction Center via Getty Images <\/p>\n

A CME is a huge expulsion of plasma from the sun\u2019s outer layer, called the corona.<\/p>\n

These expulsions shoot through space and can hit Earth.<\/p>\n

Fortunately, the solar storm predicted for April 14, is only likely to be a \u201cG2-class\u201d storm.<\/p>\n

This type of storm is considered to be moderate and can damage high-latitude power systems.<\/p>\n

A G2-class solar storm can affect the power grid and also any spacecraft in orbit.<\/p>\n

Ground control may have to keep an eye out to keep spacecraft out of harm\u2019s way.<\/p>\n

Solar storms can also confuse migrating animals that rely on the Earth\u2019s magnetic field for a sense of direction.<\/p>\n

One good thing about solar storms is that they can produce very pretty natural light displays like the northern lights.<\/p>\n

Those natural light displays are called auroras and are examples of the Earth\u2019s magnetosphere getting bombarded by solar wind, which creates pretty green and blue displays.<\/p>\n

The Earth\u2019s magnetic field helps to protect us from the more extreme consequences of solar flares.<\/p>\n

Solar storms often create wind that leads to the majestic colors of the northern lights in Iceland.Owen Humphreys\/PA Wire via ZUMA Press <\/p>\n

In 1989, a strong solar eruption shot so many electrically charged particles at Earth that the Canadian province of Quebec lost power for nine hours.<\/p>\n

As well as causing issues for our tech on Earth, they can be deadly for an astronaut if they result in injury or interfere with mission control communications.<\/p>\n

The sun is currently at the start of a new 11-year solar cycle, which usually sees eruptions and flares grow more intense and extreme.<\/p>\n
