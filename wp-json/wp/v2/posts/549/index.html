<\/p>\n

The thought of reviving the foregone dodo is no longer one for the birds.<\/p>\n

The recent discovery of a \u201cfantastic specimen\u201d of dodo DNA was the last clue needed to complete the extinct bird\u2019s genome, announced a team of biological researchers at the University of California, Santa Cruz.<\/p>\n

The findings mean scientists are one step closer to the possibility of bringing back the dodo, which disappeared more than three centuries ago.<\/p>\n

Beth Shapiro, a professor of\u00a0ecology and evolutionary biology at UC Santa Cruz, told viewers of a Royal Society webinar that her group would publish the full genetic sequence at the Natural History Museum of Denmark.<\/p>\n

Shapiro confirmed the breakthrough when pressed by her audience, according to The Telegraph. \u201cYes, the dodo genome is entirely sequenced because we sequenced it. It\u2019s not been published yet, but it does exist and we\u2019re working on it right now,\u201d she said.<\/p>\n

This early 20th-century illustration of a dodo was made by Lionel Walter Rothschild about 200 years after the bird went extinct.Getty Images \t \t <\/p>\n

The 3-foot-tall flightless bird was first recorded in modern history by Dutch sailors on the island of Mauritius, east of Madagascar in the Indian Ocean, in 1598. By the late 17th century, not a trace of the dodo could be found there \u2014 and was presumed to have been wiped out by invasive species, including dogs, cats and humans <\/p>\n

There\u2019s been a buzz in the evolutionary research community about the potential to recreate other prehistoric species, such as the wooly mammoth which died out around 4,000 years ago. Scientists have teased the plan since its complete genome was sequenced in 2015.<\/p>\n

That same year, Shapiro\u2019s book \u201cHow To Clone A Mammoth\u201d (Princeton University Press) revealed her dodo de-extinction aspirations: \u201cMore than any other species [the dodo] is the international symbol of human-caused extinction,\u201d she wrote. <\/p>\n

But bringing back the dodo poses unique challenges, Shapiro told Royal Society attendees.<\/p>\n

This \u201cSummers Place Dodo\u201d skeleton dates back to the 16th century, just before the species went extinct.Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cMammals are simpler,\u201d she said, made possible by cloning \u2014 \u201cthe same approach that was used to create Dolly the Sheep.\u201d <\/p>\n

\u201cBut we don\u2019t know how to do that with birds because of the intricacies of their reproductive pathways. So there needs to be another approach for birds,\u201d she explained, adding that she has \u201clittle doubt that we are going to get there.\u201d<\/p>\n

It\u2019s been suggested that the dodo\u2019s close cousin, the Nicobar pigeon, could have its DNA edited to include dodo DNA, the Telegraph reported, though it wouldn\u2019t be a fully borne dodo in that case.<\/p>\n

A reconstruction of the dodo to scale at the Senckenberg Museum in Frankfurt, Germany.picture alliance via Getty Image <\/p>\n

And of all the animals to invite back into existence, there\u2019s a strong case for the dodo as an appropriate tropical habitat could be easily located for it today. Plus, it\u2019s harmless.<\/p>\n
