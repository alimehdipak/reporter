<\/p>\n

Saudi Prince Alwaleed bin Talal Al Saud, a billionaire who is also one of Twitter\u2019s largest shareholders, tweeted that he will vote to reject Elon Musk\u2019s $41 billion all-cash bid for the company \u2014 leading Musk to question Saudi Arabia\u2019s commitment to free speech.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cI don\u2019t believe that the proposed offer by @elonmusk ($54.20) comes close to the intrinsic value of @Twitter given its growth prospects,\u201d the prince tweeted on Thursday.<\/p>\n

\u201cBeing one of the largest & long-term shareholders of Twitter, @Kingdom_KHC & I reject this offer.\u201d<\/p>\n

Hours later, Musk responded with what seemed to be a jab at Saudi Arabia\u2019s free speech and human rights record.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cInteresting. Just two questions, if I may,\u201d Musk replied to the prince. \u201cHow much of Twitter does the Kingdom own, directly & indirectly? What are the Kingdom\u2019s views on journalistic freedom of speech?\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Saudi Arabia frequently surveils and arrests journalists, according to Reporters Without Borders, which ranks the country as one of the worst in the world for press freedom. And the\u00a0brutal murder of Washington Post journalist Jamal Khashoggi\u00a0was\u00a0personally approved by\u00a0Saudi Arabia\u2019s Crown Prince Muhammad bin Salman, according to US intelligence.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Prince Alwaleed bin Talal Al Saud chairs the Kingdom Holding Company, the Riyadh-based conglomerate that first purchased shares of Twitter in 2011 before the company\u2019s 2013 initial public offering.<\/p>\n

KHC also holds large stakes in a wide range of businesses including the Four Seasons hotel chain, Uber, Lyft, and Citigroup. <\/p>\n

Saudi Prince Alwaleed bin Talal Al Saud, one of Twitter\u2019s largest shareholders, says the company should reject Elon Musk\u2019s $41 billion all-cash bid.AFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Twitter shares were trading upward at the start of the day on Wall Street but have since shed initial gains. Shares closed down 1.7% at $45.08.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Members of Twitter\u2019s board convened a meeting on Thursday morning to discuss Musk\u2019s offer.<\/p>\n

Despite Musk\u2019s apparent jabs at Saudi Arabia on Thursday, the Tesla CEO is not beyond taking money from the Kingdom.\u00a0<\/p>\n

In August 2018 \u2014 just months before Khashoggi\u2019s murder \u2014 Musk infamously claimed to\u00a0have \u201cfunding secured\u201d to take Tesla private\u00a0from a Saudi sovereign wealth fund at $420 per share in a deal that did not come to fruition. The tweet resulted in a\u00a0lengthy legal battle\u00a0with the Securities and Exchange Commission.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Musk\u2019s Thursday Twitter offer price of $54.20 per share, which comes just days after he rejected a seat on the social media company\u2019s board, represents a 38% premium to Twitter\u2019s April 1 close, the last trading day before the Tesla CEO\u2019s more than 9% stake in the company was made public.<\/p>\n

Musk, the CEO of Tesla and SpaceX, is currently the second largest shareholder of Twitter.REUTERS <\/p>\n

\u201cI invested in Twitter as I believe in its potential to be the platform for free speech around the globe, and I believe free speech is a societal imperative for a functioning democracy,\u201d Musk wrote in a letter to Twitter Chairman Bret Taylor.<\/p>\n

\u201cSince making my investment I now realize the company will neither thrive nor serve this societal imperative in its current form. Twitter needs to be transformed as a private company.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cMy offer is my best and final offer and if it is not accepted, I would need to reconsider my position as a shareholder,\u201d Musk said.<\/p>\n

Musk\u2019s takeout offer for Twitter caps a two-week stretch of taunts he has launched on the embattled social-media giant. Shortly after disclosing his stake, Musk immediately began teasing possible moves including converting Twitter\u2019s San Francisco headquarters into a homeless shelter.<\/p>\n

Musk also has suggested adding an edit button for tweets, suggested that Twitter might be \u201cdying,\u201d and even scrapping its business model, which relies on selling ads.<\/p>\n

The billionaire Saudi royal has significant holdings in Uber, Lyft, and Citigroup.AP <\/p>\n

In a Thursday securities filing announcing his takeover offer, Musk used blunt language, telling the Twitter board: \u201cI am not playing the back-and-forth game.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cI have moved straight to the end,\u201d the entrepreneur said. \u201cIt\u2019s a high price and your shareholders will love it.\u201d<\/p>\n

Earlier this week, Musk said he had abandoned a plan to join Twitter\u2019s board, just as his tenure was about to start. Taking the board seat would have prevented him from a possible takeover of the company.<\/p>\n

Twitter said in a statement: \u201cThe Twitter Board of Directors will carefully review the proposal to determine the course of action that it believes is in the best interest of the Company and all Twitter stockholders.\u201d<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
