  • Necessary equipment to run your company may be considered both a liability and an asset for your growing business.<\/li>\n
  • Accounting personnel should list your company’s equipment on a balance sheet as a noncurrent asset, which obtains value after a fiscal year has passed.<\/li>\n
  • The three main categories of noncurrent assets are tangible assets, intangible assets and natural resources.\u00a0<\/li>\n
  • This article is for business owners who are purchasing or selling equipment and want to classify it accurately for accounting purposes.<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

    While your company focuses on selling your products or services to make money, you may take for granted the hardware that streamlines this process. But equipment is more than just a fixture inside your company walls. Whether you are establishing a startup or expanding your company, equipment is a long-term asset that can provide value now and in the future.<\/p>\n

    How is equipment classified in accounting? For example, is equipment an asset or a liability? We’ll help you discern the difference and answer general questions along the way.<\/p>\n

    Is equipment considered an asset or liability?<\/h2>\n

    Equipment can be considered both a liability and an asset. For example, if you have a loan on your equipment, it is a liability.<\/p>\n

    As an asset, the equipment can help you increase sales. However, equipment is not a current asset, but a noncurrent asset. [Related: Complete Equipment Leasing Guide for Businesses]<\/p>\n

    What type of asset is equipment?<\/h2>\n

    Equipment is considered a noncurrent asset \u2013 or fixed asset. A noncurrent asset is a long-term investment that your company makes that is not likely to become cash within an accounting year or does not easily convert to cash.<\/p>\n

    Fixed assets generally apply to property, plant and equipment (PP&E). While noncurrent assets can lower cash flow, they can signal to investors that you are serious about growing your company and increasing your customers’ trust in your brand as you scale your line.<\/p>\n

    What sort of equipment falls under assets?<\/h2>\n

    Equipment essential to your industry or business can be considered an asset. These are examples of typical equipment assets:<\/p>\n

      \n
    • Copy machines<\/li>\n
    • Postage meters<\/li>\n
    • Computers<\/li>\n
    • Telephones<\/li>\n
    • Fax machines<\/li>\n
    • Production line machinery<\/li>\n
    • Farm combines and tractors<\/li>\n
    • Lumber-cutting machinery<\/li>\n
    • Wrecking balls<\/li>\n
    • Pneumatic drills<\/li>\n
    • Cranes<\/li>\n
    • Robots<\/li>\n
    • Medical scanning equipment<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

      How to maximize your equipment’s value<\/h2>\n

      Since your equipment is a long-term asset that provides sustainability, it’s essential to manage it properly. Only use the equipment for tasks it was made to do. The more you think of equipment as an asset and less as a tool, the easier it will be to put in the time and money for the maintenance and upgrades it requires.\u00a0<\/p>\n

      Regular audits and inspections of your equipment can maximize its efficiency and life expectancy. By accurately managing your long-term assets, you can prevent extended shutdowns that impact your profits. Plus, you can protect the value if you decide to upgrade or sell later.<\/p>\n

      Current vs. noncurrent assets<\/h2>\n

      Your business can have both current and noncurrent assets. How quickly you plan to use the resource will determine if it is recorded onto the balance sheet as a current asset or a noncurrent asset.\u00a0<\/p>\n

      Current assets<\/h3>\n

      Current assets are set to be liquidated within the year. As a result, your company will utilize existing assets to pay bills and fund day-to-day expenses. Here are some current assets:<\/p>\n

        \n
      • Inventory (finished goods)<\/li>\n
      • Accounts receivable (electricity, cell phone)<\/li>\n
      • Cash (checking accounts)<\/li>\n
      • Foreign currency<\/li>\n
      • Prepaid expenses<\/li>\n
      • Marketable securities (certificates of deposit, high-yield savings accounts, money market accounts)<\/li>\n
      • Liquid assets<\/li>\n
      • Supplies (raw materials)<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

        Here is the formula for calculating current assets:<\/p>\n

        \n

        Accounts receivable + Cash + Cash equivalents + Inventory + Liquid assets + Marketable securities + Prepaid expenses = Current assets<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n

        Tip: Don’t confuse noncurrent assets with noncurrent liabilities. While noncurrent assets are owned, noncurrent liabilities are long-term debt obligations \u2013 such as long-term leases and bonds payable.<\/p>\n

        Noncurrent assets<\/h3>\n

        A noncurrent asset will not have value until at least a fiscal year has passed. As a result, companies invest in noncurrent assets over several years to avoid huge losses during seasons of growth. Here are some standard noncurrent assets:<\/p>\n

          \n
        • Long-term investments<\/li>\n
        • Vehicles<\/li>\n
        • PP&E<\/li>\n
        • Patents and trademarks<\/li>\n
        • Goodwill (intangible asset)<\/li>\n<\/ul>\n

          Current and noncurrent assets have their own columns on an accounting spreadsheet. First, however, they are totaled together and reconciled against liabilities and equities.\u00a0<\/p>\n

          How is equipment arranged on a balance sheet?<\/h2>\n

          Equipment will be listed on your balance sheet as noncurrent assets. Therefore, it is unnecessary to have a separate balance sheet just for your equipment.<\/p>\n

          Your company may gain assets by borrowing money from financial institutions and investors, following this formula:<\/p>\n

          \n

          Assets = Liabilities + Shareholders’ equity<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n

          The balance sheet is imperative to understanding your company’s current financial condition and engaging investors to accelerate the business’s growth.           Key takeaway: Your company’s balance sheet has three parts \u2013 assets (what your business owns), liabilities (what your company owes) and ownership equity (investment amounts by shareholders).<\/p>\n

          What are some other noncurrent assets?<\/h2>\n

          There are three main categories of noncurrent assets: tangible, intangible and natural resources.\u00a0<\/p>\n

          Tangible assets<\/h3>\n

          Tangible assets are company-owned property or physical goods that are integral to the business operation. This asset is valued at its original cost minus any depreciation. However, tangible assets \u2013 such as land \u2013 may be void of depreciation because they tend to appreciate.<\/p>\n

          Intangible assets<\/h3>\n

          Intangible assets are not physical in form but offer significant company value. These assets are classified as definite (like trademarks) or indefinite (such as brand recognition).\u00a0<\/p>\n

          Did you know? Intangible assets are necessary for your business to compete in the modern economy. While physical capital is still necessary, today’s companies thrive on sharing information and ideas and deepening relationships.<\/p>\n

          Natural resources<\/h2>\n

          Natural resources are also known as “wasting assets” because of their loss during consumption. These resources from the earth include fossil fuels, minerals, oil and timber.<\/p>\n

          Here is the natural resources balance sheet formula:<\/p>\n

          \n

          Cost of acquisition + Exploration + Development costs – Accumulated depletion = Natural resources assets<\/p>\n<\/blockquote>\n
