<\/p>\n

Amazon workers at a warehouse in Staten Island voted\u00a0Friday to become the web giant\u2019s first unionized US workers \u2014 drawing praise from the White House.<\/p>\n

The vote was 2,654 in favor and 2,131 against unionization, according to National Labor Relations Board officials.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe president was glad to see workers ensure their voices are heard with respect to important workplace decisions,\u201d White House press secretary Jen Psaki said at a Friday briefing.<\/p>\n

\u201cHe believes firmly that every worker in every state must have a free and fair choice to join a union and the right to bargain collectively with their employer,\u201d Psaki added.\u00a0\u201cThe Amazon workers in Staten Island made their choice to organize a grassroots union and bargain for better jobs and a better life.\u201d<\/p>\n

The Amazon Labor Union\u2019s victory came despite the group not being affiliated with any larger union coalition such as the Retail, Wholesale and Department Store Union, which is supporting Amazon union efforts in Bessemer, Alabama.<\/p>\n

Instead, the organizing group has remained entirely independent since it was formed last year.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The union\u2019s decisive victory came on the same day Amazon disclosed CEO Andy Jassy\u00a0took in a whopping $212 million\u00a0in total compensation last year.\u00a0<\/p>\n

About 8,300 people work at the Staten Island warehouse, which is called JFK8. Fewer than 5,000 voted in the union election.<\/p>\n

\u201cWe\u2019re disappointed with the outcome of the election in Staten Island because we believe having a direct relationship with the company is best for our employees,\u201d Amazon said in a statement. \u201cWe\u2019re evaluating our options, including filing objections based on the inappropriate and undue influence by the NLRB that we and others (including the National Retail Federation and US Chamber of Commerce) witnessed in this election.\u201d<\/p>\n

Amazon\u2019s JFK8 warehouse in Staten Island employs about 7,500 people. Bloomberg via Getty Images <\/p>\n

The union\u2019s victory comes after years of knock-down, drag-out fighting between Amazon and activists.\u00a0<\/p>\n

In Staten Island, Amazon held mandatory anti-union meetings with employees at JFK8 \u2014 and blanketed them with letters and online ads urging them to vote against the effort.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cWhen you know, you vote NO,\u201d one of Amazon\u2019s Facebook and Instagram ads read.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Amazon founder Jeff Bezos \u2014 here with his girlfriend Lauren Sanchez \u2014 is worth an estimated $191 billion. <\/p>\n

Amazon also hired Global Strategy Group, a Democratic Party-aligned public relations firm with close ties to disgraced former Gov. Andrew Cuomo, to blast employees with anti-union messages, CNBC reported Thursday.\u00a0<\/p>\n

And when the Amazon Labor Union\u2019s leader, ex-Amazon employee Chris Smalls, came to a JFK8 break room in February to give food to employees, Amazon called the cops and had him arrested for trespassing, Reuters reported.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Company executives also plotted to smear Smalls, holding a strategy meeting in 2020 that included founder and then-CEO Jeff Bezos where they called him\u00a0\u201cnot smart or articulate,\u201d\u00a0Vice reported.\u00a0<\/p>\n

But Smalls had the last laugh, popping champagne and declaring victory outside the NLRB\u2019s Brooklyn office on Friday.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cWe worked, had fun and made history,\u201d Smalls wrote on Twitter. \u201cWelcome the 1st union in America for Amazon.\u201d<\/p>\n

Another Amazon warehouse on Staten Island, LDJ5, is set to hold a union election starting on April 25.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Amazon blanketed employees with anti-union messages. Pacific Press\/LightRocket via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Amazon stock was trading up about 0.3% shortly after the union vote result was announced.\u00a0<\/p>\n

On Thursday, labor officials said a separate union vote at an Amazon warehouse in Bessemer, Alabama, would likely be decided in the coming days by 416 challenged ballots. After the initial count was finished on Thursday, 993 votes had been cast against union representation and 875 for unionization.<\/p>\n

Since the margin that the Bessemer union lost by is less than the number of challenged ballots, the final decision will hinge on the challenged ballots.\u00a0<\/p>\n

This year\u2019s Bessemer election appears to be a much closer fight than last year\u2019s vote, in which labor activists lost by a two-to-one margin. Federal labor officials ordered a new election after they found that Amazon had interfered with the election process. <\/p>\n

A separate union election at Amazon\u2019s Bessemer, Alabama, warehouse will come down to 416 challenged ballots. AFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Amazon is not the only major US corporation to face a labor push in recent months. More than 160 Starbucks locations across the country have filed petitions to organize, according to NLRB data \u2014 and nine stores have successfully voted to unionize since December.\u00a0<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
