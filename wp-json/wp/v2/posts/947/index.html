Sony Group\u2019s\u00a0revamp of its PlayStation subscription service is a major step toward levelling the playing field with Microsoft\u2019s rival Game Pass, although holding back new releases may dampen enthusiasm, analysts say.<\/p>\n

Sony said its expanded PlayStation Plus service will offer hundreds of games, including recent hits such as \u201cSpider-Man: Miles Morales,\u201d for a flat monthly fee when it launches in the United States, Europe and Japan in June.<\/p>\n

The service will not include new releases, Sony Interactive Entertainment CEO Jim Ryan told Reuters, but users can still find big-budget titles such as \u201cReturnal\u201d and \u201cGod of War\u201d.<\/p>\n

\u201cThere will be more from PlayStation Studios, and all of the major publishers will be represented,\u201d Ryan said.<\/p>\n

Sony is widely seen as leading in the console war, selling 17 million PlayStation 5 (PS5) units even as chip shortages hit production, but has been under pressure to respond to the growth of Netflix-style game subscription services.<\/p>\n

The revamped Sony service combines the current PlayStation Plus, which has 48 million subscribers and offers online gaming and only a few free games each month, with PlayStation Now, which has 3.2 million users and a library of games to download and stream.<\/p>\n

Sony is widely seen as leading in the console war, selling 17 million PlayStation 5 units even as chip shortages hit production.SOPA Images\/LightRocket via Gett <\/p>\n

There are three tiers, priced at $9.99 to $17.99 monthly or $59.99 to $119.99 annually. The lowest-price tier, PlayStation Plus Essential, looks like Sony\u2019s current offering.<\/p>\n

PlayStation Plus Extra adds a catalog of PS4 and PS5 games. PlayStation Plus Premium offers a further library of older titles, cloud streaming in major markets and time-limited game trials.<\/p>\n

Such streamlining is \u201coverdue,\u201d said Lewis Ward, head of gaming research at IDC.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe number of PlayStation subscribers in the higher two tiers \u2026 will tick up over time, which is undoubtedly a core reason for this change,\u201d Ward said.<\/p>\n

Sony\u2019s newly announced pricing is seen as competitive with Microsoft\u2019s Game Pass service.Bloomberg via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Given the high cost of developing new games, analysts had raised concerns that pressure to bundle more content with subscriptions may eat into profit at Sony\u2019s coveted games unit.<\/p>\n

Sony will seek to preserve new game sales alongside the subscription service.<\/p>\n

\u201cI think this move should improve margins, as more gamers will be driven to higher-cost subscriptions,\u201d said Piers Harding-Rolls, head of games research at Ampere Analysis.<\/p>\n

\u201cIf content acquisition costs do increase, Sony will need to balance that with the additional revenue it will be generating from the PS Plus subscriber base,\u201d he added.<\/p>\n

Sony\u2019s newly announced pricing is seen as competitive. The premium tier\u2019s $120 annual fee compares with $180 for Game Pass Ultimate. Both offer cloud gaming, with Sony users able to stream titles to their consoles and PC.<\/p>\n

Cloud gaming is seen as the future for the industry because it doesn\u2019t require downloading or installing games on a console or PC. That is a potential threat to the console business, whose operators have acted as industry gatekeepers.<\/p>\n

An annual subscription to the mid-tier PlayStation Plus Extra, which lacks this cloud-gaming feature, works out at just over $8 monthly, compared with $9.99 for the Game Pass.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
