Apple\u2019s iPhones are well-oiled machines \u2013 but there are some key warning signs that things are working properly.<\/p>\n

From accidental water damage to signs of snooping, we reveal six clues that something is very wrong with your\u00a0iPhone.<\/p>\n

Battery health<\/h2>\n

Your iPhone uses a lithium-ion battery, which has a limited lifespan.<\/p>\n

Every time you\u00a0discharge and charge your iPhone, you wear down its maximum battery capacity.<\/p>\n

Over two years (or about 500 charging cycles), you\u2019d expect to have about 80% of battery capacity left.<\/p>\n

That means an iPhone that would\u2019ve lasted for 10 hours of use would now only survive an 8-hour stint off-charge.<\/p>\n

You can check your battery health by going into Settings > Battery and tapping Battery Health.<\/p>\n

If your iPhone has\u00a0a very low battery health\u00a0and it\u2019s dying too quickly, consider upgrading to a newer model.<\/p>\n

Alternatively, you can get an out-of-warranty battery replacement from\u00a0Apple\u00a0for $69\/\u00a369. This is a much cheaper option than buying a new iPhone.<\/p>\n

Phone randomly restarting<\/h2>\n

Your iPhone randomly restarting isn\u2019t always a major cause for concern \u2013 but it could be.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s especially problematic if it\u2019s happening a lot, for no apparent reason.<\/p>\n

One simple cause for your iPhone restarting is that\u00a0it got too hot or cold.<\/p>\n

A cold restart isn\u2019t too bad \u2013 it should return to normal once the battery warms up.<\/p>\n

But overheating can seriously damage parts of the iPhone, including the battery.<\/p>\n

Other reasons for a restart could be internal damage, or possibly some software issue \u2013 like a dodgy app.<\/p>\n

If your iPhone is frequently turning off (and not because of temperature) then you should get it checked out.<\/p>\n

Camera and microphone dots<\/h2>\n

Your iPhone has two warning signs to show if you\u2019re being spied on.<\/p>\n

They\u2019ll appear as either\u00a0green or orange dots at the top of the phone.<\/p>\n

They don\u2019t necessarily mean someone is snooping \u2013 but they might, and can prompt you to investigate.<\/p>\n

The alert was added in an\u00a0iOS update\u00a0in 2020 and appears whenever your microphone or camera is activated.<\/p>\n

That means if any app is secretly recording you, you\u2019ll be warned about it.<\/p>\n

To see the dots, you\u2019ll need to be using a device operating iOS 14 or 15 \u2013 go to Settings > General > Software Update to check.<\/p>\n

A green dot will appear in the upper right corner of the display when the camera\u2019s activated.<\/p>\n

And it shows as orange if the microphone is activated.<\/p>\n

By swiping into your Control Centre, you\u2019ll be able to see details about which app is using the microphone.<\/p>\n

So if it\u2019s an app that you think shouldn\u2019t be using the camera or microphone, you might want to worry.<\/p>\n

If you think something is snooping on you when it shouldn\u2019t be, you should check the app\u2019s permissions in Settings.<\/p>\n

You can even deny specific apps access to your microphone or camera, for instance.<\/p>\n

And if you\u2019re really worried, you could just delete the app altogether.<\/p>\n

No more iOS updates<\/h2>\n

Apple regularly introduces new versions of its iPhone software \u2013 and\u00a0we\u2019re currently on iOS 15.<\/p>\n

Eventually, Apple stops supporting older models with updates, which is\u00a0very bad news if you\u2019re affected.<\/p>\n

These updates include vital security updates that patch serious problems with your device.<\/p>\n

So if you can\u2019t get those updates, you\u2019re at serious risk of being hacked.<\/p>\n

The current iOS 15.4 supports phones all the way back to the iPhone 6S and iPhone SE (1st gen).<\/p>\n

Check if you\u2019re still getting the latest updates in Settings > General > Software Update.<\/p>\n

If you\u2019re not then it\u2019s highly recommended that you upgrade your iPhone to a newer model that still gets security updates.<\/p>\n

Ghost touches<\/h2>\n

Ghost touches are when your iPhone \u201ctaps\u201d parts of the screen without you doing anything.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s very unusual for this to be a software issue.<\/p>\n

Instead, it\u2019s most likely linked to screen or water damage, or some other kind of internal issue.<\/p>\n

If you notice this (especially if it\u2019s getting worse) then take your iPhone to the Apple Store for a check-up.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s possible that you might need parts replaced. This could end up being costly, so it may even be cheaper to buy a new iPhone depending on the model.<\/p>\n

Red water strip<\/h2>\n

Water damage is the bane of any gadget owner\u2019s life.<\/p>\n

If liquid gets inside your iPhone, it can void the warranty and result in costly repairs.<\/p>\n

When you take an iPhone to be fixed, Apple will usually check the Liquid Contact Indicator (LCI).<\/p>\n

This is a part of the device that changes color when it comes into contact with water.<\/p>\n

You can\u00a0find this yourself: on recent iPhone models, it\u2019s inside the SIM card slot.<\/p>\n

Use a torch and magnifying glass if you\u2019re struggling to see the color.<\/p>\n

The LCI will normally be white or silver but turns red if it\u2019s been in contact with water.<\/p>\n

Apple has a\u00a0page\u00a0revealing how to find the LCI on every iPhone model.<\/p>\n

\u201cAn LCI will be activated when it comes into contact with water or a liquid containing water,\u201d said Apple.<\/p>\n

\u201cAn LCI won\u2019t be activated because of humidity and temperature changes that are within the product\u2019s environmental requirements.\u201d<\/p>\n

Most modern iPhones are water-resistant, but it\u2019s a good idea to avoid soakings where possible.<\/p>\n
