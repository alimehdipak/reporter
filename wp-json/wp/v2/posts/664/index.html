<\/p>\n

Rich women across the world have a secret way of making big bucks: selling their castoffs on the QT. <\/p>\n

According to Ben Hemminger, founder of upscale reseller Fashionphile, \u201cThey have more bags than they need or want, so they get rid of them to make room in the closet \u2014 or to justify buying another one, and to hide it from their significant other,\u201d he told The Post.<\/p>\n

It takes a lot of wealthy women to keep Fashionphile\u2019s Herm\u00e8s stock at its usual levels: 400 or so Birkin bags and 200 slightly cheaper Kellys at any one time. And it costs Hemminger heftily, too. Two years ago, he bought a Himalaya-edition Birkin with ultra-rare hand-painted finish \u2014 original retail price $50,000 \u2014\u00a0 from one woman for $100,000.<\/p>\n

He then sold it on the site for $150,000. Recently, it cost him $180,000 to buy a similar bag from a different client; he sold that one for $230,000. Fashionphile isn\u2019t too snooty to handle relatively cheaper secondhand treats, too, like a Vuitton cardholder; these cost $250 at the LV store but are regularly out of stock.<\/p>\n

Rare Birkin bags can go for as much as $230,000 secondhand. One bag that was originally purchased from an Herm\u00e8s store for $50,000 resold for three times that amount.Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cEven at $450, that flies off our shelves in two days,\u201d Hemminger said.<\/p>\n

One of his wealthy clients admitted to The Post that she is easily bored by the assortment of designer bags in her closet. <\/p>\n

\u201cI\u2019ve always had a knack for fashion, but also have a pretty big shopping addiction,\u201d the wealthy client said. \u201cI would go into the\u00a0Chanel boutique to buy brand new bags all the time, but I was always looking for ways to transition things out of my closet and bring in new ones.\u201d<\/p>\n

Over the last seven years, she\u2019s sold top-tier purses regularly to Hemminger\u2019s team, like a Birkin 25cm bag that cost her just over $10,000, retail, from Herm\u00e8s. \u201cI think I made a $1,000 profit, or maybe it was $2,000 or something,\u201d she said of selling it on Fashionphile.<\/p>\n

Jackie Kennedy Onassis used to liquidate her closet on the downlow to help fund her shopping habits after husband Aristotle Onassis cut her budget.Bettmann Archive <\/p>\n

Dealers say there are also women keen to spend but kept on tight reins by their penny-
watching spouses. <\/p>\n

It\u2019s not a new idea, of course. Jackie Onassis was the OG of VIP \u201crecommerce.\u201d When she wed the tight-fisted shipping magnate Aristotle Onassis, she spent more than a million bucks on outfits in the first year; he responded by slashing her spending allowance to just $30,000 per month. With no cash, but plenty of cachet, Jackie turned to liquidating her closet on the downlow to help float her shopping habits.<\/p>\n

The way it works now is, if there\u2019s a wealthy woman agonizing over whether she should \u2014 or could get away with \u2014 putting a pricey purse on her black AmEx, the sales associate leans in to whisper a suggestion: Perhaps she has an older bag at home she could sell, and then buy this new treat for cash? He could recommend a great place to do that, too.<\/p>\n

Retired vintage dealer Lisa Stark, whose business was based in Palm Beach, told The Post that sales associates at Saks would \u201ctell them to bring their bags to me.\u201d<\/p>\n

Ben Hemminger is the founder of upscale reseller Fashionphile.Courtesy of Ben Hemminger <\/p>\n

When he started out, Hemminger admitted, he would press a few bills into a few sales
associates\u2019 palms to encourage them to mention that option. Handily, one of Fashionphile\u2019s first brick-and-mortar locations was in an alleyway behind a line of boldfaced boutiques in Beverly Hills.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s now set to open a 4,000-square-foot warehouse and store in the Starrett-Lehigh building in New York City\u2019s Chelsea neighborhood next month.<\/p>\n

And three years ago, Neiman Marcus took a minority stake in the company and added in-store boutiques at several of its locations, making it even more convenient to trade up. <\/p>\n

For some women, it\u2019s a matter of getting rid of bad memories.<\/p>\n

Vintage dealer Lisa Stark told The Post that sales associates at Saks would tell high-spending customers to \u201cbring their [old] bags to me.\u201d <\/p>\n

\u201cWhen I was engaged I bought\u00a0Zimmerman, Alice and Olivia and\u00a0Retrofete\u00a0dresses for my shower and engagement parties,\u201d said one woman who winters in Palm Beach and summers in the Hamptons. \u201cThe wedding was called off, and two years later I didn\u2019t have room for their energy in my closet, so I sold them on the Real Real for almost what I paid for them.\u201d<\/p>\n

Some people even gamify their resell action.<\/p>\n

Take the periodic offer Herm\u00e8s extends to its most cherished clients: the chance to custom-order a \u201chorseshoe\u201d Birkin, specially stamped with the equine insignia that gives it its nickname. Clients are known to buy them just to sell them.<\/p>\n

Some customers will ask resellers what colorways of exclusive Birkin bags will fetch the most, then order those just to turn a profit.Bloomberg via Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cSomeone will call us and say \u2018OK, I can get a horseshoe bag so what combination of colors do you want that will sell?’\u201d Taylor said. A \u201chorseshoe\u201d bag that retails for $30,000-40,000 could fetch up to $100,000 when she resells it.<\/p>\n

A socialite who works in the beauty industry told The Post:\u00a0\u201cI\u00a0bought\u00a0a Chanel bag five years ago for $6,000. I got tired of it, so in my latest closet clean-out, I dumped it on the Real Real for $7,400.\u00a0Chanel recently announced that they were raising their prices so it was a fast sale.\u201d<\/p>\n

And if the secondhand market isn\u2019t enough to fund a shopping habit, there\u2019s always creative financing like Fashionphile\u2019s Reserve program, which allows buyers to put down a 25 percent deposit, then pay off the balance over time. <\/p>\n

\u201cI asked one woman point-blank: \u2018I know you can afford to buy this Birkin today, so why are you using our layaway program?’\u201d Hemminger said. \u201d And she said, \u2018My husband never looks at credit card charges under $5,000 so if I break it up into $4,000 increments he\u2019ll never ask.’\u201d<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
