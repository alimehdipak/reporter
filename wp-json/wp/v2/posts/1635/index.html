<\/p>\n

A gung-ho investor claims to have dropped thousands of dollars on a plot of virtual land.<\/p>\n

Writing for the Singaporean website\u00a0StackedHomes\u00a0last month, the cash-flushed risk-taker explained their reasoning for apparently dropping $14,000 on real estate in the metaverse.<\/p>\n

The writer, known only as Cheryl, believes that investing in metaverse property has advantages over real-world property \u2013 though it comes with its own risks.<\/p>\n

The\u00a0metaverse\u00a0is a loosely defined virtual realm made up of connected 3D virtual worlds where people can work, play or do business.<\/p>\n

Visitors to the social spaces can buy goods and services, such as property, much like they would in the real world.<\/p>\n

And while that largely takes the form of a few square meters of space that fetch a small fee, prices can go into the hundreds of thousands.<\/p>\n

Vast metaverse estates are fetching big prices as investors buy up land where they can build virtual casinos, malls, homes and more.<\/p>\n

Traded in\u00a0cryptocurrencies, they are owned as NFTs. Much like with real property, owners splash out in the hopes they\u2019ll be worth more in future.<\/p>\n

However, just like their real-world counterparts, the value of the plots can go up or down. They are considered a risky investment.<\/p>\n

Cheryl wrote: \u201cFor me, digital real estate and the metaverse will be playing a major role in how humans communicate, transact, and interact.<\/p>\n

\u201cAs such, buying digital real estate in the metaverse is like putting your stake into the next wave of technology, commercialism, and ways of socializing.<\/p>\n

\u201cIt is pretty much like buying real estate in a country. Just that this country is a digital one, and people are not really living inside it.\u201d<\/p>\n

The mysterious investor claims to have spent $19,000 SGD ($14,000) on a plot of land in the Decentraland metaverse.<\/p>\n

They say they hope to in future rent it out to businesses or place advertising on it to make a bit of extra cash on the side.<\/p>\n

After walking readers through the process of buying their own Decentraland property, the investor gave a word of warning.<\/p>\n

\u201cI have to remind you guys to ensure the authenticity of your purchase \u2013 scammers are everywhere, so beware.\u201d<\/p>\n

Like any investment, there\u2019s also the risk that it will LOSE value.<\/p>\n

It may have experienced something of a boom, but following months of rising prices, metaverse property prices appear to be\u00a0taking a tumble.<\/p>\n

According to analytics company Meta Metrics, the average price of metaverse property dropped 18 percent in March.<\/p>\n

That\u2019s in stark contrast to the preceding six months when prices rose nine-fold. Sales in January topped $85million.<\/p>\n

However, despite the dip, things are still looking rosy for investors.<\/p>\n

Land pricing was still four times higher in March than it was a year earlier when properties were going for $2,131 on average.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s unlikely that the blip will curb sales as interest in the industry grows.<\/p>\n

Sales of virtual real estate topped $500million last year and could double this year, according to analysts and investors.<\/p>\n

So far, metaverse real estate sales have been concentrated on the \u201cBig Four\u201d \u2013 Sandbox, Decentraland, Cryptovoxels and Somnium.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s likely that other sales booms will sweep the industry in future as new metaverses become the flavors of the week.<\/p>\n
