A former senior-level worker at TikTok claimed that managers made employees work 12-hour days six days a week.<\/p>\n

Pabel Martinez, who had earned $220,000 a year at the wildly popular video-sharing app, claimed that managers at TikTok would frequently ask employees to hop on meetings during weekends in addition to working after hours, according to Business Insider.<\/p>\n

Martinez, who said he previously had worked for three years at Facebook\u2019s parent company, Meta, said he quit earlier this year after joining the Chinese-owned company last February, according to the report.<\/p>\n

\u201cI do\u00a0think that the culture of working too much or not having as much of the work-life balance does permeate throughout the organization, and it is often encouraged you work \u2018after hours\u2019,\u201d he told Insider. \u201cThe 996 policy\u2019s infamous.\u201d<\/p>\n

The numbers \u201c996\u201d refer to business practices in China, where workers are expected to start at 9 a.m. and end at 9 p.m. six days per week.<\/p>\n

A source at TikTok told The Post that the company does not institute a culture of \u201c996\u201d.<\/p>\n

Late last year, TikTok\u2019s parent company, ByteDance, scaled back working hours, telling employees they were expected to clock in at 10 a.m. and finish at 7 p.m. The work week would be limited to Monday to Friday.<\/p>\n

Nevertheless, employees reported that they were still required to log in to meetings at odd hours because of the time difference between the company\u2019s US-based operations and its headquarters in Beijing.<\/p>\n

Pabel Martinez said TikTok forces employees to attend meetings on weekends.SOPA Images\/LightRocket via Gett <\/p>\n

Martinez said that when he objected to being forced to attend meetings on Saturdays and Sundays, he was told: \u201cThat\u2019s not how we do business here.\u201d<\/p>\n

He said he was also reprimanded when he publicly shared his $220,000 annual wage on LinkedIn as part of \u201cLatina Equal Pay Day.\u201d<\/p>\n

Martinez said he felt pressured from stringent, bi-monthly revenue and growth targets as well as 360-degree performance reviews.<\/p>\n

\u201cI was made to feel like I was never doing enough,\u201d Martinez told Insider.\u00a0\u201cAt TikTok, no conversation started with \u2018How are you?\u2019 It was like, \u2018How\u2019s the revenue? What are we doing to drive more growth?’\u201d <\/p>\n

Martinez said TikTok has adopted the \u201c996\u201d culture of working 9 a.m. to 9 p.m. six days a week.SOPA Images\/LightRocket via Gett <\/p>\n

TikTok didn\u2019t immediately respond to a request for comment from The Post.<\/p>\n

TikTok told Insider that it sought to cultivate a \u201cculture of transparency and feedback\u201d through surveys handed out to employees. The company also said that because it was a global company, it needed to coordinate meetings across various time zones.<\/p>\n

TikTok said it was working to hire more localized personnel who could function more autonomously and thus negate the need for meetings at odd hours.<\/p>\n

The firm also said it offered generous parental leave and that it had instituted a policy of no meetings during hours on certain days.<\/p>\n

\u201cOur focus is on building and fostering a team which is empowered to support our growing global community,\u201d a TikTok spokesperson told Insider.<\/p>\n
