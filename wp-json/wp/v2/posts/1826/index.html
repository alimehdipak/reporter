<\/p>\n

Delta Air Lines has conducted tests of SpaceX\u2019s Starlink satellite internet with an eye toward potentially offering the wireless service to its passengers in the future, the carrier\u2019s top boss said this week.<\/p>\n

Delta CEO Ed Bastian said the airline has engaged in preliminary talks with SpaceX, the private space firm founded by billionaire Elon Musk. SpaceX is billing Starlink as a high-speed, low-cost internet service capable of providing access around the world.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s unclear what form the tests took or how soon Starlink might roll out on Delta\u2019s passenger planes.<\/p>\n

Bastian declined to discuss specific details about the tests, according to Wall Street Journal. Delta has previously called for expanded access to cheap, effective Wi-Fi service on flights.<\/p>\n

Delta representatives did not immediately return a request for comment on the matter.<\/p>\n

Delta CEO Ed Bastian declined to provide specific details on the tests.Bloomberg via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Musk has touted Starlink\u2019s potential as a source of internet for air travelers in recent months.<\/p>\n

Last October, the billionaire said SpaceX was \u201ctalking to airlines about installing Starlink\u201d \u2014 adding that the service would provide \u201clow latency\u201d and \u201chalf gigabit connectivity in the air.\u201d Musk has also said SpaceX was aiming to get Starlink certified for use on Boeing 737 and Airbus A320 planes because they \u201cserve [the] most number of people.\u201d<\/p>\n

Starlink had 250,000 global subscribers as of March, according to Jonathan Hofeller, one of the company\u2019s executives for commercial sales. Speaking at a conference last month, Hofeller said air travelers have come to expect better internet service.<\/p>\n

Elon Musk has teased Starlink\u2019s potential for use in-flight.Anadolu Agency via Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cThe expectation has changed faster than the technology,\u201d Hofeller said, according to the Wall Street Journal.<\/p>\n

Starlink likely faces some regulatory hurdles before it can gain wider adoption on airlines. While the Federal Communications Commission has cleared SpaceX to conduct tests for the service, any equipment installation must be approved by the Federal Aviation Administration.<\/p>\n

The FAA declined the Journal\u2019s request for comment.<\/p>\n

SpaceX plans to launch thousands of satellites into orbit to facilitate Starlink.Anadolu Agency via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Meanwhile, Delta has expanded its in-flight internet offerings \u2014 making Viasat satellite internet available on more than 300 of its planes for $5 per customer per flight.<\/p>\n

SpaceX has already launched more than 1,900 satellites into orbit \u2014 with plans to launch thousands more as it expands and improves the Starlink service.<\/p>\n

But the firm faces mounting permission from Amazon\u2019s secretive \u201cProject Kuiper,\u201d which announced plans last month for its own satellite launches to facilitate a low-cost broadband internet system.<\/p>\n
