Most women in elite social circles who collect pricey jewelry wear them as instructed: brooches below the collar bone, one earring per lobe, necklaces cascading down cleavage. And then there was Ann Getty, the red-haired society swan who passed away at 79 in 2020. Married to oil heir Gordon Getty, she was aunt to trans model Nats Getty, grandmother (practically mother) to Ivy Getty, and daughter-in-law to tycoon J. Paul Getty, whose oil empire funded the family\u2019s massive fortune. She was also a fashion trendsetter.<\/p>\n

Ann had a famously cavalier way with her gems, wearing earrings as brooches and brooches all over her body, including at her hip. Now her collection of prized gems \u2014 created by jeweler Joel Arthur Rosenthal, aka JAR \u2014 are headed for auction at Christie\u2019s New York on June 8.<\/p>\n

Famously elusive and exclusive, Rosenthal insisted on personally vetting and approving each client before agreeing to sell to them. \u201cThe privilege of being able to buy Rosenthal\u2019s pieces is about having a bank account and discernment,\u201d said a source close to the Getty family. \u201cIf he didn\u2019t want you to have his piece, he would refuse to sell to you. He wanted to sell to clients who understood what he was doing.\u201d <\/p>\n

Back in the 1980s, Ann Getty was at the center of the social scene, from her home base in San Francisco to New York. Here, the fiery-haired doyenne is seen sporting the JAR Vitrail Fleur-de-Lys brooch at the Literacy Benefit at Lincoln Center in 1985.Rose Hartman\/Getty Images Ann Getty, clad in a red gown and JAR\u2019s Fleur Pompons brooches, at her husband\u2019s annual birthday party in their San Francisco home. Drew Altizer Photography <\/p>\n

Money and status helped with this \u201cunderstanding,\u201d but did not guarantee access. Ann \u2014 who sat on the boards of the Metropolitan Museum of Art and the\u00a0New York Public Library \u2014 proved her worth by giving her JAR pieces the starring role in many of her outfits.<\/p>\n

To her husband\u2019s 76th birthday gala, she donned a red gown with three JAR Fleur Pompons brooches clipped to her breast. To the 2017 San Francisco Symphony, she rocked an enormous pink tulip brooch at her sternum, and to Gordon\u2019s 80th birthday bash at Davies Symphony Hall in San Francisco, she fastened her circa 1989 leaf brooch to the shoulder of her blouse.<\/p>\n

\u201cAnn\u2019s style was bold, unapologetic, sophisticated, creative, and daring,\u201d Anna Getty, Ann\u2019s great-niece, told The Post. \u201c[Her style] was utterly her own.\u201d<\/p>\n

Throughout much of the late 1980s and early 1990s, Ann purchased 12 pieces from JAR, a collection which is expected to fetch more than $1.5 million at auction, with proceeds going to the Ann Getty Trust.<\/p>\n

Ann\u2019s granddaughter Ivy, 27, who married her photographer boyfriend in an\u00a0over-the-top three day extravaganza\u00a0last year,has sported several of the pieces herself recently. She wore a zebra brooch for a May 2022 Vogue photo shoot and earrings as brooches to the 2022 Met Gala. In fact, Ivy\u2019s entire Met Ball look was a tribute to her grandmother: Her Oscar de la Renta gown was constructed using one of Ann\u2019s tablecloths. <\/p>\n

\u201c[Ann] didn\u2019t keep these [pieces] locked up in a vault. She wore them all the time\u2026she wanted me to just appreciate her things as art,\u201d Ivy told Vogue earlier this month. <\/p>\n

In 2005, Prince Charles and Camilla Parker Bowles joined Ann Getty, who wore JAR\u2019s Fleur Pompons, for a performance of Beach Blanket Babylon at San Francisco\u2019s Club Fugazi.ZUMAPRESS.com Ivy Getty, Ann\u2019s granddaughter, wears the same pansy earrings in the style of her grandmother \u2014 as brooches at her breast and hipbone \u2014 to the 2022 Met Gala. Dimitrios Kambouris\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

Ann wore her JAR jewels both boldly and casually: On the drapery of a gown to Patron\u2019s Dinner for the 1991 Black and White Ball, on the collar of a black cashmere tee shirt for Gordon\u2019s 85th birthday party at their San Francisco home. <\/p>\n

She was a hostess who could effortlessly organize a black-tie dinner party for 60, but also spent much of the 1990s on her hands and knees digging for artifacts on expeditions in Ethiopia and Turkey as a fellow with the Leaky Foundation. In between digs, Getty would throw on her couture \u2014 and one of her JAR treasures \u2014 with minimal prompting.<\/p>\n

\u201cWith San Francisco society women, like author Danielle Steele, you noticed the jewelry collection because it was always on display,\u201d said a family friend who lives four blocks from the famous Getty mansion in Pacific Heights. \u201cWith Ann, though, she would put a brooch on her shoulder or on the bustle of her skirt, which made the whole look seem less flashy.\u201d<\/p>\n

In the past, Rosenthal, who is now 79 and living in Venice, hasn\u2019t looked favorably on clients who\u2019ve sold off his creations. He hasn\u2019t spoken to Ellen Barkin since she famously dispensed with her collection of JAR pieces during her divorce from industrialist Ron Perelman in 2006. <\/p>\n
