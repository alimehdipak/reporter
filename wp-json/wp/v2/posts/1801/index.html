They kept the receipt.<\/p>\n

Thought paying $120,000 for a banana on a wall was extreme? Earlier this week, a receipt for a piece of \u201cinvisible art\u201d eclipsed expectations after being auctioned off in Paris for nearly $1.2 million.<\/p>\n

\u201cThis work is guaranteed and received an irrevocable bid,\u201d auction house Sotheby\u2019s wrote in its catalog regarding the pay stub, which went for $1,151,467.40 \u2014 over twice the estimated price of $551,000. <\/p>\n

Naturally, paying money for nothing might seem strange. However, the receipt was a rare remnant from \u201cZones of Immaterial Pictorial Sensibility,\u201d\u00a0a mid-1900s exhibition series in which pioneering French performance artist Yves Klein would sell vacant rooms to collectors in exchange for gold bullion. <\/p>\n

The radical visionary would then invite collectors to burn the receipts and dump half the gold into the Seine River \u2014 the logic being that in doing so they\u2019d become \u201cdefinitive owner\u201d of the \u201czone.\u201d<\/p>\n

A receipt intended to authenticate the transfer of an invisible work by the French artist Yves Klein, entitled \u201cZone of Immaterial Pictorial Sensitivity Series N\u00b01, Zone N\u00b002\u201d is displayed at Sotheby\u2019s auction house on April 1 in Paris. Getty Images A portrait of French artist Yves Klein.Getty Images <\/p>\n

The aforementioned certificate of authentication survived because the collector, Jacques Kugel, refused to set it alight, the Guardian reported. It has since been displayed everywhere from London to Paris, before getting bought by ex-gallery owner Lo\u00efc Malle, who auctioned it off along with other pieces from his collection.<\/p>\n

Designed to imitate a bank check, the 8-inch-wide ticket sports Klein\u2019s signature and is dated December 7, 1959 \u2014 several years before the artist\u2019s death in 1962.<\/p>\n

Experts have since credited the provocateur with anticipating the modern NFT.<\/p>\n

The receipt was dated December 7, 1959 \u2014 several years before the artist\u2019s death in 1963.Getty Images Klein has even been credited with anticipating the rise of NFTs.Sotheby’s <\/p>\n

\u201cSome have likened the transfer of a zone of sensitivity and the invention of receipts as an ancestor of the NFT, which itself allows the exchange of immaterial works,\u201d Sotheby\u2019s wrote in the catalog. <\/p>\n

As of yet, it\u2019s too early to say whether the buyer, who is identified only as a \u201cprivate European collector,\u201d is purchasing the receipt with cryptocurrency.<\/p>\n
