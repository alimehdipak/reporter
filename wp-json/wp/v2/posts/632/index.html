<\/p>\n

One of America\u2019s most acclaimed authors is wading into the culture wars, saying she believes she should be able to create characters from a diverse range of backgrounds. <\/p>\n

Anne Tyler, 80, spoke out on the issue in an interview with the Sunday Times, stating, \u201cI\u2019m astonished by the appropriation issue. It would be very foolish for me to write, let\u2019s say, a novel from the viewpoint of a black man, but I think I should be allowed to do it.\u201d<\/p>\n

Tyler \u2014 who won the 1989 Pulitzer Prize for fiction for her novel \u201cBreathing Lessons\u201d \u2014 also hit out at cancel culture. <\/p>\n

\u201cIf an incredibly talented person has written novels in the 1930s or \u201940s and all of a sudden it is discovered that there was something he said or did \u2014 even something as bad as sexual harassment \u2014 he should be condemned for it but I don\u2019t see why you should withdraw his novels from publication,\u201d she told the British publication.<\/p>\n

Tyler is one of America\u2019s most acclaimed fiction writers.Getty Images <\/p>\n

Tyler\u2019s comments sparked debate on Twitter, with some claiming Tyler simply wanted to avoid any kind of criticism. <\/p>\n

\u201cNo one\u2019s saying you can\u2019t, just that you shouldn\u2019t [write about black characters]. Sounds like you don\u2019t want the consequences of such an action. And that\u2019s privilege,\u201d one user wrote.<\/p>\n

However, others encouraged Tyler to freely write about whatever took her fancy. <\/p>\n

\u201cWriters are supposed to be truth-tellers and be free to tell whatever story they wish. The Social Justice Warriors don\u2019t dictate what a writer can or cannot do,\u201d one advocate stated.<\/p>\n

Tyler\u2019s comments sparked debate on Twitter.Getty Images <\/p>\n

The best-selling author is notoriously reclusive and rarely gives interviews. <\/p>\n

Tyler \u2014 who has been described by the British press as \u201cAmerica\u2019s answer to Jane Austen\u201d \u2014 is set to release her 24th novel, \u201cFrench Braid,\u201d on Tuesday. <\/p>\n

In addition to \u201cBreathing Lessons,\u201d she also penned the best sellers \u201cDinner at the Homesick Restaurant\u201d and \u201cThe Accidental Tourist,\u201d both of which were nominated for Pulitzer Prizes. The latter was turned into an Oscar-winning film starring Geena Davis and the late William Hurt. <\/p>\n

In recent years, debates have raged in academic circles over whether authors can write from the perspective of fictional characters with whom they do not share immutable characteristics. <\/p>\n

Tyler is the latest prominent author to speak out against cancel culture.Getty Images <\/p>\n

Back in 2020, for instance, the best seller \u201cAmerican Dirt,\u201d written by white author Jeanine Cummins, faced accusations of cultural appropriation as it revolved around the experiences of Mexican migrants.<\/p>\n

That same year, several prominent authors \u2014 including Margaret Atwood, Jeffrey Eugenides, Salman Rushie and J.K. Rowling \u2014 signed an open letter published in Harper\u2019s that bemoaned the increasing censorship in the arts. <\/p>\n

\u201cEditors are fired for running controversial pieces; books are withdrawn for alleged inauthenticity; journalists are barred from writing on certain topics,\u201d the letter asserted. \u201cAs writers we need a culture that leaves us room for experimentation, risk-taking, and even mistakes.\u201d<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
