Cosmonauts may one day be producing medical-grade marijuana in space.<\/p>\n

Retired Canadian astronaut Chris Hadfield recently spoke to\u00a0Futurism\u00a0about the future of growing\u00a0cannabis\u00a0plants in space.<\/p>\n

Hadfield, who has spent time on both the Russian space station Mir and the International Space Station (ISS), believes that someday medical marijuana may be a thing in space.<\/p>\n

He made his point by adding that mind-altering substances have always been a part of human nature.<\/p>\n

\u201cPeople have been chewing on mushrooms and various types of roots and berries for forever. There\u2019s always a role in society or human behavior for that,\u201d he added.<\/p>\n

At the present moment, astronauts cannot afford to be intoxicated or inebriated in space, Hadfield said, but as space tourism grows, so might the feasibility of recreational drug use in the cosmos.<\/p>\n

\u201cOn the space station, if there\u2019s an emergency, you are the fire department. You can\u2019t have intoxicated yourself or inebriated yourself or whatever, just because if something goes wrong, then you\u2019ll die,\u201d Hadfield said.<\/p>\n

\u201cOnce the population gets large enough, once you get to a stable enough situation, people are gonna want, you know, a drink. People are gonna want some pot,\u201d he added.<\/p>\n

Last year, Hadfield even joined the board of advisers\u00a0for BioHarvest, a biotech company that focuses on plant cell growth at an industrial scale via liquid bioreactors.<\/p>\n

Hadfield told Futurism that the company, which is currently focusing on providing future astronauts with zero gravity-enhanced nutrients, has worked with cannabis plants for years.<\/p>\n

Astronauts cant be high or drunk in space for safety reasons, but as space tourism grows, people may be able to enjoy marijuana and alcohol. Shutterstock <\/p>\n

BioHarvest has found a way to grow trichomes or the \u201clittle active part that you\u2019re actually trying to grow\u201d using bioreactors, Hadfield said.<\/p>\n

By using this novel method, researchers have been able to hone in on producing and growing just the reactive part of the plant.<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s actually replicating the natural growing process of the part that is of use to us but without the whole plant,\u201d Hadfield explained.<\/p>\n

Still, as BioHarvest\u2019s cannabis is not yet regulated for the ISS, medical marijuana may not be grown in space for a while, according to the company\u2019s CEO Ilan Sobel.<\/p>\n

However, Sobel did note that \u201conce cannabis becomes federally legal across the USA,\u201d the company\u2019s technology \u201cmay present a breakthrough from a biological science perspective\u201d.<\/p>\n

The unique microgravity environment in space may even produce medical-grade cannabis that\u2019s more effective than what we grow on Earth.<\/p>\n

\u201cWe see the potential ability for valuable minor cannabinoids to be grown at significantly higher quantities compared to its growth on Earth,\u201d Sobel said.<\/p>\n

\u201cThese unique compositions of full-spectrum cannabis could have significant value in providing more optimized treatment solutions for many palliative diseases where current pharma synthesized compounds are not delivering adequate solutions,\u201d he added.<\/p>\n
