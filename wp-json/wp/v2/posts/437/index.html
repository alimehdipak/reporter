New coronavirus lockdowns in China are snarling production for everything from Apple iPhones to Toyota SUVs \u2014 and threatening to make already red-hot global inflation even worse.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Key Apple suppliers including Foxconn have been forced to shut down factories in Shenzhen, where iPhones, iPads and Mac computers are produced, after the regional government placed the city into lockdown on Sunday.\u00a0<\/p>\n

A ban on non-essential work in Shenzhen \u2014 which is known as China\u2019s Silicon Valley and is home to major tech firms like Tencent and Huawei \u2014 is set to last for at least one week.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cThe lockdown couldn\u2019t have come in a worse city at a worse time,\u201d Wedbush Securities managing director Dan Ives told The Post. \u201cIf this lasts beyond a week or two, it will be a backbreaker for production.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Foxconn did not immediately respond to a request for comment but the company told the Wall Street Journal that it\u2019s shifting production from Shenzhen to factories elsewhere in China that have not been locked down to help keep up with demand.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The lockdown comes just days before Apple is set to release new versions of its lower-cost iPhone SE and iPad Air.SAM YEH <\/p>\n

The Foxconn shutdowns come just days before Apple plans to release new versions of its lower-cost iPhone SE and iPad Air, among other new products. Ives said the immediate rollout of those devices shouldn\u2019t be affected since they have already been shipped.\u00a0<\/p>\n

However, an extended lockdown would affect production of both the iPhone SE and Apple\u2019s flagship iPhone 13, the analyst said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cUltimately Apple doesn\u2019t have a demand problem, they have a supply problem that just got worse,\u201d he said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Authorities locked down the Chinese city of Shenzhen for at least a week. Future Publishing <\/p>\n

Apple did not immediately respond to a request for comment. The company\u2019s shares were trading down 1.6% early Friday at $152.23 even as the S&P 500 index was up 0.2%.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Meanwhile, another lockdown in the northeastern Chinese region of Changchun is creating problems for at least two major automakers.<\/p>\n

A Toyota plant in the region that produces RAV4 SUVs was shut down for an indefinite period of time starting on Monday, spokesperson Shiori Hashimoto told The Post.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Toyota and Volkswagen plants in the northeastern Chinese region of Changchun are closed. Future Publishing \t \t <\/p>\n

\u201cWe plan to resume operations as soon as the government\u2019s instructions and the safety and security of the local community and our business partners are confirmed and secured,\u201d Hashimoto said. \u201cAt this time, operations at other plants have not been affected.\u201d<\/p>\n

Volkswagen plants in the region that typically produce sedans under both the Volkswagen and Audi brands were shut down on Monday, spokesperson Leslie Bothge told The Post. She said the company expects the shutdown to last until at least Wednesday.\u00a0<\/p>\n

An ongoing chip shortage in 2020 and 2021 snarled production at auto plants and sent vehicle prices soaring, contributing to overall inflation. A continuing shortage of new cars could help keep inflation high in 2022.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The new wave of lockdowns also sent shockwaves through Chinese tech stocks. Alibaba plunged 9%, while Tencent shares fell 5.2% on Monday. The broad-based Shanghai Composite Index was down 2.6%.\u00a0<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
