<\/p>\n

NASA is getting ready to test out its mega-rocket in a \u201cwet dress rehearsal\u201d for an upcoming moon mission.<\/p>\n

All the components for the Artemis I mission have been cleared to roll out to the launch pad at the Kennedy Space Center in Cape Canaveral, Florida.<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s\u00a02022 mission to the moon is called Artemis 1.<\/p>\n

It will be testing out hardware so that NASA can land the first woman and the first person of color on the moon by 2025.<\/p>\n

That crewed mission is being referred to as Artemis 3 and a lot has to happen before it can take place.<\/p>\n

Before Artemis 1 can launch, NASA wants to test all the components.<\/p>\n

All the major pieces should all be at the launch pad on March 17.<\/p>\n

Artemis 1 isn\u2019t a crewed mission but it needs to loop around the moon to test three key components.<\/p>\n

These are NASA\u2019s Space Launch System (SLS), its Orion spacecraft, and the European Service Module (ESM).<\/p>\n

The Orion spacecraft and the ESM should get within 62 miles of the lunar surface and then travel 40,000 miles beyond this.<\/p>\n

It will take about three weeks for it to complete the mission and land in the Pacific Ocean off San Diego.<\/p>\n

All the components for NASA\u2019s Artemis I mission have been cleared to roll out to the launch pad.NASA All the major pieces should all be at the launch pad on March 17.NASA <\/p>\n

NASA also refers to the Orion spacecraft and SLS as the \u201cMega moon rocket\u201d<\/p>\n

It will take about 11 hours to take the mega-rocket from its storage location to the launch pad.<\/p>\n

Once there, NASA has said it will take about two weeks for experts to prepare for the \u201cwet dress rehearsal.\u201d<\/p>\n

This means propellant will be added to the rocket tanks and a full launch countdown will take place.<\/p>\n

That should take place in early April.<\/p>\n

NASA is trying to land the first woman and the first person of color on the moon by 2025.NASA\/Kim Shiflett <\/p>\n

However, NASA\u2019s SLS and Orion spacecraft won\u2019t be going anywhere.<\/p>\n

How the equipment behaves will determine when Artemis 1 can actually take off.<\/p>\n

Tom Whitmeyer, deputy associate administrator for exploration systems development at NASA,\u00a0previously said: \u201cThe agency is waiting to for the wet dress rehearsal to see how we\u2019re doing \u2026 we\u2019ll set the launch date at that point.<\/p>\n

\u201cWe continue to evaluate the May launch window, but we recognize that there\u2019s a lot of work in front of us and we need to make sure we get through that testing and evaluation activity before we set a launch commitment date.\u201d<\/p>\n

It will take about 11 hours to take the mega-rocket from its storage location to the launch pad.NASA\/Kim Shiflett <\/p>\n

If all goes to plan, all the equipment will be wheeled back into the Vehicle Assembly Building and a launch date will finally be set.<\/p>\n

This could be almost any time in May, June or early July.<\/p>\n

It all depends on weather conditions and where the moon is in its lunar cycle.<\/p>\n

Possible launch windows are May 7-21, June 6-16, June 29-July 1 and July 5-12 of this year.<\/p>\n
