<\/p>\n

China is working on a plan to locate the first Earth-like planet in deep space.<\/p>\n

Earlier this year, China spoke of its\u00a0space exploration ambitions, which include plans to build a base on the Moon and\u00a0collect samples from Mars.<\/p>\n

Now, it seems the nation also has hopes of uncovering a planet like our own Earth in distant solar systems, according to a new report by\u00a0Nature.<\/p>\n

The mission, appropriately designated \u2018Earth 2.0\u2019, will be funded by the Chinese Academy of Sciences.<\/p>\n

Chinese government-backed scientists will scour deep space for exoplanets orbiting a star like our Sun in the \u201chabitable zone\u201d.<\/p>\n

However, this \u2018Earth 2.0\u2019 would also need to have the right conditions to support liquid water, and potentially life.<\/p>\n

Sometime in May, astronomers are expected to release details about the mission, which is currently finalizing its early designs.<\/p>\n

Once the designs are complete, they need to pass a review by a panel of experts in June.<\/p>\n

It is around that time that the project team will receive funding to start building the satellite it will use for the mission.<\/p>\n

Comprising seven telescopes, this powerful satellite will search distant solar systems for four years.<\/p>\n

Six of the telescopes will focus heavily on the Cygnus\u2013Lyra constellations \u2013 the same part of space that Nasa\u2019s Kepler telescope observed for nearly a decade before it retired in 2018.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe Kepler field is a low-hanging fruit because we have very good data from there,\u201d Jian Ge, the astronomer leading the Earth 2.0 mission at the Shanghai Astronomical Observatory of the Chinese Academy of Sciences, told Nature.<\/p>\n

\u201cOur satellite can be 10\u201315 times more powerful than NASA\u2019s Kepler telescope in its sky-surveying capacity,\u201d Ge noted.<\/p>\n

The Chinese telescopes will work via the tried-and-true method of monitoring a star\u2019s brightness \u2013 when a large object passes in front of a star, it gets dimmer.<\/p>\n

These changes in brightness can indicate that an exoplanet may be present.<\/p>\n

The team, which consists of around 300 scientists and engineers, hopes to launch the spacecraft sometime before the end of 2026.<\/p>\n

In addition to this instrument, China is also developing another, powerful space telescope, called Xuntian or the\u00a0Chinese Space Station Telescope\u00a0(CSST).<\/p>\n

CSST is expected to possess a 6.6-foot diameter primary mirror, as well as a field of view 300 times larger than NASA\u2019s Hubble Space Telescope.<\/p>\n

A field of view that wide would allow the telescope to observe up to 40 percent of the sky over ten years.<\/p>\n
