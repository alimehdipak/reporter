You don\u2019t have to look like a Twit-wit any longer.<\/p>\n

Tired of having to delete painstakingly formulated Tweets due to minor errors? Not to fear: After almost a year of speculation \u2014 and myriad complaints from frazzled users \u2014 Twitter\u2019s long-rumored edit button has apparently become a reality. A popular Twitter code-breaker named Jane Manchun Wong recently confirmed the existence of the unreleased feature in a series of viral tweets. <\/p>\n

\u201cHow an edited tweet looks like on Twitter Web App,\u201d tweeted the Twitter wizard, who claims to have discovered the trial function by delving into the digital algorithms. <\/p>\n

According to her handy video tutorial, the Twitterati simply click on the drop-down menu next to the post, whereupon they\u2019ll be able to delete it or remove certain typos. However, users best not linger too long as they reportedly get 30 minutes after posting to employ the Twitter function, The Independent reported.<\/p>\n

\n

The editing feature also appears to adhere to journalistic standards in that, unlike other social media platforms, it reveals exactly how a Tweet has been changed. After a Tweet has been edited, the post will sport an \u201cedited\u201d update next to the timestamp as well as show both the original error and the correction, per Wong\u2019s explainer. Meanwhile, the old iteration of the tweet will bear a notification alerting users that \u201cthere\u2019s a new version of this Tweet.\u201d In other words, you can say goodbye to the notion of covering up embarrassing online gaffes. <\/p>\n

However, Jay Sullivan, the company\u2019s VP of consumer products, claims that there\u2019s a good reason for committing mistakes to digital memory. <\/p>\n

\u201cWithout things like time limits, controls, and transparency about what has been edited, Edit could be misused to alter the record of the public conversation,\u201d\u00a0he tweeted earlier this month. \u201cProtecting the integrity of that public conversation is our top priority when we approach this work.\u201d<\/p>\n

Despite its transparency, the interim edit button isn\u2019t without its downsides. Wong, who, contrary to popular Twitter rumors, is not affiliated with the company, slammed the feature for re-uploading \u201cmedia (images, videos, GIFs, etc) instead of reusing them.\u201d<\/p>\n

She dubbed the function \u201can inefficient use of the bandwidth and media processing power,\u201d noting that in her latest dry run that the trial version had inexplicably converted her video into an image. It appears that the editor will need some edits of its own before it gets the greenlight. <\/p>\n

The function, which has reportedly been in the works since 2021, will likely debut as a test function later this year, PC Mag reported. And while the feature will initially only be accessible to Twitter Blue subscribers, its availability could perhaps change with Elon Musk at the helm. <\/p>\n
