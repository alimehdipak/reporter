<\/p>\n

If you were somebody, Ron Galella pointed his lens at you.<\/p>\n

The gutsy, fearless, in-your-face paparazzo who went to extreme lengths to snap photos of the likes of Jacqueline Kennedy Onassis, who took him to court, and Marlon Brando, who famously slugged him in the kisser, has died. He was 91.<\/p>\n

The famed photographer died peacefully in his sleep in his home in Montville, New Jersey, on Saturday, his reps told the Hollywood Reporter.<\/p>\n

Known as the \u201cGodfather of the US paparazzi culture\u201d and \u201cPaparazzo Extraordinaire,\u201d the shutterbug shot more A-listers than perhaps any photographer in America during his six-decade career in photojournalism: John Lennon, Mick Jagger, Elvis Presley, Princess Diana, Paul McCartney, Bob Dylan, David Bowie, Michael Jackson, Andy Warhol and so many more.<\/p>\n

Though iconic, some of the Bronx-born fotog\u2019s shots landed him in trouble \u2014 mostly because his subjects were unaware he was shooting them.<\/p>\n

His work methods, regarded as unethical by some or genius by others, ultimately produced some of the most highly regarded iconographies \u2014 a testament to his keen eye \u2014 evident in \u201cWindblown Jackie,\u201d which infuriated the First Lady but delighted Galella.<\/p>\n

\u201cThis\u00a0decisive moment\u00a0photo, which I titled,\u00a0\u2018Windblown Jackie,\u2019 is my favorite, most published picture and the best-selling print of all time at my fine art galleries worldwide,\u201d he wrote in 2021. \u201cIt\u2019s a superior picture, like DaVinci\u2019s most famous painting, the\u00a0Mona Lisa. It embodies all the qualities of my paparazzi approach: exclusive, unrehearsed, off-guard, spontaneous, no appointments \u2014 the only game.\u201d<\/p>\n

Galella has been called the \u201cGodfather of the US paparazzi culture\u201d and \u201cPaparazzo Extraordinaire.\u201dCindy Ord Jackie Kennedy Onassis on Madison Avenue in New York City on Oct. 7, 1971.Ron Galella\/WireImage.com Ron Galella wore a helmet in 1974 while photographing Marlon Brando.Ron Galella\/WireImage.com <\/p>\n

While memorable, Galella\u2019s Jackie O portraits, which he obtained during a yearslong pursuit throughout New York City, landed him in a 1972 free-speech trial. Onassis said that he made her life \u201cintolerable, almost unlivable, with his constant surveillance\u201d and the paparazzo was slapped with a restraining order.<\/p>\n

\u201cJackie was my favorite subject,\u201d he told The Post about the 1979 shot. \u201cI had to keep 25 feet from her but in the museum I did break it.\u201d<\/p>\n

After he was caught breaking the restraining order four separate times, Galella was fined and ordered not to photograph Jackie or her children. His first book, \u201cJacqueline\u201d (1974), sold more than 10,000 copies.<\/p>\n

\u201cTo this day I cannot shoot Caroline,\u201d he told The Post in 2019. \u201cActually I could, but it\u2019s a risk. The injunction still is in effect.\u201d<\/p>\n

He prided himself on getting \u201cunrehearsed, spontaneous images of real moments,\u201d many of which included the middle finger from A-listers angry he caught them in the flesh.<\/p>\n

And he sure saw anger personified. Galella, who studied photojournalism at the Art Center College of Design in Pasadena, California, famously was sucker-punched by Brando as he trailed him in Chinatown. While it ultimately lost him five teeth, he got a $40,000 settlement out of it \u2014 not to mention incredible stills.<\/p>\n

It wasn\u2019t the only time Galella was subjected to violence over his methods either. Richard Burton\u2019s bodyguard also knocked out one of Gal\u00adella\u2019s teeth \u2014 though he lost a suit over that and was jailed in Cuernavaca, Mexico \u2014 while Elvis Presley\u2019s security slashed Ron\u2019s tires.<\/p>\n

Jerry Hall and Mick Jagger at Limelight in 1984, shot by Galella.WireImage Galella\u2019s career spanned over six decades, shooting every famous face imaginable.WireImage Robert Redford, shot by Galella.Ron Galella\/Wireimage.com <\/p>\n

Galella, who was a photographer in the Air Force during the Korean War, did have some fans, though, including Andy Warhol, who called Galella his \u201cfavorite photographer.\u201d We had the \u201csame social disease,\u201d Ron said of their celebrity obsessions.<\/p>\n

With countless photos, Galella and his wife, Betty Burke Galella, started the Ron Galella Ltd corporation in 1992 to safeguard his extensive photo library, seen in the 2010 documentary \u201cSmash His Camera.\u201d Galella also published several books with his work, including\u00a0\u201cDisco Years\u201d with Grace Jones\u2019 fierce face covering the photo art book.<\/p>\n

All of his photos weren\u2019t snapped through controversial methods, though. Galella told The Post that Cher was \u201cso nice\u201d when he was asked to get a photo of her with her new baby in 1976.<\/p>\n
