Gwen Stefani\u2019s appearance in Sean Paul\u2019s new \u201cLight My Fire\u201d music video has landed her in hot water.<\/p>\n

In the clip, the No Doubt frontwoman wears dreadlocks and a dress in the colors of the Jamaican flag, which swiftly sparked accusations of cultural appropriation on social media.<\/p>\n

\u201cno one can appropriate a culture the way gwen stefani does,\u201d one person tweeted, sharing images of Stefani sporting cornrows, henna and more in past videos.<\/p>\n

\u201cAhhhh Gwen Stefani went back to her Jamaican roots. Nature is really healing,\u201d another critic joked.<\/p>\n

Some folks called out her traditionally black hairstyle specifically, with one tweeting, \u201cGwen Stefani even has ~dread~ like twists in that video. I am proper screaming. She has seen all the tweets saying her cultural appropriation era is missed and she said BET.\u201d<\/p>\n

Another quipped, \u201cAs a south Asian who grew up in the 90s, I feel her current appropriation is cheating on us. How could you, Gwen? I thought that bindi was forever.\u201d<\/p>\n

The ska singer often wore a bindi when No Doubt shot to fame in the \u201990s. Getty Images <\/p>\n

The singer\u2019s fans fought back in the comments, with one writing, \u201cShe doesn\u2019t appropriate it. She appreciates it and she does it respectfully. She started in a ska band which is a type of reggae. She has always shown love to race and culture. Some people just do it for the profit. She\u2019s doing it cuz she loves it.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cThe Voice\u201d judge appears in the music video alongside Sean Paul and Jamaican dancehall singer Shenseea.Sean Paul\/YouTube <\/p>\n

This isn\u2019t the ska singer\u2019s first cultural appropriation callout. When No Doubt first came to fame in the \u201990s, she was dating Indian bandmate Tony Kanal, and often wore a bindi.<\/p>\n

And in the early aughts, the \u201cDon\u2019t Speak\u201d songstress performed with a group of Japanese dancers called the \u201cHarajuku Girls\u201d while promoting her first solo album, \u201cLove Angel Music Baby.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cThe Sweet Escape\u201d singer responded to cultural appropriation allegations in a Billboard interview. AFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

In 2019, the \u201cHollaback Girl\u201d hitmaker responded to the \u201cHarajuku Girls\u201d-era backlash in a Billboard interview.<\/p>\n

\u201cI get a little defensive when people [call it culture appropriation], because if we didn\u2019t allow each other to share our cultures, what would we be?\u201d she asked. \u201cYou take pride in your culture and have traditions, and then you share them for new things to be created.\u201d<\/p>\n

Page Six Style has reached out to Stefani\u2019s representative for comment.<\/p>\n
