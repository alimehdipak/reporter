Amber Heard and four other high-profile women received more than 8,000 abusive messages on Instagram, but the company failed to adequately crack down on the online hate even after the nasty content was flagged, according to a study.<\/p>\n

The Center for Countering Digital Hate, a nonprofit that \u201cseeks to disrupt the architecture of online hate and misinformation,\u201d conducted a review of what it calls an \u201cepidemic of misogynist abuse.\u201d<\/p>\n

The study found that Instagram \u201cignored\u201d 90% of the harmful messages that were sent to the women through the direct message feature on the app.<\/p>\n

The other women who took part in the study alongside Heard were British television host Rachel Riley; Jamie Klingler, a London-based journalist; Bryony Gordon, a journalist and mental health advocate; and Sharan Dhaliwal, founder of a UK-based South Asian culture magazine.<\/p>\n

All of the women have substantial followings on Instagram. Heard, the actress who starred in \u201cAquaman\u201d and \u201cJustice League,\u201d has more than 4 million Instagram followers.<\/p>\n

Heard, who has 4 million Instagram followers, has starred in hit films like \u201cAquaman\u201d and \u201cJustice League.\u201dSamir Hussein\/WireImage <\/p>\n

\u201cInstagram claim that they act on hate speech, including misogyny, homophobia, and racism; nudity or sexual activity; graphic violence; and threats of violence,\u201d the CCDH said.<\/p>\n

\u201cBut our research finds that Instagram systematically fails to enforce appropriate sanctions and remove those who break its rules.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

The group added: \u201cHarassment, violent threats, image-based sexual abuse can be sent by strangers, at any time and in large volumes, directly into your DMs without consent and platforms do nothing to stop it.\u201d<\/p>\n

A spokesperson for Instagram\u2019s parent company Meta disputed the findings of the study.dpa\/picture alliance via Getty I <\/p>\n

But a source with knowledge of Instagram\u2019s inner workings disputed the report\u2019s findings, including claims that \u201cInstagram allows strangers to place voice calls to women they don\u2019t know.\u201d<\/p>\n

A company source told The Post that it has implemented guardrails to protect women, including a \u201chidden words\u201d feature that filters direct messages and comments that contain a list of \u201chateful\u201d terms and phrases.<\/p>\n

A spokesperson for Instagram\u2019s parent company Meta told The Post: \u201cWhile we disagree with many of the CCDH\u2019s conclusions, we do agree that the harassment of women is unacceptable.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cThat\u2019s why we don\u2019t allow gender-based hate or any threat of sexual violence, and last year we announced stronger protections for female public figures.\u201d <\/p>\n

Meta Platforms Inc., the parent company of Facebook and Instagram, has been accused of turning a blind eye to the harmful effects of its apps.Bloomberg via Getty Images <\/p>\n

The spokesperson added: \u201cMessages from people you don\u2019t follow go to a separate request inbox where you can either block or report the sender, or you can turn off message requests altogether.\u201d <\/p>\n

\u201cCalls from people you don\u2019t know only go through if you accept their message request and we offer a way to filter abusive messages so you never have to see them.\u201d <\/p>\n

Meta\u2019s subsidiaries Facebook and Instagram have been accused of failing to mitigate the harmful effects of their social media platforms on young girls.<\/p>\n
