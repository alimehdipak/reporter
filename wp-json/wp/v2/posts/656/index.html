NEW YORK \u2014 Veteran daytime talk show host Maury Povich is retiring, with the last original episodes of \u201cMaury\u201d set for broadcast in September after 31 years on the air.<\/p>\n

NBC Universal confirmed the impending exit of the 83-year-old Povich on Monday. Old episodes are likely to live on in some form, however, with the company saying that \u201cthe show has thousands of lively, entertaining and dramatic episodes that will continue to be successful in daytime for years to come.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cI\u2019m so proud of my relationship with NBC Universal and all those who worked on the \u2018Maury\u2019 show but as I occasionally tell my guests on \u2018Maury,\u2019 enough already,\u201d he said.<\/p>\n

His show hit upon\u00a0a successful formula\u00a0many years ago and has stuck with it: using tests to reveal, on the air, the father of babies where paternity is in question or lie detectors to see if a spouse is cheating or not.<\/p>\n

It wasn\u2019t highbrow entertainment, but was popular enough for Povich to build a devoted audience among viewers young enough to be his grandchildren.<\/p>\n

\u2018Maury\u2019 began as \u2018The Maury Povich Show\u2019 in 1991.NBCU Photo Bank via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Povich was a television journalist before going into the talk show world, beginning in Washington, where his father, Shirley, was a well-known sportswriter. He anchored local news shows in Chicago, Los Angeles, San Francisco and Philadelphia. For a while in Los Angeles, his co-anchor was journalist Connie Chung, whom he married in 1984.<\/p>\n

In 1986, he was the inaugural anchor of the entertainment news program, \u201cA Current Affair,\u201d and began \u201cThe Maury Povich Show\u201d for Paramount in 1991. It switched to NBC Universal in 1998, shortening the show\u2019s name.<\/p>\n

\u201cMaury and I decided two years ago that this season would be the farewell season for the show, and while his retirement is bittersweet, we are so happy for him to be able to spend more time on the golf course,\u201d said Tracie Wilson, executive vice president, NBC Universal Syndication Studios.<\/p>\n

It was not immediately clear whether NBC Universal would try to start a similar-themed show or simply ride Povich\u2019s reruns: \u201cJudge Judy\u201d is still a popular daytime show even though it no longer produces original episodes.<\/p>\n
