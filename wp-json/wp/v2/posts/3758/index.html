In the country that devours the most pornography on the planet, \u201cDeep Throat\u201d still got the shaft as it looked to return to theaters for its 50th anniversary this year.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Banned, protested and subjected to numerous obscenity trials after its 1972 debut, this week\u2019s worldwide premiere for the 4K restoration of \u201cthe Golden Age of Porn\u2019s\u201d magnum opus initially had a hard time booking US theaters.<\/p>\n

\u201cEurope is much more receptive to us. We couldn\u2019t find a venue that was comfortable showing the film, and we even had a couple cancel on us at the last minute. It was bulls\u2013t,\u201d Gerard Damiano Jr., son of writer and director Gerard Sr., told The Post.\u00a0<\/p>\n

It was history repeating itself for Robin Leonardi, daughter of porn star and industry activist Gloria Leonard: \u201cFifty years later, we\u2019re still<\/em> having this conversation about free speech and censorship \u2014 the very same issues that our parents fought for.\u201d<\/p>\n

The 4K \u201cDeep Throat\u201d will have its world premiere at The Slipper Room on the Lower East Side on Sunday, June 12, followed by additional dates in the US.Bettmann Archive <\/p>\n

The film stars Linda Lovelace as a sexually unfulfilled woman, who only wants \u201cbombs going off\u201d in bed. She visits an eccentric therapist played by Harry Reems, who discovers that her clitoris is actually in her throat.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201c\u2018Deep Throat\u2019 is very vanilla, it\u2019s wholesome, almost, and naive compared to the deviant things you can find with two clicks on an iPhone under the covers at night,\u201d said Leonard, who eventually found theaters open to showing it.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cDeep Throat\u201d has reportedly made more than $600 million since its 1972 debut. The Roxy Cinema in Tribeca will host a 16mm screening and sneak peek of the restoration on Friday, June 10. The 4K \u201cDeep Throat\u201d will have its world premiere at The Slipper Room on the Lower East Side on Sunday, June 12, with burlesque dancers, a Q&A and three shows, including a midnight screening that \u201charkens back to the grindhouse days of 42nd Street,\u201d Damiano said. Additional dates in the US and Europe will be held in the fall.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cFor the past 22 years, it has been our intention to grapple with the uptight, puritan nature of the American scene and allow artists a place where they can feel free to express their passions in whatever fashion best suits them,\u201d Slipper Room Artistic Director James Habacker told The Post. \u201cAs such, we are proud to play a part in presenting to a new generation \u2018Deep Throat,\u2019 a film that did so much to push the boundaries of American taste towards a more open and free expression of our sexual natures.\u201d<\/p>\n<\/p>\n

An \u2018obscene\u2019 hit<\/h2>\n

Despite \u201cDeep Throat\u201d debuting in the middle of a revolutionary and tumultuous time in US history, the country\u2019s mindset remained very puritanical, especially in entertainment.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cAlthough you might have just been at a love-in in San Francisco, when you turned on the TV, Lucy and Ricky are still sleeping in separate beds. You couldn\u2019t even show a couple in the same bed together,\u201d Damiano recalled. But then came \u201cDeep Throat,\u201d and \u201csuddenly media was beginning to catch up with people\u2019s realities. You had hardcore sex in your life, so now you can see it in a movie.\u201d<\/p>\n

And see it America did, with celebs like Jackie Kennedy Onassis, Truman Capote and Frank Sinatra joining the \u201craincoat crowd\u201d lining up to see the \u201cporno chic\u201d sensation made for just $25,000 over six days.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Protesters outside the Frisco theater in Times Square on May 31, 1980. AP Photo\/David Pickoff <\/p>\n

The Post\u2019s film critic Archer Winsten, who wrote he was \u201cforced\u201d by \u201cpublic curiosity\u201d to see the film five months after it opened, opined that he was \u201cfrankly surprised at the people who watch it with an attention so rapt you\u2019d think they were face to face with hooded cobras about to strike.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cDeep Throat\u201d was equally difficult for others to swallow, and conservative and feminist protests ensued, as did high-profile local and federal court cases.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Manhattan Judge Joel Tyler ruled that \u201cDeep Throat\u201d was obscene in 1973 and fined the company that owned the 49th Street theater where it still drew crowds since premiering there seven months earlier. Tyler, however, repeatedly admitted he was \u201clearning something\u201d over the course of the three-month trial, The Post reported in December 1972.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Manhattan Judge Joel Tyler repeatedly said he was \u201clearning something\u201d during the three-month obscenity trial, The Post reported in December 1972.\u00a0New York Post <\/p>\n

Lovelace (aka Boreman) later joined the anti-porn movement and renounced her adult roles, saying she was coerced into making them by her violent first husband, Chuck Traynor. In her 1980 memoir, \u201cOrdeal,\u201d she details his alleged abuse, which included beatings, spying, death threats and a gang rape. <\/p>\n

\u201cEveryone that watches \u2018Deep Throat\u2019 is watching me being raped,\u201d she wrote. <\/p>\n

Traynor later admitted to hitting Lovelace and while several costars and crew backed up her claims of his domestic violence and control, many cast doubt on her claims of being coerced into making adult films.<\/p>\n

\u201cYes, she had an abusive husband, but she wasn\u2019t forced into anything. She was really into what she was doing,\u201d two-time costar Reems told The Post in 2005.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Despite its controversies, \u201cDeep Throat\u201d reportedly earned more than $600 million, making it one of the most profitable movies of all time.<\/p>\n

Its place in history was further solidified when the Washington Post adopted its title as the nickname for Mark Felt, who decades later would be identified as the Watergate informant whose tips led to the 1974 resignation of President Richard Nixon.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Ironically, Nixon\u2019s own disgraced Vice President Spiro Agnew saw \u201cDeep Throat\u201d in theaters, according to Entertainment Weekly, but that didn\u2019t stop the FBI under the administration \u2014 with Felt as the bureau\u2019s second-in-command \u2014 from trying to choke the film\u2019s release.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cMy father fought against Nixon as his nemesis. Nixon vowed to bring him down,\u201d Damiano Jr. said. \u201cMy father was always very proud to say that Nixon tried to take down \u2018Deep Throat,\u2019 but in the end, it was Deep Throat that took down Nixon.\u201d<\/p>\n

The front page of The Post on Aug. 8, 1974. <\/p>\n

Growing up with \u2018Deep Throat\u2019<\/h2>\n

Gerard Damiano\u2019s children, Gerard Jr. and Christar, were 7 and 8 when \u201cDeep Throat\u201d debuted, and both fondly recall the time they spent on sets with him, where the cast and crew were \u201cfamily.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cHe never kept it a secret from us, and certainly, we were never exposed to hardcore pornography or force-fed sex at a young age,\u201d Gerard Jr. said, with Christar adding, \u201cWe were taught that sex was a beautiful thing, the human body was a work of art, and it was something you shouldn\u2019t be ashamed of.\u201d<\/p>\n

That lesson is front-and-center in \u201cDeep Throat,\u201d where \u201cthe woman was the star,\u201d Christar said. \u201cThat\u2019s why we think that it became such an overnight success because all of a sudden there\u2019s a new language about the woman and what she desires.\u201d<\/p>\n

The elder Domanio did not see any profits from the film and \u201cwas \u201clucky to get out with his life,\u201d his son said. <\/p>\n
