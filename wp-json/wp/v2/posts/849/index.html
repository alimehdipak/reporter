<\/p>\n

NASA has finally opened a moon sample after keeping it sealed for 50 years.<\/p>\n

It was one of the last unopened Apollo-era lunar\u00a0samples and it was collected during the Apollo 17 mission in 1972.<\/p>\n

One of the main reasons\u00a0NASA\u00a0kept the sample closed for so long is that the space agency was hoping for technology to evolve.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s now almost 50 years on and we do have the technology to study the moon sample in great detail.<\/p>\n

The sample was opened at Johnson Space Center\u00a0in Houston earlier this week.<\/p>\n

Thomas Zurbuchen, associate administrator of NASA\u2019s Science Mission Directorate in Washington, said: \u201cWe have had an opportunity to open up this incredibly precious sample that\u2019s been saved for 50 years under vacuum and we finally get to see what treasures are held within.\u201d<\/p>\n

NASA just revealed close up clips of the sample on\u00a0a livestream.<\/p>\n

The mysterious sample was collected during the US\u00a0space\u00a0agency\u2019s last manned mission to the moon.<\/p>\n

The sample from the Apollo 17 mission will be exactly 50 years old on December 13 this year.<\/p>\n

The lunar sample was carefully opened in a controlled environment.NASA\/James Blair\/Cover Images\/IN <\/p>\n

Astronauts Eugene Cernan and Harrison \u201cJack\u201d Schmitt collected the moon sample by hammering a 14-inch tube into the lunar surface.<\/p>\n

They also collected another sample that wasn\u2019t sealed.<\/p>\n

Both tubes were filled up with moon rock and dust.<\/p>\n

The two samples were brought back to Earth and the one that wasn\u2019t vacuum sealed was opened in 2019.<\/p>\n

The vacuum-sealed tube is more exciting because it may contain substances called \u2018volatiles\u2019.<\/p>\n

Volatiles are gases that evaporate at normal temperatures.<\/p>\n

They would have escaped from the unsealed moon sample tube but they may be present in the vacuum-sealed one.<\/p>\n

This handout photo released by NASA shows, from left, Dr. Juliane Gross, Astromaterials Research and Exploration Science Division (ARES) deputy Apollo curator, alongside Drs. Alex Meshik, and Olga Pravdivtseva, from Washington University in St. Louis, beginning a gas extraction process of the Apollo-era lunar samples on February 11, 2022, at NASA\u2019s Johnson Space Center in Houston.NASA\/AFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

That\u2019s why gases were\u00a0carefully extracted\u00a0from the sample a few weeks ago by piercing its tube.<\/p>\n

Ryan Zeigler, Apollo sample curator said: \u201cWe have extracted gas out of this core, and we hope that will help scientists when they\u2019re trying to understand the lunar gas signature by looking at the different aliquots [samples taken for chemical analysis].\u201d<\/p>\n

It\u2019s hoped the lunar sample will give researchers a better understanding of the lunar surface before the next manned mission to the moon in a few years.<\/p>\n

If you were wondering what the sample smells like, two NASA scientists revealed on today\u2019s livestream that moon dust apparently \u201csmells like gunpowder\u201d.<\/p>\n
