Giant ice volcanos were active \u2018relatively recently\u2019 on Pluto, altering its surface, scientists say.<\/p>\n

Images taken of Pluto reveal a region of strange terrain previously unobserved in our solar system, according to a new\u00a0study\u00a0published in the journal Nature Communications.<\/p>\n

Snapped by Nasa\u2019s New Horizons spacecraft in a 2015 flyby, the photos suggest that the bizarre, lumpy surfaces were caused by the dwarf planet\u2019s ice volcanos, or \u201ccryovolcanos\u201d.<\/p>\n

\u201cHere we analyze the geomorphology and composition of the features and conclude the region was resurfaced by cryovolcanic processes, of a type and scale so far unique to Pluto,\u201d the study noted.<\/p>\n

Cyrovolanic material on Pluto consists of a \u201cthicker, slushy icy-water mix or even possibly a solid flow like glaciers,\u201d according to study-author and planetary scientist at Colorado\u2019s Southwest Research Institute Kelsi Singer.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s unclear when Pluto\u2019s cryovolcanos last erupted exactly, but the study\u2019s authors propose that \u201ca large volume of material has erupted from multiple sources, and likely in more than one episode over time.\u201d<\/p>\n

Singer also noted that the research showed no evidence of an impact crater in the area, meaning it may still be in the process of forming today.<\/p>\n

Cryovolcanic ejections on Pluto hint that the planet may have retained its internal heat longer than scientists previously thought.<\/p>\n

\u201c[The findings] suggest that a small body like Pluto, which should have lost much of its internal heat long ago, was able to hold onto enough energy to facilitate widespread geological activity rather late in its history,\u201d Lynnae Quick, a planetary scientist at NASA\u2019s Goddard Space Flight Center specializing in ice volcanoes told AFP.<\/p>\n

While there are several cryovolcanos in our solar system \u2013 most notably on Ceres\u00a0and\u00a0Saturn\u2019s moon Titan \u2013 Pluto\u2019s \u201clook so different from anything else we ever have seen\u201d, Singer said.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe features on Pluto are the only vast field of very large icy volcanoes and they have a unique texture of undulating terrain.\u201d<\/p>\n

Pluto\u2019s surface-altering volcanos include its largest, Wright Mons, which is about the size of Mauna Loa in Hawaii (or around 13,679 feet tall).<\/p>\n

Cryovolcanic activity formed strange terrain on Pluto.NASA\/Johns Hopkins University A <\/p>\n

Singer said that it\u2019s difficult to identify exactly when the ice volcanoes were formed \u201cbut we believe they could be as young as a few hundred million years or even younger\u201d.<\/p>\n

Experts have called the findings extremely significant and think they will further our understanding of Pluto, which the study describes as one of the most unique visited places in our solar system.<\/p>\n

\u201cThese findings will cause us to re-evaluate the possibilities for the maintenance of liquid water on small, icy worlds that are far from the Sun,\u201d Quick said.<\/p>\n

\u201cI love the idea that we have so much left to learn about the solar system,\u201d Singer echoed.<\/p>\n

\u201cEvery time we go somewhere new, we find new things that we didn\u2019t predict\u2014like giant, recently-formed ice volcanoes on Pluto.\u201d<\/p>\n
