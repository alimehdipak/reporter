The search for signs of life on Mars is heating up.<\/p>\n

In research published last week, scientists claim to have found new evidence of underground volcanic activity on the planet.<\/p>\n

They say this movement of hot magma is triggering marsquakes, which are similar to earthquakes, in a specific region of the Red Planet.<\/p>\n

If true, the finding would lend credence to theories that life may exist in underground caverns on Mars.<\/p>\n

Warm magma beneath the surface and groundwater thought to be present there could provide a habitat for alien microbes.<\/p>\n

Researchers at the Australian National University made their finding after combing through data from Nasa\u2019s InSight Mars lander.<\/p>\n

The car-sized robot has resided on the planet since 2018 and, among other things, takes readings of marsquakes.<\/p>\n

Little is understood about the subterranean vibrations and scientists hope to use InSight data to get a better idea of Mars\u2019 interior.<\/p>\n

The new research, published in\u00a0Nature Communications, uncovers 47 previously undetected marsquakes beneath the Martian crust.<\/p>\n

Artist rendition of the formation of rocky bodies in the solar system, how they form and differentiate and evolve into terrestrial planets.Getty Images\/Stocktrek Images <\/p>\n

They were discovered in Cerberus Fossae \u2013 a seismically active region on Mars that\u00a0is less than 20million years old.<\/p>\n

The study authors speculate that volcanic activity in the inner layers of the planet is the cause of the newly detected quakes.<\/p>\n

If true, the findings suggest magma in the Martian mantle is still active.<\/p>\n

Previously, scientists have argued that \u2013 similar to those on Earth \u2013 the quakes are the result of powerful Martian tectonic forces.<\/p>\n

The repetitive nature of these quakes and the fact they were all detected in the same area suggest that Mars is more seismically active than scientists previously thought.<\/p>\n

\u201cWe found that these marsquakes repeatedly occurred at all times of the Martian day,\u201d said ANU scientist and study co-author Professor Hrvoje Tkal\u010di\u0107.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhereas marsquakes detected and reported by NASA in the past appeared to have occurred only during the dead of night when the planet is quieter.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cTherefore, we can assume that the movement of molten rock in the Martian mantle is the trigger for these 47 newly detected marsquakes beneath the Cerberus Fossae region.\u201d<\/p>\n

He added that the conclusion is that Mars is still volcanicly active, contrary to the belief of some scientists.<\/p>\n

Illustration of the structure of the planet Earth.Getty Images\/iStockphoto <\/p>\n

\u201cKnowing that the Martian mantle is still active is crucial to our understanding of how Mars evolved as a planet,\u201d he said.<\/p>\n

\u201cIt can help us answer fundamental questions about the solar system and the state of Mars\u2019 core, mantle and the evolution of its currently-lacking magnetic field.\u201d<\/p>\n

Scientists have long known that, billions of years ago, Mars had active volcanoes and vast intertwining rivers and lakes.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s part of the reason many believe that microscopic life may have once lived on the once-fertile grounds of Earth\u2019s mysterious neighbor.<\/p>\n

Rapid changes to the planet\u2019s atmosphere led its surface to become the barren, dusty wasteland that it is today.<\/p>\n

Some experts believe, however, that the old girl isn\u2019t out of action just yet.<\/p>\n

They have bickered for decades over whether Martian volcanoes are still active or just as dead as the rest of the planet.<\/p>\n

If there is indeed magma active under the planet\u2019s surface, the chances of life growing there are far higher.<\/p>\n

That\u2019s because microbes need a mix of heat, water and nutrients to survive. Moving, hot magma would provide one of those key ingredients.<\/p>\n
