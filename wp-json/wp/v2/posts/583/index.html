<\/p>\n

NASA has added a new satellite dish to its family of giant space instruments.<\/p>\n

The powerful new antenna will help engineers and scientists on Earth communicate with spacecraft in deep space, NASA said in a\u00a0statement\u00a0on Wednesday.<\/p>\n

Dubbed Deep Space Station 53, or DSS-53, the 111-foot antenna was developed as part of NASA\u2019s\u00a0Deep Space Network\u00a0(DSN).<\/p>\n

DSS-53 became the 14th addition to DSN and the fourth among six new\u00a0beam waveguide antennas\u00a0that the agency is adding to the network.<\/p>\n

The instrument is operating from NASA\u2019s ground facility just outside of Madrid \u2013 the space agency has three of these around the world.<\/p>\n

Spain\u2019s\u00a0Instituto Nacional de T\u00e9cnica Aeroespacial\u00a0(National Institute of Aerospace Technology) is managing the instrument on NASA\u2019s behalf.<\/p>\n

King Felipe VI of Spain made an appearance at the antenna\u2019s inauguration ceremony on March 16.<\/p>\n

The monarch was joined by NASA officials and dignitaries from both Spain and the US.<\/p>\n

The 111-foot beam waveguide antenna that went online in February 2022 at the Madrid ground station of NASA\u2019s Deep Space Network (DSN). NASA\/JPL-Caltech <\/p>\n

\u201cNASA is honored and humbled to have the king acknowledge this important milestone by joining us at the Madrid station,\u201d said Badri A. Younes, deputy associate administrator for Space Communications and Navigation (SCaN) at NASA\u2019s Headquarters in Washington.<\/p>\n

\u201cHis inauguration of the Deep Space Station 53 antenna highlights the critical and historical collaboration between the Kingdom of Spain and the United States that, through the Deep Space Network, will continue to enable humankind\u2019s exploration of the heavens for many years to come,\u201d Younes continued.<\/p>\n

The DSN is managed by NASA\u2019s Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL) in Southern California.<\/p>\n

It allows up to forty missions to \u201ctrack, send commands to, and receive scientific data from faraway spacecraft,\u201d NASA officials said.<\/p>\n

The US space agency added that they are developing more antennas like DSS-53 to add to their ground stations in Canberra, Australia, and the Goldstone\u00a0facility near Barstow, California.<\/p>\n

DSS-53, specifically, \u201cadds about 8% more capacity to the network,\u201d according to JPL\u2019s Bradford Arnold, who is also the DSN project manager.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhat it\u2019s going to mean is 8% more science, and that\u2019s really significant,\u201d Arnold added.<\/p>\n

Construction of the space instrument, which features some 300 reflection panels, began in 2016.<\/p>\n
