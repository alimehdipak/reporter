A multibillion-dollar tech startup whose founder made waves earlier this year when he compared Silicon Valley to a \u201cboys\u2019 club\u201d full of \u201cmob bosses\u201d has since seen its valuation in the secondary market crumble, The Post has learned.\u00a0<\/p>\n

San Francisco-based Bolt, which makes checkout payments technology and competes with the likes of PayPal, Shopify and Square, was valued at $11 billion in January \u2014 the same month that founder 27-year-old Ryan Breslow attacked venture capital giants Sequoia and Y Combinator, as well as payments company Stripe, for allegedly conspiring to sink his company.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Bolt tried to raise more cash at an even-higher $14 billion valuation just weeks later \u2014 but the company\u2019s valuation on the secondary market has fallen about 50% from its $11 billion peak in the months since Breslow\u2019s comments, private market sources told The Post.\u00a0<\/p>\n

One investor who bought into Bolt during a previous round is looking to sell off a large stake at a valuation of just $8 billion \u2014 but buyers are only willing to buy at maximum valuation of $6 billion as of last week, a private market source said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Another private market source working to move Bolt shares told The Post in April that the company\u2019s real valuation is just $5.5 billion. \u00a0<\/p>\n

However, a source close to Bolt claimed that the company is receiving term sheets \u2014 non-binding agreements that come before legally binding contracts \u2014 from unnamed investors that value the company at $17 billion to $20 billion. <\/p>\n

Bolt declined to comment. <\/p>\n

Sources said would-be backers\u2019 confidence in Bolt has been eroded partially by concerns about its leadership. <\/p>\n

Breslow stepped down as CEO just days after his attacks on Sequoia, Y Combinator and Stripe, which is a privately held payments company backed by both venture capital firms. He now serves as executive chairman. <\/p>\n

\u201cThere is too much management turnover,\u201d a private markets source said. <\/p>\n

A broader shakeout in the payments industry is also making investors jittery. Fast, another payments startup and Bolt rival that was backed by Stripe, suddenly shut down last week after failing to secure funding.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Breslow taunted Stripe over the Fast\u2019s failure on Twitter Friday, posting a meme and writing, \u201cStripe didn\u2019t put $100m into fast, they donated $100m to bolt marketing. Best thing that could happen.\u201d <\/p>\n

Larger publicly traded payments companies have likewise seen their stocks tank this year after soaring in 2020 and 2021. <\/p>\n

Shopify shares soared as high as $1,750 in November 2021 before collapsing more than 50% to about $644 on Friday. PayPal shares, likewise, soared above $300 last summer before falling to about $113. <\/p>\n

And shares of Jack Dorsey\u2019s Square parent company Block traded above $275 in August but are now hovering around $125.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Ken Smythe of private capital advisory Next Round Capital blamed the dramatic rise and fall in Bolt\u2019s valuation on investor hubris.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cWhen Bolt did their last round at an $11 billion valuation, then stepped it up to $14 billion just as the NASDAQ 100 peaked, you could say things were getting extremely frothy,\u201d Smythe told The Post. \u201cIf you\u2019re an investor writing Bolt a check at a 183x revenue multiple, then you\u2019re just asking for the trouble.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n
