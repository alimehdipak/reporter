<\/p>\n

Uber said it plans to start listing all New York City taxis through its app \u2014 a surprise shift that is expected to throw a lifeline the city\u2019s struggling yellow cabs as Uber scrambles to address its own driver shortage.<\/p>\n

A limited number of New Yorkers will be able to start hailing yellow cabs through the Uber app starting this spring before the feature rolls out fully over the summer, taxi technology provider and Uber partner Creative Mobile Technologies said in a Thursday statement.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Customers will pay roughly the same amount for taxi rides as they would for Uber X rides, Uber spokesperson Conor Ferguson confirmed to The Post.\u00a0<\/p>\n

While Uber drivers in New York aren\u2019t able to see what a trip will pay before they decide whether to accept it, the city\u2019s roughly 14,000 cabbies will have that option. Cabbies will be paid on the same scale as Uber drivers when they accept rides through the app, according to Uber.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cUber has a long history of partnering with the taxi industry to provide drivers with more ways to earn and riders with another transportation option,\u201d Andrew Macdonald, Uber\u2019s senior vice president of mobility and business operations, said in a statement to The Post. <\/p>\n

Customers will pay roughly the same amount for taxi rides as they would for Uber X rides. Corbis via Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cOur partnerships with taxis look different around the world, and we\u2019re excited to team up with taxi software companies CMT and Curb, which will benefit taxi drivers and all New Yorkers,\u201d Macdonald added.<\/p>\n

Bruce Schaller, a former city transit staffer and taxi policy guru, told The Post that the deal is a \u201clandmark moment\u201d for both Uber and the cab industry and gave credit to Mayor Eric Adams\u2019 administration for arranging the deal.<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s a win-win,\u201d Schaller said, arguing that the deal would boost Uber by giving its users more options and help cabbies by giving them more work.<\/p>\n

\u201cI think it\u2019s a really good thing for the industry to sort of converge in this manner,\u201d Schaller added.<\/p>\n

Uber stock was up 3.4% early Friday shortly after The Wall Street Journal first reported the news. <\/p>\n

The partnership represents a dramatic shift after years of hostility between cab drivers and the ride-sharing industry.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The rise of Uber and Lyft wreaked havoc on the taxi industry. Getty Images <\/p>\n

The rise of Uber and Lyft in the 2010s contributed to the plummeting value of taxi medallions, wreaking havoc on the industry and leading to widespread cabbie protests.\u00a0Uber, on the other hand, argues that\u00a0predatory lenders are largely to blame\u00a0for the dire financial conditions faced by many cabbies.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Last fall, ex-Mayor Bill de Blasio\u2019s administration reached a deal to cap medallion loans at $170,000 after a two week hunger strike from desperate cab drivers.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Since a taxi medallion will now effectively allow its owner to be both an Uber and a cab driver, the struggling medallion market could see a boost, according to Augustine Tang, a cabbie and activist with the New York Taxi Workers Alliance.<\/p>\n

For example, a cabbie could pick up a street-hail passenger in Manhattan, drive them to Queens and then use the Uber app to find another job rather than schlepping all the way back to Manhattan without a passenger.<\/p>\n

But Tang added that he still has reservations about the Uber partnership given the taxi industry\u2019s previous\u00a0battles with the company \u2014 and the\u00a0New York Taxi Workers Alliance blasted the deal in a statement.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cIf Uber and Curb think they can slide in with a payment structure that\u2019s broken for Uber drivers and piece it together on the backs of yellow cab drivers, they\u2019re in for a sobering surprise,\u201d the union\u2019s executive director\u00a0Bhairavi Desai said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The deal comes as Uber faces a shortage of drivers and grapples with soaring gas prices. Getty Images <\/p>\n

Desai claimed that taxi drivers picking up passengers would earn an average of 15% less per trip.\u00a0<\/p>\n

On Uber\u2019s end, the company has\u00a0faced a driver shortage\u00a0since 2020 and is now\u00a0grappling with soaring gas prices\u00a0that have convinced some would-be drivers that hitting the street isn\u2019t worth it.\u00a0Adding taxis to the app could help appease\u00a0Uber\u00a0customers frustrated by a lack of available vehicles.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Taxis are already available through the Uber app in several cities including Barcelona and Hong Kong \u2014\u00a0and CEO Dara Khosrowshahi\u00a0told CNBC\u00a0in February that Uber wants to \u201cget every single taxi in the world onto our platform by 2025.\u201d<\/p>\n

Schaller said he had long expected consolidation between ride-hailing companies and cabbies, but that the pandemic\u2019s drastic effects on the industry\u00a0sped up the transition.\u00a0<\/p>\n

He said the move shows that\u00a0Uber is \u201cmaturing\u201d as it tries to finally reach profitability and said that Lyft has\u00a0\u201cgot to join the party\u201d by inking its own taxi deal in order to stay competitive.\u00a0<\/p>\n

A Lyft spokesperson told The Post that the company doesn\u2019t have similar plans to Uber\u2019s tie-up with cabs.<\/p>\n

Matt Daus, a transportation expert and former chairman of the city\u2019s Taxi and Limousine Commission, said that Uber working with cabbies could help insulate the company from\u00a0legal challenges over its classification of drivers as employees\u00a0rather than contractors.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cThe ability of cab drivers to reject rides makes them more like true independent drivers,\u201d Daus told The Post. \u201cThat\u2019s one of the biggest reasons why they\u2019re doing this\u2026 This is an argument that could be used in court.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Any money that Uber may lose through the New York cab partnership will be far outweighed by the money it will save on potential labor battles, Daus predicted.\u00a0<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
