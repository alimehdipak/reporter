Is O3 polar or nonpolar?<\/p>\n

O3 is a polar molecule and it is due to its bent molecular geometry. In O3, the electric dipole moments of the bonds don\u2019t counterbalance one another which results in a net dipole moment. Due to this, O3 (Ozone) is polar in nature.<\/p>\n

Why is O3 bent and not linear?<\/h3>\n

Based on the VSEPR (valance shell electron pair repulsion) theory, electrons will repel the electron cloud of the two oxygen atoms on each end. This will result in the end O groups being pushed down giving the O3 molecule a bent molecular geometry or V shape.<\/p>\n

Ozone has three electron groups around the central oxygen, so has the trigonal planar electron geometry. The molecular geometry is bent: The Effect of Lone Pairs (contd.) With four electron groups (tetrahedral electron geometry), there can be one or two lone pairs around the central atom.<\/p>\n
