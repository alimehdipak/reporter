<\/p>\n

Apple has revealed its 10 best \u2018Shot on iPhone\u2019 photos for 2022 \u2013 with a big twist.<\/p>\n

This year the challenge focused on close-quarters photos snapped with the iPhone\u2019s new Macro feature, and they put all of our Instagram pics to shame.<\/p>\n

The challenge was announced back in January, and was open to absolutely anyone with an\u00a0iPhone.<\/p>\n

Apple\u2019s only real catch was that contestants had to use the Macro camera feature, which is only available on the new\u00a0iPhone 13 Pro\u00a0and iPhone 13 Pro Max.<\/p>\n

Tens of thousands of photos were entered by snap-happy iPhone owners.<\/p>\n

And\u00a0Apple\u00a0has now revealed the 10 winners for its first Shot on iPhone Macro Challenge.<\/p>\n

A panel of international judges hand-picked by Apple cast their discerning eyes over the lot.<\/p>\n

They were looking for the ability of a macro snap to turn the ordinary into the extraordinary.<\/p>\n

The top 10 winners came form the USA, China, Hungary, India, Italy, Spain and Thailand.<\/p>\n

And the best photos revealed tiny details that would be missed by the naked eye.<\/p>\n

Snowflakes on puppy hair captured on an iPhone 13 Pro by Tom Reeves from New York CityApple \/ Tom Reeves <\/p>\n

This included tiny snowflakes on a dog\u2019s hair and dewdrops on a spiderweb.<\/p>\n

All of the winning photos will be featured on Apple.com, Apple\u2019s Instagram account, as well as billboards in select cities around the world.<\/p>\n

One particularly impressive shot of a leaf captured by graphic designer Trevor Collins from Boston, USA astounded judges.<\/p>\n

World-famous photographer and Apple judge Peter McKinnon said: \u201cThe reason I like this so much is the obvious layering. The depth of field created with the iPhone here very clearly shows you what the focal point is.<\/p>\n

\u201cAnd represents a fantastic example of how good the software is at completely isolating the foreground, leaving the background blurry.<\/p>\n

\u201cA perfect example of computational depth of field.\u201d<\/p>\n

iPhone macro trick<\/h2>\n

Sea glass captured on an iPhone 13 Pro Max by Guido Cassanelli from Buenos Aires, Argentina.Apple \/ Guido Cassanelli <\/p>\n

So how does the Macro feature work?<\/p>\n

On the new iPhone 13 Pro models, there\u2019s\u00a0a revamped camera system.<\/p>\n

This includes an Ultra Wide camera with a \u201cmuch wider\u201d f\/1.8 aperture.<\/p>\n

There\u2019s also a new autofocus system, and 92% improvement to low-light photography \u2013 for brighter and sharper images.<\/p>\n

Apple has combined the new lens, improved autofocus and some computational tricks to allow the iPhone to capture macro images.<\/p>\n

To use the macro feature, make sure to get very close to the subject \u2013 up to two inches away.<\/p>\n

Then make sure the primary focus point is near the center of the frame.<\/p>\n

Tap an area to set a specific focus point.<\/p>\n

And then capture your stunning macro image with a tap of the screen.<\/p>\n
