Disney memories are meant to last forever, but not when it comes to princesses. <\/p>\n

A former Disney performer shared the key to getting hired as one of the iconic characters: having a \u201cforgettable pretty face.\u201d<\/p>\n

Melanie, who withheld her last name for privacy reasons, told Insider about her experience landing a role as a Disney performer at the popular theme parks. <\/p>\n

The TikTok and Twitch star began auditioning at Disney when she turned 18 and tried out for different roles 13 times before being cast as Drizella Tremaine, one of Cinderella\u2019s evil stepsisters. <\/p>\n

\u201cThe standard that you\u2019re trying to attain when you\u2019re trying to get hired as a princess essentially is what is the most forgettable pretty face you can have,\u201d she said. <\/p>\n

She often shares stories of her experience auditioning and working at Disney parks on TikTok as @magicgirlmelanie. Melanie claimed that the first round of cuts in her audition were made after casting directors lined everyone up and gave them a once over. <\/p>\n

\u201cYou can\u2019t prove anything, it\u2019s just what some strangers think your face looks like,\u201d she said. \u201cYou can\u2019t earn it really, which is hard. It\u2019s hard to accept.\u201d <\/p>\n

Sarah Daniels, 32, who worked at Disney parks and cruises as Mickey Mouse, Alice, Wendy, Tinker Bell, Ariel and young Elsa for nearly a decade, agreed with Melanie\u2019s claims. <\/p>\n

\u201cThat\u2019s a very funny quote for her. Good for her,\u201d Daniels told The Post. <\/p>\n

The musical-theater actress said casting directors like \u201ceveryone to look like each other\u201d so that there is continuity when different people play the same role.<\/p>\n

Daniels also said that snagging a role as a face character is about \u201chaving normal features\u201d \u2014 as well as the correct height and size. <\/p>\n

\u201cIt might not even be about your face,\u201d she said. She explained that the parks are mostly searching for someone who fits the costume and looks good in the wig. <\/p>\n

\u201cThere\u2019s a thousand white women who look like Cinderella,\u201d Daniels said. <\/p>\n

Daniels claimed that being cast as a Disney parks princess isn\u2019t about being \u201cconventionally pretty\u201d but \u201chaving normal features\u201d and fitting into their costumes. While the former Disney character actor eventually earned the coveted spot as a stage performer, she said the role was not about having talent \u2014 or even being beautiful. \u201cBeing a face character, you\u2019re pretty much just a Barbie. You\u2019re just a chick in a wig,\u201d she said. <\/p>\n

And once Daniels was upgraded to a performer, two members of Disney\u2019s casting team pointed out that the 21-year-old\u2019s 5-foot, 115-pound figure didn\u2019t match the Little Mermaid Ariel\u2019s 16-year-old cartoon frame, which sent her spiraling into an eating disorder that lasted past her Disney days. <\/p>\n

A \u201csilhouette issue\u201d is a common fear for women cast in these roles, particularly those who play Ariel and splash around in her grotto in only a mermaid tail and bikini top. <\/p>\n

Daniels also posts TikToks as @sarahdanielstv about her time at \u201cThe Happiest Place on Earth\u201d and said there seems to be never-ending interest about her experiences. <\/p>\n

Disney did not respond to The Post for a comment. <\/p>\n

\u201cI think there\u2019s this weird thing about being a Disney princess. Women imagine that being told you\u2019re beautiful enough to be a princess will give them confidence and validation because you\u2019ve done the best you can do,\u201d Daniels said. <\/p>\n
