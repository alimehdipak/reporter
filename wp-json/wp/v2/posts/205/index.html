A virgin mouse has given birth to babies that were conceived from an unfertilized egg, as part of a groundbreaking new study.<\/p>\n

The experiment, which was conducted by researchers at Shanghai Jiao Tong University, was published on Monday in the\u00a0Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences.<\/p>\n

The researchers promoted asexual reproduction in the mouse by altering the DNA in 200 unfertilized eggs.<\/p>\n

The researchers altered genetic markers which require a genome from a second parent.<\/p>\n

The process bypassed the requirements of a sperm cell for reproduction and allowed an egg to grow into an embryo by replicating its own cells.<\/p>\n

Around 190 of those embryos were transferred into surrogate mother mice.<\/p>\n

The results led to an \u201cimmaculate conception\u201d of a litter of mice pups that were genetically-identical clones of their mother.<\/p>\n

But of the 190 implanted eggs, only three pups ended up being born, and just one survived.<\/p>\n

The research was into parthenogenesis \u2013 which is the spontaneous formation of an embryo from an unfertilized egg cell.<\/p>\n

Parthenogenesis is not uncommon in the animal world, especially in invertebrates like worms.<\/p>\n

But it is very rare in mammals because of genomic imprinting.<\/p>\n

When genes undergo imprinting, they often get stamped with an identifier that indicates the parent of origin during the formation of egg and sperm cells.<\/p>\n

This genetic stamping plays a pivotal role in the development of an organism and influences which parental genetics are passed down from the mother and father.<\/p>\n

In this study, researchers looked at these marked genes in mice and used a gene-editing method known as DNA methylation to see if they could \u201cimprove parthenogenetic development.\u201d<\/p>\n

The researchers said that using DNA methylation allowed them to essentially \u201crewrite\u201d seven imprinting control regions (ICRs), resulting in an immaculate conception.<\/p>\n

\u201cBy the targeted methylation editing of seven ICRs, we were able to generate viable full-term offspring directly from single unfertilized mouse oocytes,\u201d the study\u2019s authors wrote.<\/p>\n

The team used CRISPR, a gene-editing tool, to remove the genetic marks on 220 unfertilized mouse eggs, of which 190 were successfully transferred to foster female mice, according to a report by\u00a0The Daily Beast.<\/p>\n

From the successful transplants, three mouse pups were born, with only one surviving.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe transfer of modified parthenogenetic embryos into foster mothers resulted in significantly extended development and finally in the generation of viable full-term offspring,\u201d the study read.<\/p>\n

\u201cThese data demonstrate that parthenogenesis can be achieved by targeted epigenetic rewriting of multiple critical imprinting control regions,\u201d it continued.<\/p>\n

The findings in this research open many avenues in genetics research and medicine that may help us to not only regulate reproduction but to cure genetic diseases.<\/p>\n

Scientists are currently exploring genome editing for single-gene disorders such as\u00a0cystic fibrosis,\u00a0hemophilia, and\u00a0sickle cell disease, though many believe it may also hold promise for the treatment and prevention of cancer, heart disease, and HIV.<\/p>\n
