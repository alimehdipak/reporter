A Twitter user who was convicted in UK court of posting a \u201cgrossly offensive\u201d tweet about a war veteran has reportedly been sentenced to 150 hours of community service.<\/p>\n

Joseph Kelly, a 36-year-old man from Glasgow, posted a tweet last year about Captain Sir Thomas Moore, a British war veteran who became a national icon for raising money for healthcare workers in 2020.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cThe only good Brit soldier is a deed one, burn auld fella buuuuurn,\u201d Kelly wrote on Twitter in February 2021 on the day after Moore died.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Kelly was drunk, almost immediately regretted sending the tweet and then deleted it after about 20 minutes, his attorney Tony Callahan said, according to Scottish newspaper The National.<\/p>\n

\u201cHis level of criminality was a drunken post, at a time when he was struggling emotionally, which he regretted and almost instantly removed,\u201d Callahan reportedly said, adding that Kelly only had a handful of followers at the time.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cHe accepts he was wrong,\u201d Callahan added. \u201cHe did not anticipate what would happen. He took steps almost immediately to delete the tweet but the genie was out of the bottle by then.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Nevertheless, British prosecutors were pushing for jail time under a controversial UK law punishing online posts that are \u201cgrossly offensive or of an indecent, obscene or menacing character\u201d with up to six months behind bars.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Sheriff Adrian Cottam, who was responsible for Kelly\u2019s sentencing, reportedly said in court that his punishment was important for \u201cdeterrence.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s important for other people to realize how quickly things can get out of control,\u201d Cottam reportedly said. \u201cYou are a good example of that, not having many followers.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cThe deterrence is really to show people that despite the steps you took to try and recall matters, as soon as you press the blue button that\u2019s it,\u201d Cottam added.<\/p>\n

Kelly was found guilty under Section 127 of the UK\u2019s Communications Act., which is set to be replaced by the country\u2019s sweeping Online Safety Bill.
