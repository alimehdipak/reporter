<\/p>\n

NASA is in the process of opening a Moon sample that has been vacuumed-sealed for almost 50 years.<\/p>\n

The mysterious sample was collected during the US space agency\u2019s last manned mission to the Moon in 1972.<\/p>\n

The sample from the Apollo 17 mission will be exactly 50 years old on December 13 this year.<\/p>\n

Astronauts Eugene Cernan and Harrison \u201cJack\u201d Schmitt collected the Moon sample by hammering a 14-inch tube into the lunar surface.<\/p>\n

They also collected another sample that wasn\u2019t sealed.<\/p>\n

Both tubes were filled up with Moon rock and dust.<\/p>\n

The two samples were brought back to Earth and the one that wasn\u2019t vacuum sealed was opened in 2019.<\/p>\n

The vacuum-sealed tube is more exciting because it may contain substances called \u2018volatiles\u2019.<\/p>\n

Volatiles are gases that evaporate at normal temperatures.<\/p>\n

One of the Apollo 17 astronauts used a metal pole to collect a sample on the surface of the Moon in 1972.NASA\/Corbis via Getty Images <\/p>\n

They would have escaped from the unsealed Moon sample tube but they may be present in the vacuum-sealed one.<\/p>\n

NASA has waited until now to open the sealed sample because they wanted to take advantage of future technology.<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s Lori Glaze, director of the Planetary Science Division, explained: \u201cThe agency knew science and technology would evolve and allow scientists to study the material in new ways to address new questions in the future.\u201d<\/p>\n

Ryan Zeigler, the Apollo sample curator, added: \u201cA lot of people are getting excited.<\/p>\n

\u201cUniversity of New Mexico\u2019s Chip Shearer proposed the project over a decade ago, and for the past three years, we\u2019ve had two great teams developing the unique equipment to make it possible.\u201d<\/p>\n

The careful process of opening the sample has begun.<\/p>\n

The outer seal has been opened but the inner seal is still intact.<\/p>\n

Scientists will pierce the sample tube first and collect any gases that may be trapped.<\/p>\n

Astronauts Eugene A. Cernan (left) and Harrison H. \u2018Jack\u2019 Schmitt were part of the famed Apollo 17 mission in 1972.HUM Images <\/p>\n

After the gas extraction, the lunar soil and rocks will be removed.<\/p>\n

Scientists plan to do this in late spring.<\/p>\n

Francesca McDonald, who is leading the project at ESA, said: \u201cEach gas component that is analyzed can help to tell a different part of the story about the origin and evolution of volatiles on the Moon and within the early Solar System.\u201d<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s scientists look forward to learning more about \u2018volatiles\u2019 gasses inside samples from the Moon.NASA\/Corbis via Getty Images NASA scientists have been very careful with extracting gasses of the old sealed Moon samples.Robert Markowitz\/NASA-Johnson Space <\/p>\n

The painstaking work is taking place at NASA\u2019s Johnson Space Center in Houston by the Astromaterials Research and Exploration Science Division (ARES).<\/p>\n

The center is home to a selection of extraterrestrial samples collected by NASA.<\/p>\n
