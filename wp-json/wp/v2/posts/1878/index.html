<\/p>\n

Flying cars and roads in the sky will be a big part of our future, according to experts.<\/p>\n

Skyroads, a German tech company, is developing the infrastructure to give flying cars routes and communication tools that will make urban flying not just possible, but safe and reliable.<\/p>\n

When the car replaced the horse and carriage, governments added to existing infrastructure by widening dirt roads, adding tons of concrete and putting up steel guardrails.<\/p>\n

It wasn\u2019t easy, but the bones of a transportation system were there.<\/p>\n

This is not the case with flying cars.<\/p>\n

We do have air traffic control, regulated air space and runways \u2013 but this is hardly sufficient for the future of free-flowing, air traffic taking passengers and cargo across densely populated cities.<\/p>\n

The takeoff<\/h2>\n

\u201cI founded Skyroads with one goal: make flying accessible for everyone,\u201d CEO Corvin Huber told The Sun in an exclusive interview.<\/p>\n

Skyroads envisions a future where cities are populated with eVTOL aircrafts flying overheard \u2013 electric Vertical Takeoff and Landing planes that ascend and descend in a straight line.<\/p>\n

There will be take off and landing zones called \u201cvertiports\u201d scattered throughout cities in high volume areas.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe vertiports internal processes \u2013 clearing landing pads for the next vehicles, timing the eVTOL\u2019s charging processes, preparing vehicles for the next set of passengers \u2013 will mesh down to the second with the arrival of vehicles,\u201d Huber told The Sun.<\/p>\n

Essentially, Skyroads will act as the eye-in-the-sky and see that the vehicles\u2019 flight plans are not just geographically accurate but also extremely precise in timing \u2013 a feature, Huber noted, is not currently available to planes.<\/p>\n

Under these conditions, urban flying will be much more seamless than ground-based car traffic \u2013 more like a train going along its route to a subway stop rather than a car meandering and rerouting through roads and tunnels.<\/p>\n

CEO Corvin Huber founded Skyroads in 2019.Skyroads <\/p>\n

The flight<\/h2>\n

\u201cUrban Air Mobility is coming. The speed at which it comes depends on how fast some of the challenges are solved,\u201d Huber said.<\/p>\n

Battery efficiency marks one of the roadblocks on the path to flying cars.<\/p>\n

Elon Musk, a transportation visionary in his own right, is dumping resources into\u00a0Tesla\u00a0to\u00a0make batteries\u00a0cheaper and more efficient.<\/p>\n

\u201cBattery cell production is the fundamental rate-limiter slowing down a sustainable energy future,\u201d Musk said in a 2021\u00a0tweet.<\/p>\n

While industry titans square their efforts towards developing a new age of batteries, Skyroads is preparing the air for cargo and passenger traffic.<\/p>\n

The company is creating a system of \u201cdigital roads\u201d for flying cars or drones to make the air transportation functional and attractive \u2013 not unlike how Uber is, in-part, powered by Google Maps.<\/p>\n

The future of air travel could be coming to a city near you.Skyroads <\/p>\n

The landing<\/h2>\n

Huber and his team are confident in their concept and ability to beat out competitors.<\/p>\n

\u201cAll the initial founders wanted to bet their own resources on the project, so that\u2019s how we got things off the ground \u2013 by investing in ourselves,\u201d Huber said.<\/p>\n

Skyroads expects to be operational this decade, with a major product test planned to fly over Munich, Germany this year.<\/p>\n

The company is working to model sudden air disturbances like medical helicopters so that when Skyroads comes online, it\u2019s ready for anything.<\/p>\n
