Astronomers have caught a rare glimpse of a potentially hazardous asteroid ahead of its flyby of Earth this week.<\/p>\n

The space rock 2007 FF1 is expected to make a close and safe encounter with our planet on April 1, according to space trackers.<\/p>\n

The\u00a0April Fool\u2019s\u00a0asteroid will pass within 4.6 million miles of our planet \u2013 or roughly 19 times the distance between Earth and the moon.<\/p>\n

Ahead of the relatively close approach, scientists at the Virtual Telescope Project (VTP) captured an image of the asteroid in the night sky.<\/p>\n

It was imaged using one of the Italian facility\u2019s largest scopes at a distance of about 7.2 million miles from Earth.<\/p>\n

\u201cThis about 200 meters large asteroid will reach its minimum distance from us on April 1 at 21:35 UTC,\u201d VTP founder Gianluca Masi wrote.<\/p>\n

\u201cOf course, there are no risks at all for our planet.\u201d<\/p>\n

The object has been added to NASA\u2019s \u201cClose Approaches\u201d\u00a0database\u00a0\u2013 though it poses no danger to our planet.<\/p>\n

Thousands of so-called near-Earth objects (NEOs) are tracked to provide an early warning if they shift onto a collision course with our planet.<\/p>\n

Any object that comes within 4.65 million miles of us is considered \u201cpotentially hazardous\u201d by cautious space organizations.<\/p>\n

According to NASA, 2007 FF1 is traveling at roughly eight miles per second.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s one of a dozen or so asteroids expected to make close approaches this week.<\/p>\n

Fortunately, none of the asteroids being tracked by the space agency are thought to pose any danger to us.<\/p>\n

Astronomers are currently tracking 2,000 asteroids, comets and other objects that could one day threaten our pale blue dot, and new ones are discovered every day.<\/p>\n

Earth hasn\u2019t seen an asteroid of apocalyptic scale since the space rock that wiped out the dinosaurs 66million years ago.<\/p>\n

However, smaller objects capable of flattening an entire city crash into Earth every so often.<\/p>\n

One a few hundred meters across devastated 800-square miles of forest near Tunguska in Siberia on June 30, 1908.<\/p>\n

Fortunately, NASA doesn\u2019t believe any of the NEOs it keeps an eye on are on a collision course with our planet.<\/p>\n

That could change in the coming months or years, however, as the space agency frequently revises objects\u2019 predicted trajectories.<\/p>\n

\u201cNASA knows of no asteroid or comet currently on a collision course with Earth, so the probability of a major collision is quite small,\u201d NASA says.<\/p>\n

\u201cIn fact, as best as we can tell, no large object is likely to strike the Earth any time in the next several hundred years.\u201d<\/p>\n

Even if one were to hit our planet, the vast majority of asteroids would not wipe out life as we know it.<\/p>\n

\u201cGlobal catastrophes\u201d are only triggered when objects larger than 2, 952 feet across smash into Earth, according to NASA.<\/p>\n
