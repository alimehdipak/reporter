Julian Lennon broke his longstanding vow never to perform his father\u2019s most iconic solo song, \u201cImagine,\u201d during a benefit for Ukrainian refugees Saturday.<\/p>\n

\u201cToday, for the first time ever, I publicly performed my Dad\u2019s song, \u2018Imagine\u2019,\u201d Lennon, 59, wrote on YouTube. \u201cThe song reflects the light at the end of the tunnel, that we are all hoping for.\u201d<\/p>\n

The singer-songwriter son of Beatle John Lennon presented the cover version of his father\u2019s ode to peace as part of the Stand Up For Ukraine campaign, a global fund-raising effort broadcast from Warsaw, Poland.<\/p>\n

\u201cI had always said, that the only time I would ever consider singing \u2018Imagine\u2019 would be if it was the \u2018End of the World\u2019,\u201d Lennon wrote.<\/p>\n

But \u201cthe War on Ukraine is an unimaginable tragedy,\u201d he explained. \u201cAs a human, and as an artist, I felt compelled to respond in the most significant way I could.\u201d<\/p>\n<\/p>\n

The solemn video clip showed Lennon singing \u2014 in cadences uncannily like his father\u2019s \u2014 surrounded by candles, accompanied by acoustic guitarist Nuno Bettencourt.<\/p>\n

The performance closed out a televised European Union pledge drive that raised $10.1 billion in public, private, and corporate cash earmarked for refugee assistance.<\/p>\n

Lennon is not the first artist to make headlines by doing music in support of Ukraine. <\/p>\n

At midnight Friday, Pink Floyd \u2013 minus Roger Waters \u2013 released \u201cHey Hey Rise Up,\u201d its first original music in 28 years, for the UN\u2019s Ukraine Humanitarian Fund. <\/p>\n

Guitarist and singer David Gilmour told the Guardian he was inspired by Ukrainian musician Andriy Khlyvnyuk, who left his band BoomBox\u2019s US tour to fight in Ukraine. <\/p>\n

Gilmour\u00a0saw an Instagram video\u00a0of the musician in military gear singing a protest song in Kyiv\u2019s Sofiyskaya Square and then felt inspired to do something about it.<\/p>\n
