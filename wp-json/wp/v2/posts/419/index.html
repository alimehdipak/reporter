<\/p>\n

Google has issued a chilling warning to the 2.6 billion people who use its Chrome web browser.<\/p>\n

The US tech titan told fans last week to expect a rise in the number of reported cyberattacks in the coming months.<\/p>\n

Adrian Taylor, a member of Chrome\u2019s Security Team, explained the increase in a\u00a0blog post\u00a0on March 10.<\/p>\n

He was prompted to write the post in response to increasing reports of exploits found \u201cin the wild\u201d by Google\u2019s network of researchers.<\/p>\n

Those are the software vulnerabilities that are actively being used by cybercrooks to break into Chrome and attack users.<\/p>\n

Google reports the exploits it finds in a regular blog series.<\/p>\n

\u201cIf you are a regular reader of our Chrome release blog, you may have noticed that phrases like \u2018exploit for CVE-1234-567 exists in the wild\u2019 have been appearing more often recently,\u201d Taylor wrote.<\/p>\n

He added that the rise in cyberattack reports is likely a result of two factors.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhile the increase may initially seem concerning, it\u2019s important to understand the reason behind this trend.\u201d Taylor wrote.<\/p>\n

\u201cIf it\u2019s because there are many more exploits in the wild, it could point to a worrying trend.\u201d<\/p>\n

He added: \u201cOn the other hand, if we\u2019re simply gaining more visibility into exploitation by attackers, it\u2019s actually a good thing!<\/p>\n

\u201cIt\u2019s good because it means we can respond by providing bug fixes to our users faster, and we can learn more about how real attackers operate.<\/p>\n

\u201cSo, which is it? It\u2019s likely a little of both.\u201d<\/p>\n

The number of in-the-wild exploits, also known as \u201czero days\u201d, discovered by researchers more than tripled between 2019 and 2021, according to data from Google\u2019s Project Zero cybersecurity lab.<\/p>\n

The dramatic rise in Chrome\u2019s popularity in recent years may partly be to blame, Taylor said, as it makes the browser a more attractive prospect for cyber attacks due to its large base of potential victims.<\/p>\n

Also responsible for the rise is the increased complexity of browsers such as Chrome as PCs and smartphones become smarter.<\/p>\n

Taylor explained that hackers increasingly need multiple attacks to break through Chrome\u2019s defenses.<\/p>\n

This is due to its security team\u2019s decision to separate running programs so that attacks cannot spread between vulnerable parts of the browser.<\/p>\n

\u201cAn attacker generally now has to use more bugs than they previously did,\u201d Taylor wrote.<\/p>\n

\u201cFor exactly the same level of attacker success, we\u2019d see more in-the-wild bugs reported over time, as we add more layers of defense that the attacker needs to bypass.\u201d<\/p>\n

For its part, Chrome is accelerating its release cycles to try and slash the time between an exploit\u2019s discovery and its patch launch.<\/p>\n

That gap has already dropped from 35 days in Chrome 76 to an average of 18 days today, with plans in place to reduce this further in future.<\/p>\n

Users can keep their PCs protected by ensuring they keep their browsers up to date with the latest software releases.<\/p>\n

\u201cAbove all,\u201d Taylor wrote. \u201cIf Chrome is reminding you to update, please do!\u201d<\/p>\n

To update Chrome, open the browser and\u00a0click the More icon (three vertical dots) in\u00a0the top right.<\/p>\n
