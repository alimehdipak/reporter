Earth, Wind & Fire saxophonist Andrew Woolfolk, who was instrumental in defining the disco sound of the 1970s, has died at age 71. <\/p>\n

The musician \u2014 who helped create some of the band\u2019s biggest hits, including \u201cSeptember\u201d and \u201cBoogie Wonderland\u201d \u2014 passed away Monday following a six-year illness. An official cause of death has not been disclosed. <\/p>\n

The sad news was announced by bandmate Philip Bailey, who penned a touching tribute on Instagram. <\/p>\n

\u201cI met him in high school, and we quickly became friends and band mates,\u201d Bailey wrote. \u201cWe lost him today, after being ill of over six years. He has transitioned on to the forever, from this land of the dying to the land of the living.\u201d<\/p>\n

He added: \u201d Great memories. Great Talent. Funny. Competitive. Quick witted. And always styling. Booski\u2026\u00a0\u00a0I\u2019ll see you on the other side, my friend.\u201d<\/p>\n

Andrew Woolfolk \u2014 the Earth, Wind & Fire saxophonist who helped define the disco sound of the 1970s \u2014 has died at 71. Getty Images <\/p>\n

Woolfolk \u2014 who was born in Texas in 1950 \u2014 joined Earth, Wind & Fire in 1972, three years after the group first formed. <\/p>\n

The band soon shot to international fame, winning six Grammys and selling tens of millions of records during the 1970s and early 1980s. <\/p>\n

In addition to \u201cSeptember\u201d and \u201cBoogie Wonderland,\u201d Woolfolk\u2019s saxophone can also be heard in a dozens of the band\u2019s other smash hits, including \u201cLet\u2019s Groove,\u201d \u201cThat\u2019s the Way of the World\u201d and \u201cAfter the Love Has Gone.\u201d He also played flute and percussion on several tracks. <\/p>\n<\/p>\n

Woolfolk left the band in 1985 before returning again two years later. He stayed on until 1993.<\/p>\n

In 2000, the saxophonist was inducted into the the Rock & Roll Hall of Fame along with eight other Earth, Wind & Fire members.\u00a0<\/p>\n

In addition to his work with the iconic group, the star also collaborated with Phil Collins and Deneise Williams. <\/p>\n

Despite being a member of one of America\u2019s most famous bands, Woolfolk was fiercely protective of his private life \u2014 and did not disclose whether he was married or had children. <\/p>\n
