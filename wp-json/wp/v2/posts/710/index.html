<\/p>\n

Space weather experts have spotted the sun ejecting a large mass of particles and think this could hit Earth in the next few days.<\/p>\n

When ejections like this hit Earth\u2019s magnetic field, they can cause solar storms.<\/p>\n

An ejection like this is known as a solar flare called a coronal mass ejection (CME).<\/p>\n

A CME is a huge expulsion\u00a0of plasma from the sun\u2019s outer layer, called the corona.<\/p>\n

These expulsions shoot through\u00a0space\u00a0and can hit Earth.<\/p>\n

Fortunately, the solar storm predicted for this Wednesday, March 23, is only likely to be a \u201cG1 minor,\u201d meaning you probably won\u2019t even notice it if it happens.<\/p>\n

Experts at\u00a0Spaceweather.com\u00a0explained: \u201cAnother CME is heading for Earth, and it\u2019s a little off target.<\/p>\n

\u201cA glancing blow (or near miss) is possible during the late hours of\u00a0March 23rd, according to NOAA forecasters.<\/p>\n

\u201cThis will be the 3rd time in the past week that a CME has\u00a0almost<\/em>\u00a0landed a direct hit.<\/p>\n

Illustration of a coronal mass ejection impacting the Earth\u2019s atmosphere. These events, CMEs for short, are powerful releases of solar charged particles (plasma) and magnetic field, travelling on the solar wind. When a CME hits Earth, it can cause a geomagnetic storm which disrupts the planet\u2019s magnetosphere, our radio transmissions and electrical power lines. \u201cEven a near miss can produce bright Arctic auroras. Best case scenario for auroraphiles: A minor\u00a0G1-class\u00a0geomagnetic storm.\u201d<\/p>\n

The \u201cG1 minor\u201d category of solar storm could cause weak power grid fluctuations and have a small impact on satellite communications.<\/p>\n

The Earth\u2019s magnetic field helps to protect us from the more extreme consequences of solar flares.Getty Images\/Science Photo Libra <\/p>\n

A G1 storm can also confuse migrating animals that rely on the Earth\u2019s magnetic field for a sense of direction.<\/p>\n

One good thing about solar storms is that they can produce very pretty natural light displays like the northern lights.<\/p>\n

Those natural light displays are called auroras and are examples of the Earth\u2019s magnetosphere getting bombarded by solar wind, which creates the pretty green and blue displays.<\/p>\n

Experts at Spaceweather.com explained: \u201cAnother CME is heading for Earth, and it\u2019s a little off target.\u201dGetty Images <\/p>\n

The Earth\u2019s magnetic field helps to protect us from the more extreme consequences of solar flares.<\/p>\n

In 1989, a strong solar eruption shot so many electrically charged particles at Earth that the Canadian province of Quebec lost power for nine hours.<\/p>\n

\u201cA glancing blow (or near miss) is possible during the late hours of March 23rd, \u201d according to NOAA forecasters. Getty Images\/Science Photo Libra <\/p>\n

As well as causing issues for our tech on Earth, they can be deadly for an astronaut if they result in injury or interfere with mission control communications.<\/p>\n

The sun is currently at the start of a new 11-year solar cycle, which usually sees eruptions and flares grow more intense and extreme.<\/p>\n
