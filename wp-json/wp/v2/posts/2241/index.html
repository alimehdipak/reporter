<\/p>\n

US stocks tumbled Friday to cap a week of volatile trading as investors reacted to downbeat earnings reports from blue-chip tech firms and worrisome economic data.<\/p>\n

The Dow Jones Industrial Average plummeted 939.18 points, or 2.77%. The tech-heavy Nasdaq index fell 536.89 points, or 4.17%, and the broad-based S&P 500 sank 155.62 points, or 3.6%.<\/p>\n

Poor performance by major tech firms with trillion-dollar valuations were a key drag on the Nasdaq \u2013 with Amazon and Apple each providing bad news for investors. <\/p>\n

Concerns about renewed COVID-19 lockdowns in China and the looming possibility of a recession added more pain for the market.<\/p>\n

Amazon finished the day 14% lower after the e-commerce giant reported its first quarterly loss in seven years and provided a weak sales forecast for its upcoming quarter. The stock recorded its worst day since 2006 and left company shares near a two-year low.<\/p>\n

Apple shares also dipped nearly 4% after the iPhone maker warned that supply chain woes could hurt sales by a whopping $4 billion to $8 billion in its current quarter.<\/p>\n

The Federal Reserve\u2019s plan to hike interest rates has weighed on markets for weeks.REUTERS <\/p>\n

All 11 S&P 500 sector indexes traded lower. The Nasdaq has shed about 13% in April \u2014 its worst monthly performance since the Great Recession.<\/p>\n

\u201cMarket participants are nervous to begin with, so there is a quick trigger when it comes to these names when there\u2019s any uncertainty,\u201d Keith Buchanan, senior portfolio manager at Globalt Investments in Atlanta, told Reuters. <\/p>\n

\u201cWhen assumptions about these companies\u2019 growth fail to materialize, then there\u2019s definitely a \u2018shoot first and ask questions later\u2019 mentality,\u201d he added.<\/p>\n

The likelihood of aggressive action from the Federal Reserve to combat surging inflation has weighed on stocks for weeks. <\/p>\n

Fed Chair Jerome Powell has indicated the central bank could hike its benchmark rate next month at a sharper clip than the quarter-percentage-point increase it enacted in March.<\/p>\n

Investors are concerned about the possibility of a recession.REUTERS <\/p>\n

The personal consumption expenditures price index \u2013 the Fed\u2019s preferred measure of inflation \u2013 jumped by 0.9% in March alone and is up 6.6% compared to one year earlier.<\/p>\n

That came after the Consumer Price Index jumped 8.5% earlier this month \u2013 its steepest rate since 1981.<\/p>\n

Investors also digested a surprise downturn in U.S. gross domestic product, which declined at a 1.4% pace in the first quarter despite economists\u2019 expectations for a slight gain. <\/p>\n

That news was revealed at a time of mounting concern that the US economy is headed for a recession in the months ahead.<\/p>\n

Stocks are struggling despite better-than-average performances from companies this earnings season. Some 81% of S&P 500 companies who have reported so far have beat expectations, better than the 66% historic rate, according to Refinitiv.<\/p>\n
