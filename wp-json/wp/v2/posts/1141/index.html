<\/p>\n

The awe-inspiring moment that a powerful solar flare erupted from the sun earlier this week has been captured by NASA.<\/p>\n

The space agency\u2019s Solar Dynamics Observatory spotted the super hot stream of radiation as it was belched from our star on Wednesday.<\/p>\n

According to a NASA\u00a0blog post, the explosion burst in the most powerful class of flare of which our sun is capable, an X-class clocking in at X1.3.<\/p>\n

It poses no threat to Earth as it was fired in a different direction, but had it struck our planet, it could have knocked out power grids and satellites.<\/p>\n

\u201cSolar flares are powerful bursts of energy,\u201d\u00a0NASA\u00a0wrote.<\/p>\n

\u201cFlares and solar eruptions can impact radio communications, electric power grids, navigation signals, and pose risks to spacecraft and astronauts.\u201d<\/p>\n

The stream of hot material is the result of a\u00a0frenzy of solar storms\u00a0that have erupted from the sun throughout this week.<\/p>\n

A total of 17 were captured by NASA observatories exploding from the star on Monday and at least two were fired in Earth\u2019s direction.<\/p>\n

When CMEs reach Earth, they trigger what is known as a geomagnetic storm \u2014 a largely harmless disturbance of the magnetic field.<\/p>\n

Thankfully, those launched in our direction were low-intensity flares that posed no threat to technology on Earth.<\/p>\n

The March 30 flare imaged by NASA, on the other hand, could have caused chaos had it come this way.<\/p>\n

\u201cThis flare is classified as an X-Class flare,\u201d NASA wrote. This was an X1.3.<\/p>\n

NASA continued: \u201cX-class denotes the most intense flares, while the number provides more information about its strength.<\/p>\n

\u201cAn X2 is twice as intense as an X1, an X3 is three times as intense, etc.\u201d<\/p>\n

Data on this week\u2019s CMEs has been captured by NASA\u2019s Solar Dynamics Observatory and Solar and Heliospheric Observatory.<\/p>\n

A powerful X-class solar flare as it erupted from the sun on March 30.NASA Solar Dynamics Observatory <\/p>\n

They\u2019re the result of an unusually active sunspot, dark and violent regions that appear due to a tangle of powerful magnetic forces.<\/p>\n

The storms have led to showings of the northern lights at unusually low latitudes in Europe and North America.<\/p>\n

Solar storms are caused by CMEs and solar flares, which are huge expulsions\u00a0of hot material called plasma from the sun\u2019s outer layer.<\/p>\n

They can lead to the appearance of colorful auroras by energizing particles in our planet\u2019s atmosphere.<\/p>\n

Each solar storm is graded by severity on a scale of 1 to 5, with a G1 described as \u201cminor\u201d and a G5 as \u201cextreme.\u201d<\/p>\n

Storms at the upper end of the scale wreak havoc on our planet\u2019s magnetic field, which can disrupt power grids and communications networks.<\/p>\n

\u201cHarmful radiation from a flare cannot pass through Earth\u2019s atmosphere to physically affect humans on the ground,\u201d NASA says.<\/p>\n

\u201cHowever \u2014 when intense enough \u2014 they can disturb the atmosphere in the layer where GPS and communications signals travel.\u201d<\/p>\n

In the past, larger solar flares have wreaked havoc on our planet.<\/p>\n

In 1989, a strong solar eruption shot so many electrically charged particles at Earth that the Canadian province of Quebec lost power for nine hours.<\/p>\n

As well as causing issues for our tech, they can cause harm to astronauts working on the International Space Station, either through radiation exposure or by interfering with mission control communications.<\/p>\n

The Earth\u2019s magnetic field helps to protect us from the more extreme consequences of solar flares.<\/p>\n
