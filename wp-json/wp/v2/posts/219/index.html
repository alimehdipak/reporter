<\/p>\n

NASA has revealed a behind-the-scenes clip of its huge Psyche spacecraft that will one day probe an expensive asteroid.<\/p>\n

The craft will be probing Asteroid 16 Psyche, which is thought to be worth up to $10,000 quadrillion.<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s Jet Propulsion Laboratory posted\u00a0a video\u00a0of the Psyche spacecraft\u2019s new \u2018solar arrays\u2019 to its YouTube channel.<\/p>\n

The YouTube description explains: \u201cNASA\u2019s Psyche mission is preparing for a 1.5 billion-mile (2.4 billion-kilometer) solar-powered trip to the metal-rich asteroid of the same name.<\/p>\n

\u201cIn a cleanroom at NASA\u2019s Jet Propulsion Laboratory in February 2022, twin solar arrays were attached to the spacecraft body, unfolded lengthwise, and then re-stowed as tests on Psyche continue.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe five-panel, cross-shaped solar arrays are the largest ever installed on a spacecraft at JPL, so engineers had to test them one at a time.\u201d<\/p>\n

Pysche should be sent on its mission in August this year.<\/p>\n

NASA isn\u2019t planning on mining Asteroid 16 Psyche for its gold but it does want to study it.<\/p>\n

Scientists want to work out exactly what it\u2019s made of and how much it\u2019s really worth.<\/p>\n

The video shows the spacecraft\u2019s large solar \u2018wings\u2019 extending.NASA Jet Propulsion Laboratory <\/p>\n

The mission will be NASA\u2019s first visit to a metallic asteroid.<\/p>\n

So far Psyche has been studied from afar by the Hubble Space Telescope and most things we know about it are estimations.<\/p>\n

We know 16 Psyche is nestled between the orbits of Mars and Jupiter and we think it\u2019s made of solid metal.<\/p>\n

The giant potato-shaped rock is about 140 miles wide.<\/p>\n

Its brightness suggests it\u2019s largely made up of nickel, iron, or gold.<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s Discovery Mission should arrive at 16 Psyche in 2026.<\/p>\n

It reckons Psyche is a survivor of violent hit-and-run collisions between planets which were common when the solar system was forming.<\/p>\n

That means it could tell us how Earth\u2019s core and the cores of the other terrestrial planets were formed.<\/p>\n

Artist\u2019s impression of Asteroid 16 Psyche. NASA\/JPL-Caltech\/ASU <\/p>\n

NASA wants to determine its exact composition and also examine its four-mile crater.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s hoped the crater could teach us how to deflect an asteroid of this side and prevent it from hitting Earth.<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s spacecraft should also map the asteroid and work out how old it is.<\/p>\n

The current plan is to launch it on one of Elon Musk\u2019s SpaceX Falcon Heavy rockets this summer.<\/p>\n

The Psyche spacecraft should fly past Mars in May 2023 before continuing another three years into space for its final destination.<\/p>\n
