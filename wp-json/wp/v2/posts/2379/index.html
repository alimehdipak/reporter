Let them eat cake. <\/p>\n

As Americans pinch pennies to pay for the soaring costs of groceries and gas, Met Gala organizers are being slammed for celebrating over-the-top opulence. <\/p>\n

This year\u2019s gala \u2014 to be held tonight at the Metropolitan Museum of Art \u2014 features the dress code \u201cGilded Glamor and White Tie,\u201d paying homage to America\u2019s era of economic ascent. <\/p>\n

The Gilded Age \u2014 occurring between the years of 1870 and 1900 \u2014 marked a period of rapid economic expansion across the country, during which a small group of American families, including the the Roosevelts, the Vanderbilts and the Rockefellers, acquired outsized wealth. <\/p>\n

But as the cashed-up elites flaunted their fortunes \u2014 with lavish homes, glamorous gowns and priceless Renaissance artworks \u2014 millions of citizens lived in abject poverty, trying to make ends meet. <\/p>\n

Now, some critics have taken to Twitter, pointing out the problematic parallels between the Gilded Age and our current context \u2014 marked not only by growing inequality, but the highest inflation levels in more than 40 years. At the same time, the US economy shrank by 1.4% in the most recent quarter, contracting for the first time since the pandemic-induced recession two years ago.<\/p>\n

The Met Gala, led by Vogue editor-in-chief Anna Wintour (right), has been slammed for this year\u2019s \u201cGilded Age\u201d theme, which is set to celebrate extreme wealth as average Americans struggle amid the soaring cost of living. Getty The Gilded Age was characterized by extreme wealth and opulent fashions. Socialite Alva Vanderbilt (pictured) was new to high society, and wore extravagant dresses in a bid to be accepted by her peers.Wiki Commons <\/p>\n

They added: \u201cTo be clear, I\u2019m normally a big fan. But this year\u2019s theme is a slap in the face to average Americans.\u201d<\/p>\n
