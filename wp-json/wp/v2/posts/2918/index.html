Definition of cannon fodder<\/p>\n

1 : soldiers regarded or treated as expendable in battle.<\/p>\n

What is the literal meaning of fodder?<\/h3>\n

Definition of fodder<\/p>\n

1 : something fed to domestic animals especially : coarse food for cattle, horses, or sheep. 2 : inferior or readily available material used to supply a heavy demand fodder for tabloids This sort of breezy plot line has become cheap fodder for novelists and screenwriters \u2026\u2014 Sally Bedell.<\/p>\n

Where did the term cannon fodder come from?<\/p>\n

The first use of cannon fodder<\/p>\n

In William Shakespeare\u2019s play, Henry IV, Part 1, there is a quote referencing soldiers as \u201cfood for powder.\u201d Here, key character, John Falstaff, is discussing gunpowder and the soldiers who lost their lives along the way. This takes us back as late as the 16th century.<\/p>\n

Is cannon fodder derogatory?<\/p>\n

Cannon fodder is an informal, derogatory term for combatants who are regarded or treated by government or military command as expendable in the face of enemy fire.<\/p>\n

Does fodder mean gossip?<\/p>\n

Fodder is not just used to describe cattle feed. We use the word to talk about other kinds of feeding that don\u2019t involve actual food. A new celebrity marriage is fodder for gossip magazines.<\/p>\n

Does fodder mean useless?<\/p>\n

Fodder for conversation is generally where you\u2019re talking about something specific, and it can be useless, idle chatter, but fodder by itself does not mean chatter. Since fodder is food fed to fatten cattle, it\u2019s a perfectly understandable extended use for feeding into the rumor mill or chat column.<\/p>\n

Are soldiers expendable?<\/h3>\n

Soldiers, like tax dollars, are expendable.
