US stocks turned positive after volatile trading Monday as investors shook off\u00a0mounting anxiety\u00a0as Wall Street enters a week of crucial earnings reports from major tech firms.<\/p>\n

One the bright spots on US indices was Twitter, with shares rallying nearly 6% after the embattled social media platform\u2019s board said it would accept billionaire Elon Musk\u2019s $44 billion offer to buy the company.<\/p>\n

The Dow Jones Industrial Average reversed earlier loses to close more than 230 points higher, or about 0.7%. Earlier in the day, the Dow fell by nearly 500 points. The tech-heavy Nasdaq and the broad-based S&P 500 also finished in the green.<\/p>\n

Stocks clawed back their losses after the Dow posted its worst one-day performance since October 2020 last Friday\u00a0\u2014\u00a0plunging more than 900 points\u00a0after top Federal Reserve officials indicated sharper-than-expected rate hikes might be necessary to curb inflation.<\/p>\n

The Federal Reserve has indicated it could hike interest rates at a sharper pace than expected.REUTERS <\/p>\n

Twitter\u2019s decision to accept Musk\u2019s offer followed days of uncertainty about the company\u2019s future \u2013 with the billionaire securing outside funds to back his bid and the social media firm\u2019s board enacting a \u201cpoison pill\u201d to limit his leverage.<\/p>\n

\u201cI think it\u2019s just a confidence thing that, hey, there are still people that are willing to pay ridiculous valuations for some companies out there,\u201d Bright Trading LLC trader Dennis Dick told Reuters.<\/p>\n

The Dow has finished lower for four consecutive weeks, while the S&P 500 has experienced three straight weeks of declines.<\/p>\n

Major indices have been under pressure as Wall Street reacts to global uncertainty, including mounting fears of a global economic slowdown, new COVID-19 lockdowns in China, surging US inflation and the escalating Russian invasion of Ukraine.<\/p>\n

Several blue chip tech firms are set to report results this week.AP <\/p>\n

Monday\u2019s trading volatility unfolded as closely-watched blue-chip tech companies prepare to report earnings. Apple, Amazon, Google parent Alphabet, Facebook parent Meta and Microsoft all will provide their latest quarterly results this week.<\/p>\n

Last week, Netflix\u2019s revelation that it lost 200,000 subscribers spooked the broader tech market as investors reacted to the possibility that pandemic-era boom times may be coming to an end.<\/p>\n

The CBOE Volatility index, a metric known as Wall Street\u2019s \u201cfear gauge,\u201d briefly jumped above 31 points \u2013 its highest level since mid-March.<\/p>\n

The possibility of more lockdowns in China also prompted a selloff on local markets. Hong Kong\u2019s Hang Seng fell 3.7%, while the Shanghai Composite fell 5.1%.<\/p>\n

\u201cChina lockdowns are getting worse. It slows general economic growth and also creates supply chain issues that will continue to make inflation bad and lower earnings growth in the United States,\u201d Christopher Grisanti, chief equity strategist at MAI Capital Management in New York, told Reuters.<\/p>\n
