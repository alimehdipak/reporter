<\/p>\n

\u201cMurder\u201d in the metaverse isn\u2019t a 25-to-life prison sentence \u2014 or even a felony \u2014 but it could be a crime, some law experts believe.<\/p>\n

The Sun spoke to two lawyers, who have written about crime in the metaverse, and a former Manhattan prosecutor turned law professor about violence in the virtual world and if they can be prosecuted.<\/p>\n

Two of the three experts said violent crimes like murder, rape or assault in the metaverse can arguably be speech-related charges like menacing, harassment or stalking.<\/p>\n

It boils down to the wording of the laws as they\u2019re currently written, according to the experts.<\/p>\n

They\u2019re written to protect \u201creal, living people,\u201d said John Bandler, who teaches cyber security and cyber crime at New York\u2019s\u00a0Elisabeth Haub School of Law at Pace University.<\/p>\n

The law isn\u2019t meant to protect avatars or software codes, which populate the metaverse.<\/p>\n

\u201cI would view it more like speech or expression; less as a physical act against a person,\u201d Bandler said.<\/p>\n

\u201cThen we can analyze whether that speech or expression is permissible, protected, or not.\u201d<\/p>\n

That argument feeds into the larger societal First Amendment debate about what speech is protected, what isn\u2019t and what can be prosecuted.<\/p>\n

\u201cAll the trolling, virtual bullying, threats and bad behavior online happens all the time. It\u2019s nothing new, and it\u2019ll happen in the metaverse,\u201d said Greg Pryor, a lawyer at the\u00a0lawfirm Reed Smith LLP.<\/p>\n

\u201cBut if I say something racist or abuse someone based on their race or religion or sexuality, then you can potentially be prosecuted.\u201d<\/p>\n

Laws against criminal acts protect \u201creal, living people,\u201d not avatars, which populate the metaverse, one expert said.Vittorio Zunino Celotto\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

A third expert \u2013 Patrick Roberts, of the\u00a0Roberts Law Group\u00a0\u2013 said it would be difficult to prosecute a typically-anonymous user and prove the user committed the act.<\/p>\n

Consequences will likely be some sort of virtual punishment, such as a user\u2019s avatar being deacativated or restricted, he said.<\/p>\n

\u201cAnd the person who used the avatar for virtual violence might be restricted or prevented from access for a time, maybe,\u201d the North Carolina lawyer said.<\/p>\n

\u201cThis is all conjecture and has free speech implications. After all, people kill each other in video games all the time without consequences. I can\u2019t imagine a real world penal consequences for virtual crime.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cI don\u2019t think the criminal laws should be amended to protect avatars as people,\u201d according to one expert.Vittorio Zunino Celotto\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

Will avatar get \u2018personhood?\u2019<\/h2>\n

This question split the experts who spoke to The Sun throughout the last week<\/p>\n

Bandler, who has an extensive history and knowledge with cyber crimes, said protecting avatars through criminal law \u201ccould not work.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cI don\u2019t think the criminal laws should be amended to protect avatars as people. It would not make sense, and we have enough challenges just protecting people,\u201d Bandler said.<\/p>\n

A virtual reality headset is one of the ways users can access the metaverse.Shutterstock <\/p>\n

\u201cOnline gaming means thousands (millions) of avatars are \u2018injured\u2019 or \u2018killed\u2019 daily. Indeed, such acts are either \u2018part of the game\u2019 or at least allowed by the game.\u201d<\/p>\n

Even now, very few digital harassment crimes or threats on the internet are prosecuted, according to Bandler.<\/p>\n

\u201cEvery case is individualized, but a lot of threats get made and criminal enforcement isn\u2019t frequent,\u201d he said. \u201cI can\u2019t imagine threats in the metaverse will get a lot of traction with law enforcement.<\/p>\n

The metaverse has untapped potential and an unknown amount of criminal acts.Vittorio Zunino Celotto\/Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cYou can try to report them to the FBI, but good luck. The main recourse is through the platform.\u201d<\/p>\n

On the flip side, Pryor and Roberts said they can envision a future where laws are amended or new laws are created to reflect potential violence in the metaverse.<\/p>\n

\u201cCould the law give greater protection to avatars because they\u2019re like our personal persona? Could the law extend protection? Yeah, I think potentially. But that\u2019s not how it is right now,\u201d Pryor said.<\/p>\n
