<\/p>\n

In Ukraine, where city dwellers are often scrambling for cover, the nation\u2019s biggest delivery app has resumed service.<\/p>\n

The 2,000 plus couriers who have signed up to work for Glovo in the war-torn country have fanned out now to 23 cities, where they deliver groceries, restaurant meals and prescriptions to people who are often hurting for basic necessities.<\/p>\n

Glovo says the couriers \u2014 whose signature backpacks caused a stir on social media when one was photographed full of assault rifles \u2014 want to work despite the dangers: They need the money and say they\u2019re proud to be delivering the things of everyday life to people in need. <\/p>\n

The Barcelona-based company, meanwhile, says it\u2019s now operating its Ukraine routes as a nonprofit.<\/p>\n

It\u2019s a service that concerned family members and friends are using from afar: Last week, Stephan Soroka, a Ukrainian who has been living in Prague since the war started, says he sent his girlfriend\u2019s family some juice, chocolates and snacks.<\/p>\n

Some 2,050 couriers in Ukraine are making deliveries.SOPA Images\/LightRocket via Getty Images <\/p>\n

It took just 40 minutes from the time Soroka placed the order to when the Glovo courier arrived, Soroka said. Glovo is not exactly promoting the service for faraway orders because it can\u2019t guarantee delivery times \u2014 and Soroka confirmed it took him several tries to get his order placed.<\/p>\n

Still, he said, \u201cduring a war when the city is under constant threat of rocket attacks, it\u2019s amazing to have an opportunity to get the necessities for your loved one.\u201d<\/p>\n

The Glovo app notified Soroka, who\u2019s the chief business development officer for wearyourbrand.org \u2014 a provider of delivery gear \u2014 that the courier had arrived and he told his girlfriend\u2019s brother to meet the courier at the entrance of the apartment building.<\/p>\n

Stephan Soroka was able to order food for loved ones in Kyiv using fast delivery app, Glovo.Stephan Soroka <\/p>\n

The courier, according to Soroka, said he makes about 10 deliveries a day that are interrupted by air raid sirens and frequent checkpoints by authorities. Glovo, the courier said, has been \u201cpaying well\u201d but that it\u2019s a \u201cdangerous\u201d job. <\/p>\n

\u201cMy girlfriend has seen couriers wearing bullet proof vests,\u201d Soroka said.\u00a0<\/p>\n

The company halted its operation in Ukraine on the day the war started on Feb. 24, but just two days later it dispatched hundreds of couriers in the safest cities at that time in the west of Ukraine, including Lviv, Drigibych, Chernivtsi, Uzhorod and Truskavets.<\/p>\n

In the first week of March, Glovo couriers completed 10,000 deliveries in Ukraine.<\/p>\n

Glovo\u2019s couriers said they needed to work, a company spokesperson, Kasia Kosior, told The Post in an email \u2014 and some were already.<\/p>\n

Glovo is now delivering in 23 Ukrainian cities.SOPA Images\/LightRocket via Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cWe decided to reopen cities so they can use our technology,\u201d Glovo spokeswoman Kasia Kosior wrote in an email, noting that some couriers were already volunteering their services to ad-hoc groups that had sprouted up to keep supplies flowing to citizens.<\/p>\n

\u201cWe can offer secure connection, better coordination, safety protocols, insurance, and they get paid while doing what they want to do [in additions to getting] access to food for themselves and families,\u201d Kosior said.<\/p>\n

Glovo, which was acquired in January by German giant Delivery Hero, is waiving the fees and the commissions it normally charges businesses. Consumers pay a fee that is donated to a Ukrainian nonprofit, Come Back Alive. \u201cWe have stopped thinking of Glovo as [a] business,\u201d Kosior said. <\/p>\n

Glovo, which was acquired in January by German giant Delivery Hero, is waiving the fees and the commissions it normally charges businesses.NurPhoto via Getty Images <\/p>\n

The couriers are receiving \u201chigher bonuses\u201d Kosior said. \u201cThey are the heroes on the street,\u201d she added. They also receive local and international medical insurance \u201cin case of any accident,\u201d she added.<\/p>\n

And when air raid sirens go off, the app is immediately turned off.<\/p>\n

The decision to relaunch in Kyiv on March 9 was complicated by a lack of supplies to deliver and a dearth of couriers, Dmitry Rasnovsky, general manager of Glovo, said recently in a podcast interview \u201cFriday Takeaway.\u201d<\/p>\n
