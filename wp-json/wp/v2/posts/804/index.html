<\/p>\n

Genetically modified salad could be crucial to keeping the bones of humans on Mars healthy.<\/p>\n

This is according to researchers from the University of California who have added a human gene to regular lettuce.<\/p>\n

The gene-edited lettuce could help astronauts avoid osteoporosis, a disease which thins and weakens the bones so much that they can break.<\/p>\n

Scientists say it can be grown in space from mini seeds.<\/p>\n

The human gene incorporated into the lettuce allows it to produce a hormone that helps with healthy bone formation.<\/p>\n

Weak bones are a problem humans will face in space because living in microgravity environments leads to a loss of bone mass.<\/p>\n

Scientists were able to take regular lettuce genes and modify them so they can produce the parathyroid hormone (PTH).<\/p>\n

Somen Nandi, of the University of California at Davis, said: \u201cAstronauts can carry transgenic (GM) seeds, which are very tiny \u2014 you can have a few thousand seeds in a vial about the size of your thumb \u2014 and grow them just like regular lettuce.<\/p>\n

\u201cThey could use the plants to synthesize pharmaceuticals, such as PTH, on an as-required basis and then eat the plants.\u201d<\/p>\n

NASA plans to grow food on future spacecraft and on other planets as a food supplement for astronauts.NASA <\/p>\n

Astronauts have proven that they can grow lettuce on the International Space Station so growing lettuce on Mars could be possible.<\/p>\n

Space agencies\u00a0are well aware\u00a0that time in space can weaken human bones and there\u2019s concern this could prevent long distance space travel.<\/p>\n

The Nasa website\u00a0explains: \u201cNasa has learned that without Earth\u2019s gravity affecting the human body, weight-bearing bones lose on average\u00a01% to 1.5%\u00a0of mineral density per month during spaceflight.<\/p>\n

Astronauts on the International Space Station are ready to sample their harvest of a crop of \u201cOutredgeous\u201d red romaine lettuce from the Veggie plant growth system that tests hardware for growing vegetables and other plants in space.NASA <\/p>\n

\u201cAfter returning to Earth, bone loss might not be completely corrected by rehabilitation; however, their risk for fracture is not higher.\u201d<\/p>\n

There\u2019s concern that bone loss could be worse on longer missions, like those planned for Mars.<\/p>\n

It could take 10 months to get to Mars and astronauts would need to do special exercises and take supplements to make sure their bones don\u2019t weaken.<\/p>\n

NASA\u2019s Matt Romeyn in the Veggie Lab of the Space Station Processing Facility at the agency\u2019s Kennedy Space Center in Florida.Cory Huston\/NASA The first growth test of crops in the Advanced Plant Habitat aboard the International Space Station yielded great results.NASA <\/p>\n

A trip there and back with a landing stop could take about three years.<\/p>\n

To counteract the negative microgravity effects, astronauts would need to eat a lot of the salad.<\/p>\n

The researchers think they\u2019d need 380 grams, or about 8 cups, per day.<\/p>\n

As for the flavor of the space lettuce, no one is sure yet because it hasn\u2019t been deemed safe to try.<\/p>\n

Researchers hope to conduct more experiments with it and see if it can also help people with brittle bones here on Earth.<\/p>\n
