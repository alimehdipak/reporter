Oscar winner Ariana DeBose earned her place at the table \u2014 and\u00a0booth\u00a0\u2014 in one of the Theater District\u2019s hottest celebrity\u00a0haunts.<\/p>\n

DeBose, 31, who scored the Best Supporting Actress award for playing Anita in \u201cWest Side Story,\u201d will get a piece of Bond 45 named after her\u00a0\u2014\u00a0\u00a0booth\u00a0M42 in the VIP section.<\/p>\n

\u201cShe probably came once a week while Donna Summer ran down the street at the Lunt-Fontanne Theatre \u2026 she was part of the family,\u201d said Ben Grossman, CEO of the Fireman Hospitality Group, which includes Bond 45 and a list of other notable Big Apple eateries such as the Brooklyn Diner, Caf\u00e9 Fiorello, Trattoria Dell\u2019Arte and the Redeye Grille.<\/p>\n

The name plaque is a nod to DeBose\u2019s loyal patronage \u2014 she was a regular back in 2017 after her nights performing in\u00a0\u201cSummer: The Donna Summer Musical\u201d \u2014 and was already being planned before she got her Oscar nomination. <\/p>\n

DeBose nabbed the Oscar for her role as Anita in \u201cWest Side Story.\u201dAFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Five years ago, DeBose could be found at the restaurant\u2019s\u00a0Donna Summer After Parties, which were held nightly, \u201cdancing, drinking and trying to promote the show.\u201d \u201cShe was a big fan of the Prosecco,\u201d Grossman said.<\/p>\n

DeBose\u2019s performance was as sparkling as her favorite drink \u2014 her Donna Summer earned her a Tony nomination. \u00a0Even back then, the actress, a North Carolina native, who left her home to move to New York at 19, stood out amongst her peers.<\/p>\n

\u201cShe was always the first person to make sure that her table had the food and everything they needed,\u201d\u00a0said the hospitality group\u2019s\u00a0marketing manager, Beth Tuminello.<\/p>\n

Five years ago, DeBose could be found at the restaurant\u2019s Donna Summer After Parties, which were held nightly.Helayne Seidman <\/p>\n

Restaurant group founder and CEO, Shelly Fireman\u00a0\u2014\u00a0who has been in the New York restaurant business since 1963\u00a0\u2014\u00a0has a\u00a0long-standing tradition of naming\u00a0booths\u00a0after his famous patrons.<\/p>\n

\u201cEveryone likes their names in lights \u2026 It makes people come back. If they know they have their names up, they bring their friends and family, their grandmothers to show them that they\u2019re stars,\u201d Fireman said.<\/p>\n

The Bronx native-turned Upper West Side restaurateur began the\u00a0booth\u00a0naming at Caf\u00e9 Fiorello\u00a0\u2014\u00a0which he lives above\u00a0\u2014\u00a0in the early \u201880s. The custom then spread to the Brooklyn Diner in the \u201890s and Bond 45 in the early \u201800s. \u00a0<\/p>\n

Shelly Fireman \u2014 who has been in the New York restaurant business since 1963 \u2014 has a tradition of naming booths after his famous patrons.Michael Berman <\/p>\n

Throughout his close to 60-year reign over his empire of dining landmarks in the Big Apple, Fireman has witnessed some memorable moments surrounding the plaques. \u201cI\u2019ll tell you the one that got most stolen, Monica Lewinsky,\u201d he said. \u201cI swear to you, it was the craziest \u2026 We put up maybe 10.\u201d<\/p>\n

And the\u00a0most photographed is the Joy and Regis Philbin plaque at Caf\u00e9 Fiorello. \u201cHe was a true treasure and Fiorello regular diner,\u201d Grossman said.<\/p>\n

Given her meteoric\u00a0rise, DeBose\u2019s plaque will be larger than originally intended when it\u2019s unveiled on April 15.<\/p>\n
