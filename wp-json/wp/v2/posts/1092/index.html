Elon Musk has slammed the White House\u2019s plan to impose a \u201cbillionaires\u2019 tax\u201d on the wealthiest Americans \u2013 claiming that his companies likely wouldn\u2019t have survived if the levy had been in place following the 2008 financial crisis.<\/p>\n

The so-called \u201cBillionaire Minimum Income Tax\u201d included in Biden\u2019s fiscal 2023 budget proposal would implement a 20% minimum tax on US households worth more than $100 million. The plan would target both stated income and unrealized capital gains.<\/p>\n

Musk responded a blog post by American Enterprise Institute fellow James Pethokoukis, which detailed University of Chicago economist\u00a0Steven Kaplan\u2019s argument that cash-strapped Tesla and SpaceX could have collapsed in 2008 if the billionaire had exited PayPal with $60 million less due to a wealth tax.<\/p>\n

\u201cGood point. SpaceX & Tesla would probably have died, since both narrowly escaped bankruptcy in 2008,\u201d Musk tweeted in response to the post.<\/p>\n

Musk has plenty of incentive to speak out against a billionaire\u2019s tax. The Tesla founder is the world\u2019s richest individual, with a personal net worth estimated to exceed $290 billion as of Wednesday.<\/p>\n

By one estimate, Musk would pay an additional $50 billion in taxes under the latest Biden proposal.<\/p>\n

Musk has described 2008 as \u201cdefinitely the worst year of my life\u201d as he struggled to keep Tesla and SpaceX afloat while going through a divorce. He invested all of the $180 million in his proceeds from the sale PayPal into the firms.<\/p>\n

President Biden and other proponents of a billionaire\u2019s tax \u2013 including frequent Musk foe Sen. Elizabeth Warren (D-Mass.) \u2013 argue it is necessary to ensure all Americans are paying their fair share in taxes. The Biden administration has also floated a wealth tax as a key source of revenue to pay for its expansive slate of social spending programs.<\/p>\n
