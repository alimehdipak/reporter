On her viral hit \u201cabcdefu,\u201d 17-year-old TikTok sensation Gayle launches an F-bomb tirade against a shady ex: \u201cF\u2014k you and your friends that I\u2019ll never see again\/Everybody but your dog, you can all f\u2014k off.\u201d<\/p>\n

But she was worried that her foul-mouthed rant would piss off her grandmother. \u201cWhen I played that song for my grandmother, I was so nervous,\u201d Gayle told The Post. \u201cAnd, you know, she told me that I got to say all the things at 17 that she never could. And that really put things into a different perspective [about] a woman being outspokenly angry at a man.\u201d<\/p>\n

After getting Grandma\u2019s seal of approval, \u201cabcdefu\u201d has become a cathartic, liberating anthem for fed-up young women everywhere. The pop-punk single is No. 3 on this week\u2019s Billboard Hot 100, building buzz for Gayle\u2019s debut EP, \u201cA Study of the Human Experience Volume One,\u201d which arrives March 18. And just a few days later, the Dallas-born, Nashville, Tenn.-based singer \u2014 whose full name is Taylor Gayle Rutherford \u2014 will play two sold-out shows at Mercury Lounge on March 21 and 22.<\/p>\n

Gayle has hit No. 3 on the Billboard Hot 100 with her viral single \u201cabcdefu.\u201dAcacia Evans <\/p>\n

The fiery rage in \u201cabcdefu\u201d was fueled by Gayle\u2019s pent-up emotions after a real-life breakup. \u201cI went through this relationship where I never let myself get angry. Even if they didn\u2019t treat me well, I would ignore it and only focus on the good parts because I was committed to that person,\u201d she said. \u201cAnd then we broke up, and I was trying really hard to, like, be friends with him. I actually thought we got to a decent spot.\u201d<\/p>\n

But after Gayle found out that her ex was \u201ctalking s\u2014t about me,\u201d she was done playing nice.<\/p>\n

\u201cI was pissed,\u201d she said. \u201cIt all just came out at once. And it kind of came out in an extreme way, like, \u2018F\u2014k you, and your mom, and your sister, and your job.\u2019 Because there\u2019s so many things I never told him I hated \u2026\u00a0All bets were off.\u201d<\/p>\n

Gayle will play two sold-out shows at Mercury Lounge on March 21 and 22.David Livingston <\/p>\n

Growing up, Gayle was a fan of CeeLo Green\u2019s 2010 hit \u201cF\u2014k You\u201d \u2014 or at least the \u201cForget You\u201d censored version. \u201cI actually remember I wanted to sing that song for a talent show,\u201d she said. \u201cI think I was probably around 9 or 10. My mom was like, \u2018No! You cannot do that!\u2019 But with \u2018abc,\u2019 I honestly \u2026 wasn\u2019t particularly thinking about that song.\u201d<\/p>\n

While she started out singing country music, Gayle\u2019s childhood hero was the Queen of Soul. \u201cThe person who inspired me to do music in the first place was Aretha Franklin,\u201d she said. \u201cI [discovered] her when I was around 7, and I just got hooked. I mean, I just loved her soul.\u201d<\/p>\n

Later, Gayle discovered female rockers such as Joan Jett and Courtney Love, but it was another influential woman \u2014 hit-making songwriter and former \u201cAmerican Idol\u201d judge Kara DioGuardi \u2014 who would prove instrumental in her development.<\/p>\n

\u201cI would not be the writer I am without her,\u201d said Gayle. \u201cShe has mentored me and my songwriting, and really inspired me to dig deeper and be more vulnerable in my songs and really fight for my artistry \u2026 It was really great to have a powerful, badass woman helping me and inspiring me.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cThat\u2019s just the reality of teenagers,\u201d she said. \u201cTo acknowledge it helps people feel less alone.\u201d<\/p>\n
