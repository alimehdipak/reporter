BOULDER, Colo. \u2013\u00a0The ambitions to return to the moon and beyond over the next several decades means astronauts will be dealing with problematic dust, but NASA hopes new technology will zap away any issues previously faced by space explorers.<\/p>\n

Scientists in Colorado have developed what they call a \u201cMoon Duster\u201d that will help clear the dust from surfaces that attract the unwanted particles.<\/p>\n

Lunar dust caused significant issues for the Apollo missions, and NASA says it provided challenges from everything from causing damage to technology to impacting astronauts\u2019 health.<\/p>\n

An Apollo crew who landed on the moon noted at the time dust particles, which are known as regoliths, stuck to everything from spacesuits to lenses.<\/p>\n

Humans\u2019 interactions with the celestial bodies can stir up the particles much like a meteor strike.<\/p>\n

But NASA says, unlike on Earth, the dust is electrically charged, making it more susceptible to sticking on surfaces.<\/p>\n

University of Colorado Boulder\/NASA Solar System Exploration Research Virtual Institute testing the new electron beam technology.NASA <\/p>\n

Previously, experts have tried low-tech methods for cleaning the suits, but the stubborn dust has remained.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe Moon duster will be like a typical duster spray on your desk, but it operates without air,\u201d Dr. Inseob Hahn, a program technologist and the project lead at NASA\/JPL, said in a statement.<\/p>\n

Scientists say the \u201cMoon Duster\u201d emits an electron beam causing the unwanted particles to develop a negative charge, leading to them to fall off the surface.<\/p>\n

So far, in the laboratory, scientists have observed this technique working on spacesuits, glass and solar panels, with the efficiency of removing between 75 to 85 percent of the dust.<\/p>\n

NASA hopes to have the technology in place by the first\u00a0Artemis mission\u00a0to the moon in 2025 and says it\u2019ll be an asset for when there is a sustained lunar presence.\u00a0<\/p>\n

If the Artemis program is successful with returning astronauts to the moon, the agency hopes to have human exploration of Mars by 2040.<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
