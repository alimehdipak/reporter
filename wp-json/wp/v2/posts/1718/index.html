AMC\u2019s new legal drama, \u201c61st Street,\u201d revolves around a black high school track star, Moses (Tosin Cole), whose promising future is derailed when he\u2019s arrested as an alleged gang member.<\/p>\n

The series, airing Sundays at 10 p.m., is executive-produced by Michael. B. Jordan.<\/p>\n

\u201cI liked the character [and] the story, which is so complex,\u201d Cole, 29, told The Post. \u201cEspecially getting different sides of the story, I felt like it\u2019s a well-rounded conversation. And getting to work alongside great people like Courtney B. Vance and [creator Peter Moffat] felt like a good environment to be in.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

The series, filmed on location in Chicago, begins with a drug bust that kills Rufus (Kevin Tre\u2019von Patterson), a black teenager, and police officer Mike Rossi (Patrick Mulvey), who was investigating corruption. Moses, who\u2019s bound for a college scholarship, is in the wrong place at the wrong time, and quickly gets blamed for Officer Rossi\u2019s death. Franklin Roberts (Vance), a veteran public defender with a complicated personal life, takes on Moses\u2019 case. Meanwhile, it doesn\u2019t help the situation that the police chief, Brannigan (Holt McCallany, \u201cMindhunter,\u201d) is corrupt and pulling the strings.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Tosin Cole as Moses Johnson, a high school track star, in \u201c61st Street.\u201d George Burns\/AMC Coach Angelo (Sammy A. Publes) and Moses Johnson (Tosin Cole) stretch out in \u201c61st Street.\u201d George Burns\/AMC <\/p>\n

\u00a0\u201cCourtney [Vance] is such a gentleman,\u201d said Cole, who is British and best known for \u201cDoctor Who.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cHe\u2019s so honorable and peaceful, but a great leader at the same time. He makes everyone feel comfortable, and he sets the tone for a good environment to work in. Everyone just kind of followed suit.\u201d Cole added that he didn\u2019t interact closely with Jordan, because, \u201cHe was busy doing Michael B. Jordan stuff.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Cole, who\u2019s based in London, said that he could relate to many aspects of Moses\u2019s life.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cBeing a son, being a brother, trying to stay out of trouble, having to focus. And, the foundation of what it is to be a \u2018good kid.\u2019 Obviously, you have situations where you might have had interactions with the police, and [feel] what\u2019s it like to be racially profiled. But for the most part, I\u2019ve never been in this situation,\u201d he said. \u201cGoing through these raw emotions, having your world crumble, and being in this world that you never wanted to be in was vulnerable. That was difficult to tap into that every day. Every episode, there\u2019s something new.\u201d<\/p>\n

He said that even though he grew up in another country, Moses\u2019 experience didn\u2019t feel foreign to him.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cLittle cultural nuances might be different here and there, but I feel like the foundation of life is universal. It\u2019s hard to have your world snatched up from you, and it\u2019s hard to beat the impossible,\u201d he said. \u201cI spent time in Chicago before, maybe in 2014 or 2015 \u2026 I was there for a couple months doing a pilot. I\u2019ve got family out there as well. It\u2019s a great city. Great food, great community, everyone is proud to be from there.\u201d<\/p>\n
