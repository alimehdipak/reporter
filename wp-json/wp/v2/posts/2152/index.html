Outraged liberals were fleeing Twitter left and right Tuesday in\u00a0the wake of Elon Musk\u2019s $44 billion takeover\u00a0\u2014 as prominent progressives claimed the social-media platform would become a \u201chellhole\u201d with the billionaire Tesla and SpaceX CEO at the helm.<\/p>\n

Black Lives Matter activist Shaun King\u00a0appeared to have quit the platform\u00a0after labeling the billionaire\u2019s purchase a show of \u201cwhite power\u201d \u2014 only to resurface early Tuesday to lash out at conservatives,\u00a0including Donald Trump Jr.,\u00a0who were celebrating his absence.<\/p>\n

\u201cAt its root, Elon Musk wanting to purchase Twitter is not about left vs right. It\u2019s about white power,\u201d King had tweeted before Twitter\u2019s board of directors\u00a0accepted the billion-dollar bid.<\/p>\n

\u201cThe man was raised in Apartheid by a white nationalist. He\u2019s upset that Twitter won\u2019t allow white nationalists to target\/harass people. That\u2019s his definition of free speech.\u201d<\/p>\n

King, who claimed he never deleted his account, only tightened security measures, clapped back at Trump Jr. after the former first son quipped that the activist\u2019s absence from Twitter would alone be worth $44 billion.<\/p>\n

Black Lives Matter activist Shaun King deleted his Twitter account to protest Elon Musk\u2019s imminent acquisition of the social media company.Getty Images Erik Larsen, a comic book artist who was one of the creators of \u201cThe Amazing Spider-Man,\u201d followed through with his vow to delete his Twitter account.Getty Images Warren called the deal \u201cdangerous for our democracy.\u201d AFP \/AFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

MSNBC anchor Joy Reid took aim at \u201cright wing trolls\u201d who she said were targeting the \u201cnormal, thoughtful people\u201d planning to delete Twitter once Musk\u2019s transaction is approved, saying: \u201cIf y\u2019all are so excited about this site turning back into a hellhole, well good on you.\u201d<\/p>\n

Sen. Elizabeth Warren also blasted Musk\u2019s planned takeover as \u201cdangerous for our democracy.\u201d<\/p>\n

Billionaires like Elon Musk play by a different set of rules than everyone else, accumulating power for their own gain,\u201d the Massachusetts Democrat railed. \u201cWe need a wealth tax and strong rules to hold Big Tech accountable.\u201dAnd \u201cThe View\u201d co-host Sunny Hostin used a portion of Tuesday\u2019s show to claim Musk\u2019s takeover would benefit \u201cpredominantly straight white men.\u201d using the social-media platform.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhen Elon Musk says, \u2018Wow, this is about free speech,\u2019 it seems to me that this is about free speech of straight white men, so let them have it,\u201d Hostin said on air. \u201cI think he is saying, \u2018You get to say whatever you want. I\u2019m going to take away the guardrails.\u2019 And for me, that is something that is a bit scary.\u201d<\/p>\n

A number of others in meltdown mode made dramatic announcements that they were leaving the platform, including\u00a0\u201cThe Good Place\u201d actress Jameela Jamil\u00a0and Erik Larsen, the comic book creator behind \u201cThe Amazing Spider-Man.\u201d<\/p>\n

Jameela Jamil was also quick to announce she was leaving the platform. Getty Images Twitter\u2019s board of directors on Monday accepted Musk\u2019s offer to buy the company at $54.20 a share.Bloomberg via Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cAh he got Twitter. I would like this to be my what lies here as my last tweet. Just really any excuse to show pics of Barold,\u201d Jamil wrote alongside photos of her dog.<\/p>\n

Meanwhile, Republican lawmakers lauded Musk\u2019s takeover as a win for free speech and democracy after the billionaire vowed to have less stringent content moderation policies on the platform.<\/p>\n

\u201cTwitter openly censors speech based on political ideologies and makes a mockery of our Constitutional liberties. If Elon Musk can turn that around to actually protect free speech and encourage open discourse, Twitter\u2019s full potential will be unlocked,\u201d Sen. Ted Cruz (R-Texas) said.<\/p>\n

Tennessee Sen. Marsha Blackburn also hailed the Tesla CEO\u2019s takeover, saying, \u201cElon Musk buying Twitter terrifies the left because they don\u2019t want their power to censor conservatives threatened.\u201d<\/p>\n

And Trump Jr. weighed in, accusing the left of \u201cexhibiting mass hysteria with the notion that others could also express their ideas on an equal footing.\u201d<\/p>\n

Fox News hosts Tucker Carlson and Mark Levin ended their months-long Twitter boycotts soon after Musk\u2019s deal was announced.<\/p>\n

Musk has vowed to take an \u201cfree speech absolutist\u201d approach to guiding the company.AFP via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Carlson, who had his account locked earlier this year for calling Rachel Levine, a transgender woman who is currently serving as the Biden administration\u2019s assistant secretary for health, a man, tweeted: \u201cWe\u2019re back.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cThanks to new ownership, I\u2019ve decided to come back!\u201d tweeted Levin, whose account was never limited or suspended.<\/p>\n

Rep. Marjorie Taylor Greene, who had her personal account permanently banned in January for spreading COVID misinformation, claimed that she would be reinstated by Musk.<\/p>\n

\u201cPrepare for blue check mark full scale meltdown after Elon Musk seals the deal and I should get my personal Twitter account restored,\u201d she tweeted from her congressional account.<\/p>\n

Off the back of Monday\u2019s announcement, Twitter was reportedly taking drastic steps to maintain smooth operations this week amid fears that disgruntled employees could try to disrupt the service, a source told Bloomberg.<\/p>\n

The platform blocked routine changes, such as product updates, through Friday \u2014 with alterations only possible if they\u2019re identified as \u201cbusiness critical\u201d and approved by an executive with a vice president title or higher.<\/p>\n

A ban on product changes is not unprecedented at Twitter, which takes similar action ahead of major events such as the Super Bowl to ensure there are no hiccups on its platform.<\/p>\n

Twitter reps did not return a request for comment on whether a specific concern prompted the policy.<\/p>\n
