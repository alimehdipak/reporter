A Silicon Valley venture capital firm is in the hot seat after asking job applicants for their scores on an IQ test \u2014 even though hiring based on IQ is legally questionable.\u00a0<\/p>\n

San Francisco-based Soma Capital, which has invested in startups including self-driving car company Cruise and defunct social media app HouseParty, posted a job listing for a \u201cMulti-Stage Investor Role\u201d earlier this month. <\/p>\n

The listing required applicants to go to a website called iqtest.com, take a free test and include their score.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Applicants were also told to take what the company said was a Myers-Briggs personality test on a website called 16personalities.com and share their personality type, which includes categories such as \u201cextraversion\u201d versus \u201cintroversion\u201d and \u201csensing\u201d versus \u201cintuition.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cAttach your test score and personality test results\u2019 screenshots here,\u201d Soma Capital\u2019s listing prompted.\u00a0The questions were mandatory.<\/p>\n

Soma Capital asked job applicants to take an IQ test. <\/p>\n

But after screenshots of the job listing circulated on Twitter on Monday, Soma Capital removed the questions.\u00a0<\/p>\n

In a phone call with The Post, Soma Capital\u2019s managing partner Aneel Ranadive blamed the job listing on an intern.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cBasically I just saw this post that an intern put up,\u201d Ranadive, who\u2019s the son of Sacramento Kings owner Vivek Ranadive, told The Post. \u201cI was horrified to see that. I had the team take it down immediately.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cThey\u2019re in trouble,\u201d Ranadive said of the intern, adding, \u201cI take responsibility for my team.\u201d \u00a0<\/p>\n

Under the Civil Rights Act of 1964, using IQ tests could violate the law if the tests are shown to have a disproportionate impact on racial minorities or women, and are job-related, New York University School of Law Professor Samuel Estreicher told The Post.<\/p>\n

Soma Capital required job applicants to include their IQ scores and what it said was a Myers-Briggs personality test, which the Myers-Briggs company later said wasn\u2019t legitimate. <\/p>\n

Estreicher added that the use of IQ and Myers-Briggs tests also risks violating the Americans with Disabilities Act, which bars companies from requiring applicants to take mental or physical examinations prior to offering them jobs.<\/p>\n

\u201cTo avoid legal risk,\u00a0companies shouldn\u2019t rely on these tests,\u201d Estreicher said. \u201cThey should just be talking\u00a0to\u00a0job applicants.\u201d<\/p>\n

Lydia Brown, a policy counsel at advocacy group the\u00a0Center for Democracy and Technology, said trying to quanitfy\u00a0an applicant\u2019s intelligence is a \u201d\u00a0rather slippery concept.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cEmployers need to really carefully consider whether their test is actually measuring a quality or trait that is necessary to perform the job \u2014 and that\u2019s a legal standard,\u201d Brown told The Post.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Ranadive said he was \u201chugely apologetic\u201d for the IQ and Myers-Briggs questions and that they are \u201cnot what Soma stands for.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cWe want mission-driven, thoughtful teammates from diverse backgrounds and I believe the best way to suss that is through writing and conversations,\u201d he said. \u201cThe most legendary entrepreneurs of all time come from unexpected, unique, diverse backgrounds.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n
