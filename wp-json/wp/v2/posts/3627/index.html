More than three decades since it was last printed, this iconic rock \u2018n\u2019 roll magazine has been born anew.\u00a0<\/p>\n

CREEM, launched out of a derelict Detroit bank in 1969, made a name for itself as the underground editorial project that could before suspending production in 1989. Now, 33 years later, \u201cAmerica\u2019s Only Rock \u2018N\u2019 Roll Magazine\u201d is back with a fresh print edition, as well as a newly available digital archive \u2014 and a bigger plan to become its own entertainment company.<\/p>\n

The revival is the passion product of JJ Kramer, the son of the magazine\u2019s founder, the late Barry Kramer.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cWhen CREEM ceased publication in 1989, it left a massive void that has never been filled, though many have tried,\u201d JJ told The Post. \u201cThat\u2019s why CREEM\u2019s return is so significant; its unapologetic and wickedly funny sensibilities have been sorely missed by fans and bands alike. As the news has started to spread, the response has been nothing short of tremendous.\u201d<\/p>\n

Dave Marsh, Barry Kramer and Lester Bangs in the early days of CREEM. Charlie Auringer \t \t <\/p>\n

Subscriptions for CREEM 2.0 launched online June 1, with the first quarterly issue set to drop in the fall. The resurrected countercultural brand promises to bring its new readers content from \u201ccontributors ranging from established writers & photographers to up-and-coming new voices, and even random meme lords found online\u201d in its resurrected, subscription-only print magazines, according to a press release.\u00a0<\/p>\n

As for the digital archive of 228 issues and 69,000 photos, it features \u201cdefinitive\u201d writings by the likes of Lester Bangs, Cameron Crowe, Patti Smith, Greil Marcus, Dave Marsh, Robert Christgau and Jaan Uhelszki \u2014 all \u201cavailable for the first time over 30 years.\u201d The archive also still embraces the mag\u2019s memorable mascot, \u201cBoy Howdy!\u201d \u2014 designed by underground artist Robert Crumb.<\/p>\n

The archive is publicly accessible, with a 30-day free trial offer through August, after which time it\u2019ll be free with print subscriptions.<\/p>\n

A spread from CREEM\u2019s September 1981 issue.Courtesy CREEM <\/p>\n

And looking forward, there\u2019s a broader plan in place to launch a media empire in the brand\u2019s name, complete with concerts, podcasts and perhaps a TV show, the Detroit Free Press reported.\u00a0<\/p>\n<\/p>\n

The push to relaunch this year was significantly inspired by a 2020 documentary about the Detroit-born mag that, at its peak, had a circulation of over 250,000 \u2014 a number second in the nation\u2019s music publications only to Rolling Stone, according to the release.\u00a0<\/p>\n

A staff photo of the original CREEM team. Richard Lee <\/p>\n

\u201cCREEM was the one place where I felt like I was part of something bigger than myself,\u201d CREEM editor Jaan Uhelszki told Rolling Stone following news of the relaunch. \u201cAll of the early staffers serendipitously finding our way to a busted three-story old bank building in downtown Detroit, like we were characters in \u2018Close Encounters of the Third Kind,\u2019 needing to come together to understand why we were sculpting our mashed potatoes into the dirigible on the cover of Led Zeppelin \u2014 being obsessed by a five-note musical sequence. Not even the same one.\u201d<\/p>\n
