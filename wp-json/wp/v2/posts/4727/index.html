The Beatles famously sang about \u201cLucy in the Sky With Diamonds.\u201d But for one of the Fab Four, it was George in the Back Room with Rubies at the iconic New York rock club Max\u2019s Kansas City.<\/p>\n

The new documentary \u201cNightclubbing: The Birth of Punk Rock in NYC\u201d \u2014 which will be screening at Joe\u2019s Pub on Tuesday, Wednesday and July 30 \u2014 reveals that George Harrison had a gem of a way of picking up chicks in the VIP back room of Max\u2019s.<\/p>\n

\u201cHe always carried a little pouch of rubies,\u201d recalled Max\u2019s legend Alice Cooper in the doc. \u201cAnd once he decided which one he wanted to be with, then he would put the ruby down in front of her. And if she picked up the ruby, that was a done deal then.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cNightclubbing\u201d \u2014\u00a0which premiered at the Dock of the Bay Film Festival in San Sebasti\u00e1n, Spain,\u00a0 in May \u2014 takes you inside the sex, drugs and rock \u2019n\u2019 roll that went down at Max\u2019s Kansas City in the nascent thrashing of the punk-rock movement with NYC-bred acts such as the Velvet Underground, the New York Dolls and the Ramones. Not just a punk mecca, it was also the place where the likes of Bruce Springsteen and Aerosmith were signed by Clive Davis, and where Debbie Harry served new-wave attitude as a waitress before forming Blondie.<\/p>\n

Debbie Harry worked as a waitress at Max\u2019s Kansas City before forming Blondie.Getty Images <\/p>\n

\u201cIt was the cool factor,\u201d said Cooper. \u201cThere was a lot of other little places to play, but Max\u2019s was the place. It was the cool place.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cIt was one of the homes of punk,\u201d added Billy Idol. \u201cThat\u2019s what it was for us.\u201d<\/p>\n

Restaurateur Mickey Ruskin opened Max\u2019s Kansas City at 213 Park Ave. South in December 1965 \u2014 eight years before the more-heralded CBGB. With a restaurant in the front and a back room for VIPs, it turned into a hipster hangout when Andy Warhol became a regular along with scenesters from his nearby studio, the Factory.<\/p>\n

Billy Idol performed on the closing night of Max\u2019s Kansas City in 1981.Redferns <\/p>\n

Models and movie stars \u2014 from Warren Beatty and Jane Fonda to Jack Nicholson and Elizabeth Taylor\u2014 would mix and mingle in the buzzy back room. \u201cIt was a great place to hang out,\u201d said Cooper. \u201cThere was nobody there trying to get your autograph, \u2019cause everybody was bigger than you.\u201d<\/p>\n

Max\u2019s added live music in 1969, featuring acts such as the Stooges. Cooper recalls one particularly piercing performance by Stooges frontman Iggy Pop, who, bloodied from rolling around on glass onstage, had to get 16 stitches afterward. \u201cI think [Stooges manager] Danny Fields and I were the ones who took him to the hospital,\u201d he said.<\/p>\n

Max\u2019s Kansas City was open from 1965 to 1981 at 213 Park Ave. South.Ray Stevenson\/REX USA Restaurateur Mickey Ruskin opened Max\u2019s Kansas City in December 1965.Getty Images <\/p>\n

It was also at Max\u2019s where Iggy Pop met David Bowie, sparking their renowned creative collaboration, while Ziggy Stardust also drew inspiration from Cooper and his eponymous group for his Spiders from Mars backing band.\u00a0<\/p>\n

As Max\u2019s became more of a proper live music venue with its upstairs room in the early \u201970s, there was plenty of debauchery. It wasn\u2019t uncommon to find people having sex in the bathroom or shooting amphetamine into their butt.<\/p>\n

David Bowie met his creative collaborator Iggy Pop at Max\u2019s Kansas City.Redferns <\/p>\n

\u201cIt was all about sex and drugs,\u201d said trans punk-rocker Jayne County.<\/p>\n

County also recalled Dee Dee Ramone revealing his scarred penis after he was attacked during an altercation with his girlfriend. \u201cShe freaked out and tried to stab Dee Dee in the c\u2013k, and tried to cut his c\u2013k off,\u201d she said. \u201cAnd he had scars on his c\u2013k.\u201d<\/p>\n

David Johansen and the New York Dolls helped lead the punk-rock movement at Max\u2019s Kansas City.Redferns <\/p>\n

\u201cNightclubbing\u201d also recounts how Sid Vicious played his last show at Max\u2019s in 1978, 12 days before his girlfriend Nancy Spungen was found dead at the Chelsea Hotel. Vicious was arrested for her murder but never went to trial, because he died from a heroin overdose while out on bail.<\/p>\n
