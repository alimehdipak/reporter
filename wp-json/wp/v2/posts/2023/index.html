<\/p>\n

A top scientist is plotting a mission to find what he believes is alien technology lying at the bottom of the Pacific Ocean.<\/p>\n

Controversial astrophysicist Avi Loeb believes an interstellar object that crash-landed on Earth in 2014 was some form of spacecraft.<\/p>\n

A US Space Command (USSC) report\u00a0released last week\u00a0confirmed that the object was from another star system.<\/p>\n

The agency concluded that the projectile \u2013 which streaked across the sky off the coast of Manus Island, Papua New Guinea \u2013 was a meteor.<\/p>\n

Prof. Loeb, however, is having none of it. He claimed on Wednesday that the object could have been built by extraterrestrials.<\/p>\n

\u201cOur discovery of an interstellar meteor heralds a new research frontier,\u201d the Harvard astronomer\u00a0wrote in an essay for\u00a0The Debrief.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cThe fundamental question is whether any interstellar meteor might indicate a composition that is unambiguously\u00a0artificial\u00a0in\u00a0origin.<\/p>\n

\u201cBetter still, perhaps some technological components would survive the impact.\u201d<\/p>\n

A US Space Command memo released last week confirmed that the object was an interstellar meteor, but Professor Loeb disagrees.Getty Images\/iStockphoto <\/p>\n

Prof. Loeb has spent decades studying astronomy and more recently has trained his sights on the possibility that life exists beyond Earth.<\/p>\n

His bold claims frequently make headlines and he has faced criticism from others in his field over his outlandish extraterrestrial theories.<\/p>\n

Working with a student at Harvard, Prof. Loeb was actually the astronomer who identified the object as interstellar a few years ago.<\/p>\n

The pair wrote a paper about it but were instructed not to publish it because they used classified government data for their research.<\/p>\n

Avi Loeb, the chair of Harvard University\u2019s Astronomy Department, poses for a portrait with the 15-inch telescope known as \u201cThe Great Refractor,\u201d which was installed in 1847, in Cambridge, MA on March 27, 2019.Boston Globe via Getty Images <\/p>\n

After the USSC confirmed their hunch on April 7, Loeb is calling for an expedition to find whatever\u2019s left of the object.<\/p>\n

In his essay, he noted that a retrieval expedition could be achieved using \u201cscooping\u201d magnets to explore the 10 square kilometer region of the Pacific Ocean where the object is thought to have landed.<\/p>\n

\u201cMy dream is to press some buttons on a functional piece of equipment that was manufactured outside of Earth,\u201d he added.<\/p>\n

The prolific astrophysicist is no stranger to controversy.<\/p>\n

He has produced provocative research on black holes, space radiation, the early universe and other topics of his field.<\/p>\n

Over the past decade, his focus has been trained on a more contentious topic: The possibility that Earth has been visited by extraterrestrials.<\/p>\n

Prof. Loeb has repeatedly claimed that Oumuamua \u2013 an interstellar object that zipped through the Solar System in 2017 was technology sent by aliens.<\/p>\n

He doubled down on the heavily contested comments \u2013 which earned him headlines across the globe \u2013 in a book published last year.<\/p>\n

\u201cWhat would happen if a caveman saw a cellphone?\u201d Loeb wrote. \u201cHe\u2019s seen rocks all his life, and he would have thought it was just a shiny rock.\u201d<\/p>\n

He took fault with astronomers who argued that the object was a comet, saying it was akin to letting \u201cthe familiar to define what we might discover.\u201d<\/p>\n

Many scientists have rubbished his outlandish claims, branding them cavalier and irresponsible.<\/p>\n

Loeb heads up the Galileo Project, which aims to establish a network of advanced telescopes that will scan the skies for signs of alien life.<\/p>\n
