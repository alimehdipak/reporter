<\/p>\n

For a while, it was always Sunny in Silicon Valley. <\/p>\n

From 2009 until 2016, Ramesh \u201cSunny\u201d Balwani \u2014 then the boyfriend of Elizabeth Holmes and president of her buzzy Theranos, Inc. \u2014 seemed to be everywhere in his puffy-sleeved shirts: traveling the world on behalf of the company, leaving a trail of cologne, tooling around in his Porsche with its DAZKAPTL vanity license plates (a reference to Karl Marx, naturally).<\/p>\n

In the coming weeks, though, America will learn more about Balwani than he ever intended to reveal. <\/p>\n

As a key player in the Theranos scandal, he is set to stand trial for his role in fraud and deception related to blood testing machines that never did what they were claimed to do. Like his former partner \u2014 who was found guilty on four counts of fraud in January and is out on $500,000 bail while awaiting sentencing \u00a0\u2014 he faces multiple counts of wire fraud. Jury selection is currently underway in San Jose, Calif.<\/p>\n

Sunny Balwani is set to stand trial for wire fraud charges related to Theranos blood testing machines that never did what they were claimed to do.REUTERS <\/p>\n

During her trial, Holmes claimed her ex was physically, emotionally and sexually abusive.<\/p>\n

\u201cHe told me that I didn\u2019t know what I was doing in business, that my convictions were wrong, that he was astonished at my mediocrity and if I followed my instincts I was going to fail,\u201d she testified. \u201cHe would force me to have sex with him when I didn\u2019t want to because he wanted me to know he still loved me.\u201d<\/p>\n

She also alleged under oath that he exerted high degrees of control over her. Balwani is said to have told Holmes what to eat, estranged her from her family, and put her on a schedule that had her working seven days per week. According to Slate, he snooped on her text messages and hurled \u201csharp, hard objects\u201d at her.<\/p>\n

\u201cMy take on Sunny is that he is a highly manipulative, bald-faced liar,\u201d a source close to the situation told The Post. \u201cHe has been caught dead-to-rights lying.\u201d<\/p>\n

Elizabeth Holmes, Balwani\u2019s former partner, claimed during her own trial that Balwani was physically, emotionally and sexually abusive.Getty Images <\/p>\n

The indictment against Balwani alleges that he defrauded doctors and patients, \u201cmaking false claims concerning Theranos\u2019s ability to provide accurate, fast, reliable and cheap blood tests and test results.\u201d<\/p>\n

Theranos\u2019s product, the so-called Edison machine, promised to deliver accurate, low-cost blood tests in record time with just a finger prick. But after the company reportedly raised some $724 million in investments, the machine was proved inoperable \u2014\u00a0never mind that it was promoted by Holmes and Balwani as working just fine, potentially putting test subjects at risk.<\/p>\n

According to the book \u201cBad Blood\u201d by John Carreyrou, one of the Wall Street Journal writers who broke the Theranos fraud story, Balwani was \u201ca force of nature but not in a good way.\u201d <\/p>\n

Balwani allegedly was an aggressive manager at Theranos.Kris Tripplaar <\/p>\n

He arrived in the United States from Pakistan in 1986 for undergraduate studies and put in a decade as a software engineer at Microsoft and Lotus. In 1999, he signed on with a startup called Commerce.Bid, which developed a software program for business-to-business auctions.<\/p>\n

The company hit bigduring the dot-com tsunami of the late \u201990s, sold in November 1999 for a stunning $232 million, and Balwani, the president and CTO, received a windfall of $40 million. <\/p>\n

By the time he met Holmes in 2002 in Beijing \u2014 he was 37 and enjoying his riches; she was 19 and studying Mandarin while attending Stanford \u2014 he seemed like an apt mentor.<\/p>\n

The indictment against Balwani alleges that he defrauded doctors and patients, \u201cmaking false claims concerning Theranos\u2019s ability to provide accurate, fast, reliable and cheap blood tests and test results.\u201dREUTERS <\/p>\n

He joined Theranos in 2009 and quickly made his presence felt. According to \u201cBad Blood,\u201d during an early meeting he attended at Theranos, Balwani claimed to have \u201cwritten a million lines of [computer] code.\u201d <\/p>\n

That was viewed as ridiculous by Theranos employees. As Carreyrou points out in his book, teams of developers wrote the Windows operating system at only 1,000 lines per year.<\/p>\n

Also disturbing, according to \u201cBad Blood\u201d and to the close source, was Balwani\u2019s personal interactions with employees and investors. <\/p>\n

\u201cHe was ruthless within Theranos,\u201d said the source. \u201cHe intimidated personnel and made sure employees were deterred from saying anything negative about the company.\u201d<\/p>\n

Holmes was found guilty on four counts of fraud in January and is out on $500,000 bail while awaiting sentencing.Getty Images <\/p>\n

According to \u201cBad Blood,\u201d he had no qualms about putting down employees and reprimanding them with an \u201cin-your-face management style.\u201d This later evolved into full-blown paranoia and accusations of trade secrets being stolen. According to Hastings Law Journal, NDAs were so much a part of the culture that when a departing employee refused to sign one, Balwani summoned the police. To explain the crime being committed, he insisted that the employee \u201cstole property in his mind.\u201d <\/p>\n

But Balwani failed to work his dark magic with employee Tyler Shultz, the grandson of George Shultz, a former secretary of state and a board member of Theranos. In early 2014, Tyler became an initial whistle-blower at the firm, reporting to New York State Department of Health regulators that the blood testing machine was a sham.<\/p>\n

Soon after, he told CBS News, Balwani responded aggressively. Shultz said that the boss told him he was \u201carrogant, ignorant, patronizing, reckless \u2026 [and] that if I had any other last name, I would have been held accountable [for airing concerns] to the strongest extent.\u201d<\/p>\n

\u201cHe told me that I didn\u2019t know what I was doing in business, that my convictions were wrong, that he was astonished at my mediocrity and if I followed my instincts I was going to fail,\u201d Holmes (lower right) testified of Balwani.REUTERS <\/p>\n

Shultz quit the firm in April 2014. By the next year, the jig was up for Theranos as news broke about fraudulent claims and deceptive practices.<\/p>\n

Theranos staffers came back with accurate results that were purported to have come from the Edison.\u201d<\/p>\n
