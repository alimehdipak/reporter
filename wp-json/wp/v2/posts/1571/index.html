<\/p>\n

Apple CEO Tim Cook came out swinging against proposed antitrust legislation, claiming that proposals to loosen the iPhone maker\u2019s grip over the app market would lead to grave \u201cunintended consequences\u201d for users\u2019 security and privacy.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cWe are deeply concerned about regulations that would undermine privacy and security in service of some other aim,\u201d Cook said in a defiant speech at a digital privacy conference in Washington, DC on Tuesday.<\/p>\n

His comments come as lawmakers in the US and Europe weigh bills that would require Apple to let users download apps from third-party app stores \u2014 a process known as \u201csideloading.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Backers of the bills \u2014 including many app developers \u2014 argue that Apple\u2019s tight grip over its \u201cwalled garden\u201d store allows the company to charge excessively high transaction fees and stifle competition, while Apple has countered that the model is necessary to protect users.\u00a0<\/p>\n

\u201cHere in Washington and elsewhere, policymakers are taking steps, in the name of competition, that would force Apple to let apps onto iPhone that circumvent the App Store through a process called sideloading,\u201d Cook said in a defiant speech at a digital privacy conference in Washington, D.C. \u201cIf we are forced to let unvetted apps onto iPhone, the unintended consequences will be profound.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Critics say that Apple\u2019s app store charges excessive fees and stifles competition. NurPhoto via Getty Images <\/p>\n

Sideloading is already allowed on Android phones, Windows computers and even Apple\u2019s own Mac computers \u2014 but Cook nonetheless claimed that allowing the practice on iPhones would mean that \u201cdata-hungry companies would be able to avoid our privacy rules and once again track our users against their will.\u201d\u00a0<\/p>\n

Tuesday isn\u2019t the first time that Cook \u2014 who raked in $99 million last year \u2014 has shown a willingness to fight tooth-and-nail against antitrust bills.\u00a0<\/p>\n

In January, Sen. Ted Cruz (R-Texas) revealed that Cook had personally called him for 40 minutes to lobby against a bill called the American Innovation and Choice Online Act, which would forbid tech companies from favoring their own products over competing ones in search results.\u00a0Google CEO Sundar Pichai also lobbied against the bill. <\/p>\n

Tim Cook personally called Sen. Ted Cruz (R-Texas) to lobby against an antitrust bill in January, according to Cruz. Getty Images <\/p>\n

Despite Cook and Pichai\u2019s opposition, the bill was later advanced by the Senate Judiciary Committee and now awaits a vote on the Senate floor.\u00a0<\/p>\n

Cook\u2019s combativeness stands in stark contrast to Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg, who has largely delegated political battles to subordinates as he focuses on his metaverse pet project.\u00a0<\/p>\n","protected":false},"excerpt":{"rendered":"
